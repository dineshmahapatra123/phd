**Components of the Land Records Modernization Program (DI LRMP)**¬Ý

Digitization converts physical land records into digital format; survey and resurvey update and verify records using modern technology; digitization of cadastral maps creates digital versions; integration links textual and spatial data; online service delivery provides land and revenue services digitally. DI LRMP components include computerized RoR, linkage with AAR, digitally signed copies of RoR, digitized cadastral maps, integration with RoR, real-time updation, georeferencing, modern record rooms, and survey/resurvey of cadastral and non-cadastral villages. ‚Äã‚ÅÝ

**Field Insights: Digitization Status in Non-ADC Areas**¬Ý

Primary surveys in three non-ADC districts‚ÄîKach, Dugar, and Sonetur‚Äîeach sampled two randomly selected villages. Although secondary data on DI LRMP showed 100% digitization of land records in the state, primary data showed only 35% of respondents possessed current RoR, mostly sourced from circle officers; less than 5% used common service centers (CSCs), a key DI LRMP access point. Sonetur had 84% respondents with RoRs, while Kachar had only 5%. Kachar is completely non-cadastral, with cadastral survey in process under Swamitva. Many lacked RoRs because they had not applied due to outstanding land revenue payments and fears of paying backlog in one go. Many villages were unaware of exact land details. **Land Administration in ADCs**¬Ý

Assam‚Äôs three ADCs are Karbi Anglong Autonomous Council, Dima Hasao Autonomous Council (formerly North Cachar Hills Autonomous Council), and Bodoland Territorial Council. KAC and NCHAC have their own acts and rules; BTC follows state acts. BTC record-keeping resembles the plains districts and is progressing, with Mission Bisumati on lines of Mission Basundra, still in progress and lagging behind plain areas due to indigenous practices. In Dima Hasao, villagers have rights to use and occupy land but cannot sell, mortgage, or inherit it. In Kiong, there is no right of ownership. Both councils act differently, with six to seven tribes each and varied indigenous customs for land ownership. In DHAC, everything related to land is oral and maintained in council; KAC records are codified. Patas are not mandatory in rural areas; councils issue land patas to local tribes based on formal applications and consent, increasingly used to resolve family disputes. Patas are not properly written or recorded, lack hereditary laws, and have no permanent tenancy right in jhum land, though such rights exist for permanent wet paddy land. Karianglong prohibits sale, barter, lease, or mortgage. ‚Äã‚ÅÝ

**Barriers to Digitization in ADC Areas**¬Ý

Administrative powers are given to ADCs, and digitization is not prohibited. However, field interactions revealed traditional, institutional, and bureaucratic factors‚Äîincluding vested interests‚Äîimpeding digitization. Villagers fear publishing records could lead to loss of land. ‚Äã‚ÅÝ

**Way Forward: Governance, Capacity, and Awareness**¬Ý

There is a governance gap: non-ADC sees land record management as a governance need for clarity; ADC sees it as a burden better handled administratively by officials. Certification must be present; training and capacity building with ground-level awareness drives by LRMP should be launched; survey of all land parcels with parcel-wise data should be finalized quickly. Major awareness is needed about Mission Basundra and Mission Visumati; field surveys showed most are unaware. Awareness camps must clarify misunderstandings and fear. Village-level revenue collection centers should be set up for ease of payment; operational CSCs are required, as their absence results in middlemen. Digitization and CSC prevalence were meant to remove middlemen and enable direct payments to departments, but this is not happening. Village maps should be available to headmen, not only with patwaris or circle officers. Executive councils must include women representation, currently lacking across all three ADCs; 30% representation would ensure compassionate understanding of citizen issues. The Government of India must relook at Assam‚Äôs monetary burden and help complete the MR execution as initially planned; survey/resurvey under DI LRMP must be accelerated. Thank you. ‚Äã‚ÅÝ

**From Individual Factors to Social Settings**¬Ý

Technology adoption studies often focus on individual-level factors. I examine how those individual factors are shaped within social settings, focusing on migration and differential adoption patterns between migrants and non-migrants. **Theoretical Lens: Diffusion of Innovations and Aspirations**¬Ý

Using Everett Rogers‚Äô framework (compatibility, trialability, observability, complexity, and relative advantage), I add an aspiration-based lens: aspirations have cultural capacity (whether local culture and neighborhood allow realization of dreams) and navigational capacity (practical ability and finances to move from present circumstances to desired goals). These are shared and shaped by norms, values, and beliefs; marginalized groups build capacities through challenges. Migrants develop maps of possibilities by leaving comfort zones, traveling to urban areas, and engaging broader social networks. ‚Äã‚ÅÝ

My dependent variable is ‚Äústacking‚Äù‚Äîusing improved cookstoves (ICS), LPG, and traditional stoves together.

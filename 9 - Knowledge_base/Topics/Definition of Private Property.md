# Topic: Definition of Private Property

## Our Unified Understanding
As of April 2026, our research environment defines **Private Property** as a multi-dimensional institutional construct comprising:

### 1. The Right of Exclusion (Economic Basis)
- **The Functionalist Definition**: Following **Demsetz (1967)**, property rights are "social instruments" that help a man form **expectations** which he can reasonably hold in his dealings with others. These expectations are derived from the laws, customs, and mores of the society.
- **Internalization of Externalities**: The primary function of private property is to guide incentives toward **internalizing externalities**. It ensures that the owner/user bears the full social cost and benefit of their actions, thereby reducing the "Costs of Monitoring/Negotiation" inherent in communal regimes.
- **The Property Right Bundle**: While **Alchian & Demsetz (1973)** formalize the "bundle of sticks" paradigm, the logic originates in the 1967 thesis of partitionable and circumscribed actions. Its private nature is defined by the owner's ability to exclude others and capture the value of the demarcated uses.
- **The Schlager-Ostrom Taxonomy**: **[[Elinor Ostrom]]** and **[[Edella Schlager]]** (2000) operationalize this by identifying five distinct rights: **Access, Withdrawal, Management, Exclusion, Alienation**. "Full Private Ownership" occurs only when all five are present (especially Alienation).


### 2. The Behavioral Basis (Institutional Basis)
Following **North (1990)**, "Private" property is an institutional solution to the **Cooperation Problem**. It provides the **"rules of the game"** that reduce uncertainty in human interaction. It is the framework within which **Organizations** (the players) operate. Without these rules, complex impersonal exchange is impossible.

### 3. The Institutional Mirror (Legal Basis)
Following **[[D. C. Wadhwa]]**, property is only "Private" and secure when it is **Conclusive**. This requires an absolute symmetry between:
- **Acts in Law**: Private transactions.
- **Acts of the Sovereign**: The state's guarantee (e.g., the **[[Torrens System]]**).
Without this, property remains **[[Presumptive vs Conclusive Title | Presumptive]]** (merely fiscal).

### 4. The Power of Override (Political Basis)
As shown by **Ghosh & Deodhar (2025)**, private property is always bounded by the state's reserve power of **[[Eminent Domain]]**. Private rights are not absolute but exist in a "negotiated state" with the sovereign's public purpose requirements.

### 5. The Relational Basis (Exchange Context)
Following **North (1987)**, the definition of "Private" property changes based on the exchange context.
- In **Personal Exchange**, property is managed through kinship and repeat dealings. Private rights are embedded in community norms.
- In **Impersonal Exchange**, property must be abstracted and state-guaranteed. The "Private" nature is defined by formal, impersonal rules and third-party enforcement that allow strangers to trade rights with confidence.

### 6. The Indigenous Critique (Philosophical Basis)
- **Vasundhara vs. Commodity**: Ancient Indian thought via **[[Vasundhara]]** suggests land is a "wealth-holder" (medium), not wealth itself.
- **Communal Bundles**: The **[[Khuntkatti]]** system represents a "Lineage-based Bundle" of rights.

### 7. The Financial Basis (Asset Basis)
Following **[[Gershon Feder]] (1999)**, property becomes "Private" in a modern economic sense when it is transformed from a use-right into a **liquid capital asset**.
- **Collateral Value**: The definition of private property includes the state-guaranteed right to mortgage. This is enabled by **[[Collateral Value of Land]]**, which eliminates information asymmetry between owners and institutional lenders.
- **Titling Premium**: Private property is defined by its ability to command a market premium (up to 58.0% in urban Davao) based on its de jure **[[Tenure Security]]**.

## Scholarly Synthesis
In our PhD framework, property is the **institutional resolution** of the tension between private information (exclusion), state legibility (registration), and historical customary rights. We reject the monolithic definition of private property in favor of **[[Classes of Property Rights Holders]]**. Private property is not a single point on a graph but a spectrum of partitioned authority whose "efficiency" depends on the subtractability and exclusion costs of the underlying **[[Common-pool resources]]**.

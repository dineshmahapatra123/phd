# Does Land Registration Lead to Economic Development?

This topic synthesizes the empirical evidence regarding the impact of land registration and titling on various developmental outcomes, addressing the "panacea" debate in development economics.

## **1. Tenure Security and Investment**
**Consensus**: There is strong evidence that land registration enhances tenure security, which in turn increases long-term land-attached investment.
- **Evidence**: Positive impacts in Thailand, Vietnam, Ethiopia, and Nicaragua (Deininger & Feder, 2009).
- **Caveat**: The impact is context-specific; in some areas (e.g., Madagascar), the effect on productivity is negligible (Jacoby & Minten, 2007).

## **2. Credit Access (The de Soto Effect)**
**Contention**: The argument that formalization unlocks "dead capital" by facilitating credit is the most debated and least uniformly supported.
- **Evidence**: Credit supply effects are often limited to large landowners (Paraguay) or state-sponsored banks (Peru) rather than the private sector (Carter & Olinto, 2003; Field & Torero, 2006).
- **Mechanisms for Failure**: Illiquid land markets, "risk rationing" (fear of losing land), and inability of banks to foreclose in poor neighborhoods.

## **3. Labor Mobility and Efficiency**
**Emerging Evidence**: Registration improves allocative efficiency by allowing labor to migrate to more productive non-farm sectors.
- **Peru**: 50% increase in labor supply due to reduced need for home protection (Field, 2007).
- **Vietnam**: Shift toward non-farm employment for the rural poor (Do & Iyer, 2008).

## **4. Gender and Social Equity**
**Finding**: Titling can empower women and improve child welfare if rights are granted jointly or individually to females.
- **Impacts**: Reduced fertility, better nutrition, and higher bargaining power in India and Peru.

### 4. Property Rights as Cause or Effect? (Bromley, 1989)
**[[Daniel Bromley]]** (1989) provides a foundational critique of the "Conventional Wisdom" (Demsetz, Feder) that private property is a **cause** of economic wealth.
- **The Causality Challenge**: The mere fact that high productivity is correlated with private land does not prove that private land *causes* high productivity. High productivity is often the result of irrigation, infrastructure, and proximity to markets—factors that also make the high administrative costs of individual titling worthwhile.
- **Efficient Boundaries**: Bromley argues that the observed boundary between private and public land in the tropics often represent the **efficient boundary**. Extending privatization into the public domain (extensive margin) may be a net loss when the high infrastructure and record-keeping costs are factored in.
- **Institutional Fix first**: Capital investment in land (new tree species, irrigation) will be "squandered" unless an **institutional fix** (converting open access to common property) is established first.

> [!TIP]
> **PhD Research Vector**: When evaluating Odisha's modernisation, we must ask: Are we pushing the "private boundary" into areas where the administrative costs exceed the social benefits? Or are we ignoring the need for an "institutional fix" in the commons?

## **5. Technocracy and Exclusion (New Critique)**
**Finding**: Modernisation through technocratic means can reproduction or deepen historical exclusions.
- **Telangana Case ([[R. Ramakumar]] & [[Padmini Ramesh]], 2023)**: The shift from developmental to technocratic administration (e.g., [[Dharani Portal]]) can marginalize groups like tenants who are removed from records to facilitate owner-centric subsidies like the [[Rythu Bandhu Scheme]].
- **Illegibly Legible**: A state where records are digitally "pure" but disconnected from the ground reality of possession, creating a new form of opacity for the poor.

## **6. Synthesis: Not a Panacea**
Deininger & Feder (2009) conclude that land registration is an institutional intervention that requires:
1.  **Good Governance**: Impartial courts and an honest bureaucracy.
2.  **Diagnosis**: Interventions must respond to grassroots demand and land scarcity levels.
3.  **Cost-Effectiveness**: High-precision systems may be unsustainable in low-value rural areas; low-cost certification may be superior.

### Key Answer
The importance of cadastral maps in India is rooted in their role as the scientific bridge between **colonial revenue extraction** and **modern land administration**. 

*   **Colonial Significance**: 
    *   **Transition to Taxable Asset**: Prior to the late 18th century, land was often a communal resource. The Cadastral Survey (alongside the Great Trigonometrical Survey) allowed the British to quantify land into parcels (*Khasras*), making it a quantifiable, taxable asset.
    *   **Marketable Commodity**: By precisely defining boundaries and ownership, land was transformed into a commodity that could be bought, sold, or mortgaged—a radical departure from pre-colonial Hindu and Mughal systems.
    *   **Extraction Enabler**: Clear titles created by cadastral maps allowed the East India Company to enforce contracts for cash crops like Opium and Tea, which were vital for correcting Britain's trade imbalance with China.

*   **Structural Legacy**: 
    *   **The Khasra & Khata System**: The surveys established the village-level records that are still in use today. Every landholding was assigned a *khasra* number, and owner-wise records were maintained in a *khata* ledger.
    *   **Administrative Hierarchy**: It created the organized tapestry of land administration involving Tehsildars, Kanungos, and Patwaris.
    *   **Legal Foundation**: These maps remains the most reliable source for revenue, taxation, and the exercise of Eminent Domain by the state.

## Key Answer
The RoR in India serves as a **Presumptive** fiscal document rather than a conclusive proprietary record. Its critical importance lies in the **Institutional Alignment** between private trade and state recognition.

### 1. The Alignment of Acts (Wadhwa's Framework)
The RoR is the state's primary mechanism for matching **Acts in Law** (private transactions like sales or inheritance) with **Acts of the Sovereign** (public certification). Wadhwa (1989) argues that without this alignment, the "justifiability of the law of evidence concerning public records falls to the ground," leading to the **Record-Reality Gap**.

### 2. Credit and Liquidity (Feder & Nishio)
The RoR acts as the gatekeeper for the **Security Dividend**. By providing a recognized (if presumptive) base of ownership, it allows land to transition from a "dead" rural asset to liquid capital, enabling institutional credit access (often a 52-521% increase in credit availability).

### 3. Reducing Transaction Costs (North)
A reliable RoR reduces the costs of **Measurement** and **Enforcement**. Without a centralized "Act of the Sovereign" like the RoR (or ideally the **[[Torrens System]]**), every buyer must perform an expensive, recursive "retrospective investigation" into the chain of title.

### 4. Historical Finality (The Domesday Precedent)
The ultimate institutional goal of an RoR is to reach the level of the **Domesday Book (1086)**—a record so final and accurate that its evidence "cannot be set at naught." Current Indian RoR systems (e.g., *Patwari* records) remain stunted at the fiscal stage, failing to reach this proprietary finality.

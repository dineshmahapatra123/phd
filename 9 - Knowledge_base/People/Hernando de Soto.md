# Hernando de Soto

## Scholarly Lens
**Hernando de Soto** is a Peruvian economist best known for his argument that the absence of formal property rights — particularly for land and housing — is the primary cause of poverty in developing countries. His work frames informal assets as "dead capital": economically inactive because they cannot be legally leveraged. Formal titling, in his view, converts dead capital into live capital by enabling collateralisation and market participation.

## Core Argument
Secure property rights, as evidenced by formal land records, incentivise long-term capital investment by property owners. Owners with formal title can use land as collateral to secure loans and make agricultural, housing, or business investments. Without formal title, land is economically frozen. This theoretical claim is the principal intellectual justification for land titling programmes globally — including DILRMP in India.

## PhD Application
Kapoor et al. (2024) invoke de Soto's theory as the rationale for meticulous land records: "maintaining updated and comprehensive land records aligns with de Soto's theory, asserting that secure property rights, evidenced by such records, incentivise property owners to make long-term capital investments." However, [[Daniel Bromley]] directly challenges this formalization-first logic, arguing that titling without accompanying economic conditions (jobs, credit access, planned urbanisation) does not produce development.

The de Soto thesis is also contested empirically by [[Gershon Feder]] and [[David Feeny]], who show the benefits of land registration are strongest where credit markets and enforcement institutions already function adequately (see [[Land tenure and property rights_ theory and implications for development policy]]).

## Methodological Bias
De Soto relies on descriptive institutional analysis and case-level evidence from Peru and Egypt. He does not use quasi-experimental methods. His arguments have faced criticism for under-specifying the institutional prerequisites that make formal titling effective.

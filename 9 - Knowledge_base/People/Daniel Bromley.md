# Daniel Bromley

## Scholarly Lens
**Daniel W. Bromley** is an American institutional economist and emeritus professor at the University of Wisconsin-Madison. He is a prominent critic of the formalization-first approach to land tenure reform, arguing that the belief in titling as a pathway to development rests on under-theorised assumptions about how markets and institutions actually work in lower-income contexts.

## Core Argument Against Formalization
Bromley (2008) directly challenges the [[Hernando de Soto|de Soto]] thesis: formalisation of land records does *not* automatically create development. Converting informal possession into formal ownership requires a set of pre-existing conditions — functioning credit markets, employment opportunities, planned urbanisation, and agricultural support systems — without which a title document has limited economic effect. The ability to buy and sell land (which is what land records ultimately record) is itself dependent on these broader conditions. Addressing tenure insecurity through formal titling, while ignoring these structural conditions, is treating a symptom rather than a cause.

## Application to India (DILRMP)
Kapoor et al. (2024) invoke Bromley's argument to critique DILRMP's neo-liberal framing: the programme — pushed by the World Bank and donor agencies — treats land records as an apolitical, technical problem solvable through digitisation, ignoring:
- Redistributive dimensions of land reform.
- Socio-cultural aspects of land tenure (particularly for tribal and indigenous communities).
- The political economy of who benefits from formalisation (often elites rather than smallholders).

## Scholarly Debate
### ⚠️ Contradiction with De Soto
Bromley's position contradicts the standard World Bank / de Soto position that formalization drives investment and poverty reduction. The contradiction is empirically unresolved — the literature on titling outcomes is mixed, with results highly dependent on institutional context (see [[Land tenure and property rights_ theory and implications for development policy]]).

## Methodological Bias
Institutional economics and heterodox political economy. Sceptical of econometric claims about titling benefits that do not account for selection bias (who gets titled first, and why) or for local institutional context.

# Gershon Feder

**Gershon Feder** is a prominent land economist, formerly of the **World Bank** and **IFPRI**, best known for his empirical work on the link between **formal land titling** and **economic development**.

## **Core Contributions**

### 1. The Thailand Study (1988)
In his seminal study of rural Thailand, Feder provided the first quantitative benchmarks for the impact of secure titling. He demonstrated that titled farmers invested more and realized higher revenues than untitled farmers, largely due to the formalization of their access to the credit market.

### 2. Theoretical Linkages Framework
Feder identifies three core channels through which land rights affect development:
1.  **Investment Incentives**: Long-term security encourages capital improvements.
2.  **Credit Access**: Land as collateral reduces borrowing costs.
3.  **Transferability**: Lowers transaction costs in rental and sales markets.

### 3. Governance and Real-Life Complications (2009)
In later collaborations with [[Klaus Deininger]], Feder moved toward a more nuanced view, acknowledging that theoretical benefits often fail to materialize due to:
- **Deficient Governance**: State abuse of power or elite capture.
- **Land Scarcity Variation**: Customary systems may be more efficient in land-abundant settings.
- **Credit Market Imperfections**: Lack of foreclosure ability or "risk rationing" by the poor.

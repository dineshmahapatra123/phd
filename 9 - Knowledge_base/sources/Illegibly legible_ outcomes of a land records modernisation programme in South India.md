# Illegibly legible: Outcomes of a land records modernisation programme in South India

## Ramakumar R1 | Padmini Ramesh2

1School of Development Studies, Tata Institute of Social Sciences, Mumbai, India 2Kudumbashree NRO, Thiruvananthapuram, India

Abstract

This article analyses an initiative in 2017 to update and digitise textual land records in Telangana, a south Indian state. Its premise is that experiments to modernise land records have not met with pre-determined standards of success due, primarily, to the historically evolved contradictions around land as a resource and a commodity. In countries like India, the colonial policies on land and the post-colonial success of landlords to manipulate land records had already left the post-War task of ensuring conclusive land titles intractable. In more recent times, however, the agenda of land records modernisation has been absorbed by neoliberalism, which aims to create free land markets and replace traditional subsidies with direct cash transfers. This article shows that, consequently, the task of land records modernisation in Telangana became disembedded from agendas of agrarian egalitarianism and was rendered more complexhistorical errors and exclusions were reproduced in new ways; technocratic solutions of the bureaucracy made governmental processes opaque; landed sections continued to subvert implementation; tenants were excluded from the land titles; and there were fears that the scheme would be misused to ease corporate land acquisition. Land records modernisation remains important for agrarian reform, but its success remains contingent on a greater appreciation among policymakers for the historical and political economy aspects of land ownership and possession.

- 1 | INTRODUCTION

In societies where access to land is mediated by private property rights, updating and modernising land records is an important task of governance—with implications for rural equity and class and caste relations. Formalisation of land titles, accompanying or following land reforms, provides small and marginal farmers, tenants and the near-landless with secure tenure; it saves their time, reduces their costs of document storage and search and ensures document recovery in times of disasters. They improve the society's ability to reduce corruption in land administration systems—i.e., helps ensure ‘transparency guarantee’ (Sen, 1999). Even in societies that have not had land reform, formalisation of land titles helps in making the concentration of land ownership visible and help galvanise campaigns for land redistribution. Formal land titles can help tenants access bank credit (Karmakar et al., 2011). Above all, a modern land records system is important to maintain a robust statistical system and adopt scientific land use planning (Ramachandran, 2020).

Despite these obvious benefits, land records modernisation has remained a contentious policy project. On the one hand, contemporary policy processes around land records modernisation are increasingly aligned with the neoliberal goal of ‘free land markets’ and have shifted from the developmental arena to the technocratic sphere. On the other hand, efforts to modernise land records have been less successful than expected, invited resistance and subject to bureaucratic intransigence.

Our paper, then, begins with the following question: Do the sociocultural and political economy characteristics of land limit the possibilities of success in such schemes? The paper begins by laying down the arguments on the motivations for land records modernisation. It then documents and analyses one of the first large-scale efforts in India to update and digitise land records at the level of one state—Telangana. Telangana is a relatively new state in the Indian union, carved out of the erstwhile unified Andhra Pradesh State in 2014. The analysis is based on published literature, official reports, secondary data, primary data from the field, interviews with officials and farmers and data from a farmer-level helpline.

- 2 | THE CHALLENGE OF LAND ADMINISTRATION


- 2.1 | Changing conceptions of land


To begin with, land has always been a site of production. As human populations grew, the production of more food came to be associated with both extensive and intensive uses of land. However, the physical finiteness of land constrained its unmitigated extensive use, and the possession of land became a pre-requisite for controlling and regulating direct production (Marx, 1959). These constraints developed unevenly in different contexts and gave rise to varying forms of tensions around land as a resource.1

The development of large-scale farming in the frontier regions became a possibility in the 19th century due to mechanisation, and the potential for trans-nationalisation of land possession and the attendant emergence of ‘land grabs’ expanded in the 20th century (see Byres, 1988; Levien, 2018).

Later, debates developed around a second dimension: the commodification of land. The extensive and intensive uses of land as a resource coincided with the development of agrarian capitalism. Accumulation of capital centred around land as ‘private property’, which in turn was accompanied by increasing dispossession of people from land (Marx, 1959). After Marx, Karl Polanyi too noted how the commodification of land in market economies—disembedded from the society—gave rise to new contradictions. For Polanyi, land was eminently ill-suited to be a commodity; he termed the commodity description of land as ‘entirely fictitious’ (Polanyi, 1944).

The third dimension that emerged over time was a by-product of the commodification of land. As the demand for land increased, land was no more the site of agricultural production alone but also of multiple non-agricultural uses. Land was demanded for industries, services, housing, tourism, recreation and, above all, conservation. These competing uses tested the survival of land as a productive resource, intensified speculative activities in its purchase and sale and exacerbated conflicts around land (Sud, 2021).

Finally, none of the above tensions and conflicts within capitalist societies ameliorated the need for improving the administration of land. Modern forms of land administration, especially in maintaining land records and recording land transactions, became essential for the capitalist state to regulate the use of land as private property and contain the contradictions it gave rise to. As nation states developed, there also arose the need for uniformity in the design and architecture of land administration. Thus, over time, land administration came to be intrinsically linked to state formation, political processes, territorial disputes and economic policy.

The question we move ahead with, then, is the following: How did the need for ‘efficient’ land administration interface with the contradictions generated by the unique features of land?

- 2.2 | The challenge of land administration


Given the unique features of land, nation states have attempted to raise the efficiency of land use alongside the introduction of regulations to contain rising dispossession. In other words, governments have had the unenviable task of designing systems of land administration corresponding to both to the stage of development of productive forces and the changing nature of conflicts around land. The contemporary discourse around land administration is powerfully moulded by these twin tasks of modern-day governance.

In the post-War developing world, the task of land governance was additionally complicated by the refractory nature of land relations left over by colonialism. In colonies like India, the British empire broke down the traditional framework of land relations. It restructured land relations to maximise land revenues, created new landlords and vested them with legal powers of private property. But these new systems and records ended up generating fresh contradictions in the agrarian economy, as they were ‘made not for the people, who cultivate the soil, nor for the holder, who owns it, but for the government that taxes it’ (Marx, 1853).

Such contradictions, indeed, are the focus of Scott's (1998) argument that a new ‘politics of measurement’ emerged under the regimes of colonialism. The creation of land records led to the simplification, codification and standardisation of local customary practices around land. Scott refers to this process as a shift from the ‘illegibility’ to the ‘legibility’ of traditional communal land tenures. In particular, the preparation of cadastral maps allowed the colonial states to abstract and universalise. These efforts sought to transform and flatten the diversity in customary land regimes; concurrently, they also gave rise to movements of anti-colonial resistance.

Even after colonialism ended, land administration in most erstwhile colonies was locked into trajectories of ‘path dependence’. Other than a few societies that transformed agrarian relations through land reforms, colonial systems of land administration survived (see Byres, 1988). To escape provisions of land ceiling laws and evict tenants, landlords and the revenue bureaucracy colluded and extensively manipulated land records.

Consequently, land records lost their correspondence with the reality on the ground. This lack of correspondence has led to much flailing of the existing systems of land administration. ‘Illegible’ land records—where a series of traditional and informal set of tenures and settlements survived over many layers of claims and counterclaims, and

- 2.3 | Land records in the neoliberal era


From the 1980s and after, the task of ‘modernisation of land records’ became more prominent in policy discourse. Neoliberal economists, international organisations and policy consultants argued that land records must be modernised not to transform agrarian relations but to create a free land market, where land would be freely transferable and new investments in land would be facilitated (see Byamugisha, 1999; de Soto, 2000; Feder & Nishio, 1998; McKinsey, 2001). They argued that conclusive land titles improve the Ease of Doing Business (EoDB).2 The World Bank's EoDB rankings were used to argue the case for conclusive land titling (NCAER, 2020).3 New solutions like block chain were also prescribed to create a robust property rights system that facilitated investment in land (see Bal, 2017).

Thus, in the 1980s, bilateral and multilateral institutions, such as the United States Agency for International Development (USAID), began to fund land titling projects in developing countries (Deininger & Feder, 2013). In the 1990s, the World Bank assisted governments in 11 former Soviet Bloc countries to develop and implement land registration programmes. By 2005, the World Bank was supervising a portfolio of land administration projects worth more than $1 billion (Bell, 2006; see also Nayak, 2020).4

Neoliberalism in land administration was closely associated with the rise of ‘technocracy’ in policy. Technocracy is defined in many ways—as the replacement of ‘government of persons’ by ‘administration of things’; as a ‘government by experts’ necessitated by the incapability of citizens to self-govern; as ‘the rise to power of those who possess technical knowledge or ability, to the detriment of the traditional type of politician’; and as ‘the administrative and political domination of society by a state elite … through the application of instrumentally rational techniques’ (Cole, 2022). But essentially, technocracy implies a depoliticisation of policy, actively intermediated by state bureaucracies, which tends to insulate the state from democracy to promote a set of efficiency goals. The rise of technocracy does not just maintain, if not deepen, elite domination; it fetishises the need to contain political pressures and legitimises the autonomy of ‘experts’.

There is little excitement in the literature on the robustness of EoDB indices, even within institutions like the World Bank (Nagaraj, 2019). According to Nagaraj, ‘there is no statistically valid association between the EoDB index rank … and growth in FDI and private investment’. Given the many controversies surrounding the estimation of EoDB ranks, the World Bank itself discontinued its publication after 2020.5

The empirical literature on land titling and its relationship with land values, equity and investment is also ambiguous on whether conclusive land titling leads to economically ‘efficient’ outcomes. Miceli and Kieyah (2003) reviewed the evidence for the United States where many states attempted a shift from a recording system to a voluntary Torrens-based land registration system. They argued that the policy was only successful in ‘solving idiosyncratic problems in the record’ and did not lead to a reduction in costs of transacting land. Even in the most successful states, only less than half of the records entered the new system.

An early review of the African efforts to transform customary land systems in sub-Saharan Africa argued that land titling or registration ‘would not have the intended impact, would not be economically justifiable, or would even be counter-productive’ (Atwood, 1990, p. 668). They fail to account for local and broader national class relations (as, for instance, described in Capps, 2016). In his work, Atwood also remarks that the theoretical framework on free land markets failed to ‘take adequate account of the extra-legal, informal, local institutional environment’ within which most rural households accessed land. The case of the Brazilian frontier discussed in Alston et al. (1999) provided similar proof of why title reforms may fail. Further, even if distortions are removed in the land market, distortions that survived in other markets, and market failures therein, may defeat the purpose of land title reforms (Dong, 1996).

An alternative perspective is that land records modernisation in rural areas, viewed from within the EoDB framework, seeks to facilitate large-scale corporate investments in land and agri-business (see Mittal, 2014). The example of a state-level initiative launched in India in the state of Karnataka—titled Bhoomi—is a good example.

Launched in 2000, the Bhoomi initiative aimed to computerise land records and provide online delivery of revenue documents. It was not based on a fresh resurvey of land. The Record of Rights, Tenancy and Crops (RTC) was digitised, that is, entered in a portal on an ‘as is what is’ basis, and then subjected to preliminary verification. Bhoomi was also a manifestation of technocracy, the argument being that illegible land records reduced efficiency and fostered corruption. However, as Benjamin and Raman (2011) argued, the premise of Bhoomi was to allow big businesses to locate in the rapidly urbanising peripheries of Karnataka's big cities like Bengaluru. The scheme became a tool to reshape power relations around land on new terms while erasing much of the traditional claims over land. The problem, according to them, was in seeing the illegibility of land records not as a ‘political condition’ but as a ‘normative’ problem (see also De, 2016).6 Such evidence that questions the faith of neoliberalism in creating free land markets has not slowed down governmental efforts in that direction.

More recently, there is an additional reason why the presence of accurate land records has become significant. The increasing reliance on cash transfers, which crowd out funding for traditional in-kind schemes in different social and economic sectors, has dovetailed the growth of neoliberalism in countries like India. Eligibility to access the benefits of specific government programmes is linked to the possession of accurate land titles. For example, under the programme of liberalisation in agriculture, fertiliser subsidies are being monetised and replaced with direct cash transfers. The quantum of these cash transfers is linked to the extent of land owned; thus, a farmer must possess a clear land record to be eligible for the transfer (Ramesh, 2020; Uday & Zaveri, 2020).

In May 2018, the Telangana government introduced a scheme called the Rythu Bandhu Scheme (RBS). The RBS involved a direct cash payment to each farmer—an ‘agricultural investment support’ of Rs 5000 (USD 60) per acre per season—where only those who possessed accurate land titles were recognised as farmers. [Correction added on 18 September 2023, after first online publication: dollar conversion for ‘Rs 5000’ has been provided in the preceding sentence.] As land records were outdated, the Telangana government initiated a scheme to update textual land records in September 2017. The programme was called the Land Records Updation Programme (LRUP). Its objective was limited: fix private property rights and use it to identify the beneficiaries of the RBS. Nevertheless, the outcomes of LRUP remained vulnerable to the systemic problems that beset other land records modernisation programmes driven by a neoliberal persuasion.

This brings us to the more specific questions that we try to probe in this paper, drawing from a case study of the LRUP. Can the prospects of land records modernisation be divorced from the political economy of land ownership and possession? Can technocratic solutions bypass the complexities in land systems inherited from colonialism and ensure ‘legibility’ in land titles? Can land records modernisation be conceived of as autonomous from the power relations that are historically embedded in land ownership and possession? What do programmes that seek to dis-embed claims over land from history, and re-embed them in modern notions of ownership and possession, imply for specific agrarian groups like tenants, labourers and tribal households?

- 3 | LAND RECORDS IN INDIA: COMPLEXITY AND THE CHALLENGE


- 3.1 | An introduction to land records


Land is a state subject under the Indian constitution, and land records are maintained by the state governments. Land records are official documents that contain information about each parcel of land. The information contained in the documents include the extent of area, its boundaries, the owner of the property, nature of rights of its occupants, nature of use and details of sale and purchase. There are several types of land records that contain distinct sets of information about a land parcel. As such, land records can be classified into three: (a) textual records, maintained by the Revenue Departments; (b) spatial records, maintained by the Survey and Settlement Departments; and (c) transaction records, maintained by the Registration Departments (see Table 1 for a summary).

These three departments have historically worked in compartments or silos. Data collected or recorded by one department are not necessarily reflected in the records for the same plot maintained by other departments. We shall provide some typical examples. First, when the sale of one plot of land takes place by executing a deed in the Registration Department, the information on change in ownership is not reflected as a change in the Record of Rights (RoR) maintained by the Revenue Department. - 3.2 | A review of past efforts


In India, the need for updated and accurate land titles was recognised as important in most Five-Year Plan documents from the 1950s (Wadhwa, 2002). The interest in land titling in the 1950s arose from the need to implement land reforms. As is well documented, land reforms in India were a failure. According to Appu (1996), the neglect of land records in India after independence was closely associated with the efforts of landlords to evade land reform laws; in this sense, the poor quality of land records had the implicit sanction of the Indian state. For example, tenants were systematically omitted from mention in the land records, which allowed landlords to evict them. Land records were also regularly manipulated to ensure that landlords escaped the provisions of land ceiling laws (see also Srivastava, 2015).

In 1973, a Task Force of the Planning Commission observed that ‘the absence of up-to-date land records is a serious obstacle in the implementation of land reforms’ (Planning Commission, 1973). The Panel on Land Reforms in 1961 and the National Commission on Agriculture in 1976 made similar observations (GoI, 1961; NCA, 1976).

With the end of official interest in land reforms by the 1980s and the initiation of economic liberalisation policies in the 1990s, the motivation for modernising land records changed qualitatively. In the new era, modernisation of land records came to be linked to assured private property rights and the improvement of business confidence (Nayak, 2020). In 1987, the Planning Commission headed by Manmohan Singh, who inaugurated India's liberalisation programme as Finance Minister in 1991, appointed a one-man committee headed by D. C. Wadhwa to study the problem (Wadhwa, 1989). This committee recommended that India should shift from a presumptive land titling system to a modern and computerised conclusive land titling system.7 After the Wadhwa committee report, India conducted the first pilots on computerising and updating land records in Bihar and Odisha in 1988. In 1989–1990, a similar initiative was launched in all states (Panicker, 2015). However, the implementation was poor.

### 4 | TELANGANA'S LAND RECORDS: THE COLONIAL LEGACY

The unreliability of land records in Telangana in 2017 was an outcome of more than a century of changes in the land revenue system. The accumulation of changes in the land revenue system, and the nature of landlord-led manipulation of land records after the 1940s, must be documented to better appreciate the links between agrarian change, class relations and land titling in the region.

During the colonial period, the present-day states in India belonged to different regimes of British land administration, such as zamindari, ryotwari, mahalwari, khatedari, taluqdari and malguzari. In addition, there were princely states that followed distinct systems of land administration. Telangana has had a complex legacy of land records because the region inherited multiple systems of land administration from the colonial period. Before the 18th century, Hyderabad State was part of the Mughal Empire. In 1724, Asaf Jah Nizam-ul-Mulk I declared independence from the Mughals and established the Hyderabad State. For a while, the Nizam continued with the land records and settlement systems used by the Mughals. Over the years, however, revenues from land declined. By the early 19th century, Hyderabad was close to bankruptcy due to more than one reason: the Treaty of Subsidiary Alliance between the Nizam and the British and Nizam's heavy debts to the Palmer and Company, the Rohillas and the Arab merchants (Bobbili & Manohar, 1999, 2001).

In the later part of the 19th century, the then Nizam introduced changes in the land revenue system to bolster land revenues. In 1866, he abolished the earlier revenue system, eliminated intermediary revenue collectors and established direct contact between the state and the peasant (ibid.). In 1875, a new land settlement was put in place. In 1907, the Land Revenue Act 1317F was passed. Between 1932 and 1934, a fresh settlement survey was conducted. This ‘survey settlement and bandobust operation’ of 1932–1934 is considered by today's revenue authorities as the ‘most authentic’ source of land records in Telangana. It produced the sethwar, a new settlement register.

Although these legislations and measures were primarily aimed at raising land revenues, it also facilitated the concentration of land in the hands of landlords. During this period, of all land in Hyderabad State, 60% was under the governmental land revenue system, called diwani or khalsa area. About 30% was under the jagirdari system, and about 10% was Nizam's direct estate, or sarf khas (Sundarayya, 1972). It was in the jagirdari and sarf khas areas that the exploitation of tenants and labourers was most intense. A major portion of the land previously cultivated by tenants was captured by the landlords, who got lands registered in their names, sometimes even without the knowledge of the tenants.

The heavy exploitation of the peasantry resulted in the famous Telangana struggle led by the Communist Party of India in 1946. In 1948, Hyderabad was annexed to the Indian Union. In 1950, the Hyderabad Tenancy and Agricultural Lands Act was passed as a response to the Telangana struggle. This Act abolished the sarf khas and jagirdari systems and merged them with the diwani areas. It also introduced a document called the 38-E certificate, which accorded protection to tenants holding land for a period of 6 years and gave them permanent rights to remain as tenants subject to payment of rents and not sub-letting to others (Khusro, 1958).

With the 1950 Act and the abolition of sarf khas and jagirdari, a new responsibility of the government was the identification of the actual cultivators and recording their names in the revenue records. For this purpose, the preparation of the khasra pahani began in 1954 (Bobbili & Manohar, 1999, 2001). In the khasra pahani document, khata numbers were to be given to landholders, with the extent of land in possession and survey numbers allotted to the plots. The khasra pahani was to have four components: the bandobust record (details of survey settlement), RoR, agricultural accounts and cultivation details. Curiously, no new survey was undertaken prior to the preparation of the khasra pahani. Instead, the old sethwar was used and a mutation register was prepared with the history of possession and transaction of each plot after 1934 (such as purchase, inheritance, partnership, maintenance or gifts). The name of the existing holder was recorded as the pattadar. In many cases, the pattadar was a landlord or a large landowner, and hence, the names of his/her tenants were also to be recorded. However, no details of the location or extent of the lands of tenants were entered. This, later, became a major anomaly in the land records.

After the khasra pahani was prepared in 1954, the Revenue Department demanded that each village send information on actual land possessed and cultivated. Under pressure from landlords, the village patwaris sent the department the list of original holders listed in the sethwar and not the names of actual cultivators. Based on this faulty list sent by the patwaris, the Revenue Department prepared a new vasoolbaki register, which consisted of the old survey number with the area of land and the new survey number with the area of land. Given the way the vasoolbaki register was prepared, the total area of land in the sethwar, khasra pahani and vasoolbaki registers of the same village did not match in most cases. Over time, this emerged as another anomaly in the land records.

In 1971, the Andhra Pradesh Record of Rights in Land and Pattadar Passbooks Act was passed. This 1971 Act prescribed a format for the RoR and mandated the issue of a new title record to each landholder called the Patta Passbook (PPB). In 1973, the Andhra Pradesh Land Reforms (Ceiling on Agricultural Holdings) Act was passed. When the sethwar (updated from 1932–1934 to 1955) was shared with the villages to implement the 1973 Act, another series of manipulations in land records began, particularly to exclude the names of tenant farmers. The name of the original pattadar was to be changed with the name of the actual tiller, but instead, the names of benamis of landlords and large landowners were inserted. In addition, new survey numbers were added against their names. The old survey numbers could no longer be realistically compared with the new survey numbers, as the areas of land changed with the names of the possessors. In many cases, landholders were not even aware of these changes in land records. As a result, the khasra pahani prepared in 1954 had little comparability with the khasra pahani prepared after 1973.

In sum, no survey of land took place in Telangana after 1932–1934. Most registers prepared after 1932–1934, especially in the 1970s, were products of manipulation engineered by landlords—to evade land ceiling laws—with the connivance of revenue officials and the patwaris.

In summary, the system of land records inherited by Telangana in 2017, which emerged from the Nizam's rule before 1948 and later the united state of Andhra Pradesh, presented a state of disorder. An official report in 2016 listed the ways in which the reality on the ground was different from the information contained in official land records (CAG, 2016). A pilot governmental exercise showed the major reasons for the divergence: irregular mutations in land records; unrecorded partitions; errors in recording names, extent and survey numbers; duplication of khata numbers; issues with inam land; and details of government lands, forest lands, wakf land, bhoodan land and endowment land.12 Here was a typical case of ‘illegibility’ inherited from the past, where a maze of historical sociolegal relationships manifested in a ‘heterogeneity of tenure forms’ and a ‘bundle of competing claims’ in land records (Benjamin & Raman, 2011).

### 5 | THE IMPLEMENTATION OF LRUP

The implementation of LRUP was organised into two parts: The first part (Part A) involved identifying land parcels with either no disputes or undisputed errors (such as errors in names, surnames, extent of land and khata number, and the deletion of portions of land already sold). The second part (Part B) involved setting aside records with unresolved disputes or pending court cases.

- 5.1 | The administrative process


The first aim of LRUP was to update the PPBs of landowners and converge the information in the PPBs and the RoR. The second aim was to update the information on all government land, forest land, wakf land and endowment land. The third aim was to issue new PPBs with security features like QR codes, bar codes and the Aadhaar number. Detailed training sessions were organised for district collectors, tehsildars, deputy tehsildars, senior assistants, junior assistants, RIs and VROs. Publicity was ensured through vehicles fitted with mikes, local television channels and newspapers. 6.1 | The errors of measurement

Beginning from 2018, despite the guidelines set out to address specific disputes, there were a deluge of complaints from affected persons regarding measurement, recording and classification of land under the LRUP.

- 6.1.1 | Types of complaints


The first set of complains were related to Part B. Landowners complained that undisputed lands were classified in Part B; that despite numerous applications, the revenue authorities were hesitant to reclassify their plots; that applications for correction were rejected without citing any reason and the online status was just provided as ‘Grievance Rejected’; that applications remained unresolved for months with the online status staying as ‘Issue has been forwarded to the MRO Office’; and that for these reasons, they were not supplied with new PPBs. When aggrieved landowners went to courts, the government provided a standard response in its affidavits: that the government had notified the Telangana Rights in Land and Pattadar Passbooks Act 2020 and launched the Dharani portal; that it had prescribed various procedures under Section 6 in the Act to apply for mutation of the names; and that all such applications must be made online through the portal. Citing these responses from the government, lower courts dismissed petitions that were filed. If lower courts rejected most petitions, the Telangana High Court admitted some appeals and sent notices to the government.

### 7 | WHY DID THE ERRORS PERSIST?

The errors that accumulated in LRUP and Dharani were not purely administrative or technical in origin. We argue that these issues largely, also, originated from the design of the scheme that spilled over into the processes and outcomes. In this section, we discuss the important points of disjuncture between the aims of LRUP and the outcomes.

- a. LRUP: Conclusive or presumptive titles? What did the LRUP aim for? Did it aim to create a conclusive system or a presumptive system of land titling? It appears that Telangana chose neither; it chose a curious third way. On the one hand, conclusive titling needs a fresh resurvey of land. But Telangana's LRUP was not designed to be a resurvey; it was just an updating of textual records using a labour-intensive house-to-house ascertaining of whether the records held by households were ‘close to the truth’. Only 3.6% of area in Telangana was resurveyed by April 2020. On the other hand, elements of presumptive titling existed in the design of LRUP itself. For instance, sale deeds were extensively used to update textual land records with scant regard to the reality on the ground. The new land titles given were, largely, a derivative of the existing presumptive titles. ### 8 | CONCLUDING COMMENTS

Land is an economic entity embedded in the social, political and cultural specificities of rural areas. The nature of land distribution influences social relations and shapes local power structures. Hence, the need for formal, accurate and updated records of land titles has been an important policy concern in the developing countries. Modern land records are necessary to further redistributionist agrarian goals, reduce exploitation of poor peasants, expand transparency guarantees and facilitate scientific land use planning. This paper was an assessment of efforts to modernise land records in India, with special reference to an effort to update textual land records in one state, Telangana.

Despite the strong case that exists for modernising land records, our overall conclusions highlight the tensions that surround the politics of land in rural societies. Given the strong landlord-led resistance to land reforms, land administration was always a vexed question in the post-War development project. However, the fundamentals of land administration shifted under neoliberalism; governments eschewed egalitarianism, discarded land reforms and embraced the goal of free land markets. Modernisation of land administration also became a tool to further technocratic goals of neoliberal policy, such as digitalised and targeted cash transfers.

Yet, as this case study in Telangana illustrates, the nature of political economy around land can frustrate attempts by the neoliberal state to dislocate land from the bundle of claims and counterclaims that have survived and accumulated over centuries and ‘fix’ conclusive land titles. Although the LRUP was a commendable effort, the Telangana government's claim of having ‘purified’ 93% of the land titles was far from the reality on the ground. There were numerous complaints on the accuracy of the new land records, which snowballed into political protests, violence and interventions by courts. By 2022, the government admitted to multiple issues with the project and its online land management portal—Dharani—and recommended a fresh digital survey.

The problem, we argue, is that the neoliberal state envisages land records modernisation as an erasure of history, in disregard of egalitarianism in land distribution and under the assumption that the ‘illegibility’ of records is a technical problem of administrative dysfunction. Aided by technocracy, the neoliberal state seeks to flatten out the historical multiplicities of claims and counterclaims on land; in their place, it superimposes a new system, actively intermediated by poorly designed portals, which essentially puts in place a new set of claims inadequately vetted by democratic exercises and laced with a new logic of power. The complete exclusion of tenants from the LRUP in Telangana is an illustration. All grievances hit a dead end, with an impersonalised portal absorbing complaints with no accountability to respond—either effectively or in time.

The outcomes of the LRUP reflect the continuing role of land as a marker of rural power. A lesson, surely, is that public policy on land administration must imbibe a better appreciation of broader historical and political

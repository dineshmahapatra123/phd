# Spain vs England Institutional Divergence

## The Theoretical Problem (North 1989)

North (1989) uses the historical divergence between early modern Britain and Spain to test the hypothesis that **institutional differences produce different economic consequences**. Both countries faced the same fiscal crisis in the 15th–16th centuries: changes in military technology (crossbow, longbow, gunpowder) massively increased warfare costs, forcing rulers to bargain with constituents for revenue. The resulting bargains were structurally different — and that difference determined centuries of divergent economic performance.

## The Common Starting Point: The Fiscal Crisis

Both England and Spain at the start of the 16th century faced the same structural problem. A king was traditionally supposed to "live on his own" — income from estates plus feudal dues. The military revolution made this impossible. To survive, the ruler had to bargain: offer constituents "protection and justice" (reduction of internal disorder, specification of property rights) in exchange for tax revenue. The initial institutional response looked similar in both countries — representative bodies (Parliament, Cortes) emerged. The divergence lies in what happened next.

## The English Path: Decentralization and Growth

**Phase 1 — Fiscal bargain creates parliamentary power**: The Hundred Years War fiscal crises under Edward I and Edward III forced the Crown to purchase parliamentary legitimacy in exchange for revenue. As Stubbs (1896) summarizes: "The admission of the right of parliament to legislate, to inquire into abuses, and to share in the guidance of national policy, was practically purchased by the monies granted to Edward I and Edward III."

**Phase 2 — Wool trade tripartite deal**: The wool trade became a vehicle for institutionalizing the bargain. The Merchants of the Staple gained a monopoly of export trade and a depot in Calais; Parliament received the right to set the tax; the Crown got the revenue. A three-way arrangement that gave Parliament real leverage.

**Phase 3 — Common law asserts supremacy**: In the early 17th century, Edward Coke insisted that the common law was the supreme law of the land — repeatedly incurring the anger of James I. Coke led the parliamentary opposition that established common law control over commercial law in the 1620s. The Darcy vs. Allein case and the Statute of Monopolies represented ongoing institutional battles to create rights outside Crown control.

**Phase 4 — Escape from guild control**: Tudor efforts to freeze the economy into guilds and monopolies were ineffective because (a) statutes only covered existing industries — new ones escaped; (b) industries moved to the countryside and escaped guild control; (c) the Statute of Artificers on wages was sporadically enforced; (d) rural justices of the peace had little incentive to enforce it. Economic diversity survived and expanded despite Crown intent.

**Phase 5 — 1689 Glorious Revolution**: The final triumph of Parliament shifted the locus of decision-making from Crown to Parliament, creating imperative credibility (see [[Credible Commitment]]). The consequent Bank of England (1694) and new financial instruments produced a dramatic decline in interest rates — North calls this "the English financial revolution" and identifies it as the best quantitative indicator of improved institutional performance.

## The Spanish Path: Centralization and Stagnation

**The Castilian template**: While Aragon had a commercially oriented Cortes with real legislative power, Castile — defined by centuries of warfare against the Moors — had a Cortes that was rarely summoned. When Ferdinand and Isabella unified the kingdoms, it was Castile's template that prevailed. The Crown gained control over both barons and church.

**The Mesta deal**: The Mesta (the sheep-herders' guild) provided the Crown a secure, easily-monitored revenue stream in exchange for the right to migrate sheep across Castile. The cost: it suppressed arable agriculture, created insecure property rights in farmland, and caused soil erosion. A textbook case of revenue-efficiency tradeoff — the [[State as a Discriminating Monopolist]] choosing a monitorable but inefficient arrangement over a productive but complex one.

**Bureaucratic hypertrophy**: As the Spanish empire expanded, internal control required an enormous hierarchical bureaucracy. Over 400,000 royal decrees had been issued concerning governance of the Indies by 1635 — an average of 2,500 per year since Columbus. Designed to provide minute regulation, this apparatus generated massive agency costs: the history of controlling the Indies is "an elaborate story in agency theory," with distance magnifying the monitoring problem at every level of hierarchy.

**Fiscal collapse**: When military costs of empire outstripped revenues, the Crown raised the alcabala (internal sales tax) from 1.2% to 10% and repeatedly went bankrupt — resolving each crisis by seizing properties and financial assets. The consequence was systematic destruction of property right security, eliminating the investment incentive that growth requires.

## Downstream: The New World Divergence

The institutional templates were exported wholesale. English North America was colonized during the very century of the Parliament-Crown struggle — diversity in the mother country was paralleled in the colonies, with local assemblies developing clear and unambiguous self-governance. The Northwest Ordinance (1787) crystallized fee-simple land ownership and territorial self-governance from 150 years of colonial precedent.

The Spanish Indies were conquered at the precise moment the Castilian Cortes was at its weakest. Conquerors imposed uniform religion and uniform bureaucratic administration. The wars of Latin American independence produced US-inspired constitutions — but without the underlying institutional substrate that had generated those constitutions in the English case. As North notes: Latin American federal schemes were "all tried but none worked." Same formal rules, radically different institutional outcomes — because informal constraints and organizational skills do not transfer with constitutions.

## The Key Analytical Point

The divergence is not about geography, culture, or chance. It is about the structure of the fiscal bargain and its institutional consequences. England's parliamentary constraint forced the Crown into arrangements that, as a by-product of revenue-raising, distributed property rights broadly and created a class of constituents with incentive to enforce those rights. Spain's centralized alternative concentrated property rights under Crown and guild control, generating short-run revenue but long-run stagnation. Path dependence then locked each trajectory in. ## PhD Application

The India parallel is direct: colonial land administration (Zamindari, Ryotwari) instantiated a Spanish-type fiscal bargain — revenue-maximizing rights structures that were easy to monitor and tax but generated no investment incentive for cultivators. DILRMP attempts institutional reform without altering the underlying fiscal logic that originally produced the extractive registry. The absence of conclusive title means the state retains the Spanish option: arbitrary revision of property records for fiscal advantage.

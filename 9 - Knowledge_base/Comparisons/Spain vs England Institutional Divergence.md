# Spain vs England Institutional Divergence

## The Theoretical Problem (North 1989)
North (1989) uses the historical divergence between early modern Britain and Spain to test the hypothesis that **institutional differences produce different economic consequences**. This comparison illustrates how the initial fiscal needs of the state can lock an economy into a path of either growth or stagnation.

## 🇬🇧 The English Path (Decentralization & Growth)
### 1. Political Context
- Power was relatively decentralized.
- Interests unconcerned with the Crown (merchants, lower gentry) coalesced into competing political units (Parliament).
### 2. The Institutional Fix
- The Glorious Revolution (1689) created **Imperative Credibility** by shackling the arbitrary power of the Crown.
- Evolution of **Common Law** provided a flexible framework for property rights and impersonal exchange.
### 3. Economic Outcome
- Secure property rights, low transaction costs, and sustained industrial growth.

## 🇪🇸 The Spanish Path (Centralization & Stagnation)
### 1. Political Context (Castile)
- Extremely centralized enforcement mechanisms.
- The **Cortes** (Parliament) was weak, and the Crown acted as a pure **[[State as a Discriminating Monopolist]]**.
### 2. The Institutional Fix
- Prioritized **fiscal revenue** over efficiency.
- Monopolized industry and trade (e.g., the *Mesta* guild) to simplify tax collection, even though it stifled innovation and competition.
### 3. Economic Outcome
- High transaction costs, bureaucratic bloat, and long-term economic stagnation.

## Downstream Consequences (The New World)
North (1989) argues that these institutional frameworks were exported to the colonies:
- **North America**: Inherited the English decentralized framework, leading to successful development.
- **Latin America**: Inherited the Spanish centralized, revenue-maximizing framework, leading to persistent developmental obstacles.

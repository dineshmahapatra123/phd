# Path Dependence

## Standard Definition
In the context of New Institutional Economics, **Path Dependence** is the theory that "history matters" because the present and future are constrained by the continuity of a society's institutions. It explains why inefficient institutional frameworks can persist for generations despite competitive pressures, and why formally identical rules produce radically different outcomes in different societies.

## Mechanisms of "Lock-In"
North (1990) identifies four self-reinforcing mechanisms:
1. **Large Setup/Fixed Costs**: Creating an institutional framework requires significant initial investment that is sunk once committed.
2. **Learning Effects**: Organizations develop specialized skills and knowledge tailored to existing rules — skills that lose value if the rules change.
3. **Coordination Effects**: Benefits an individual receives from an institution increase as others also adopt it (network externalities). Switching becomes costly even if everyone prefers the alternative.
4. **Adaptive Expectations**: If people expect an institution to persist, they invest in ways that make its persistence more likely — a self-fulfilling dynamic.

## The Process of Change (North 1989)
The mechanism of institutional change is sequential and nested:
1. A relative price change (population growth, military technology shift, new trade routes) alters the opportunity costs of one or more parties to an exchange.
2. The party who could benefit from altered rules renegotiates — first at the contract level, then, if necessary, by devoting resources to changing the underlying rule.
3. Rules are **nested in a hierarchy**: specific contracts, then laws, then constitutional order. Each higher level is more costly to change, creating the "precedent-based" quality of institutional evolution.
4. The result is incremental drift rather than radical replacement — old precedents shape new rules even when new issues arise.

## The Northwest Ordinance as Path Dependence Case
North (1989) uses the Northwest Ordinance (1787) as a paradigm case. Its provisions — fee-simple land ownership, inheritance rules, territorial self-governance, bill of rights — were almost entirely traceable to precedents from 150 years of colonial legal practice. New issues (size of states, slavery prohibition) were resolved through vote-trading with the Constitutional Convention, but the basic framework was path-dependent: prior charters defined the agenda, the decision rules, and the method of resolution. The Ordinance then set the path-dependent template for US territorial expansion over the next century.

## The Serf-to-Copyhold Evolution
A second case: medieval lord-serf relations reflected the overwhelming bargaining power of the lord. But 14th-century population decline (the Black Death) shifted the margin — it increased the serf's opportunity cost of staying, raised their relative bargaining power, and gradually produced copyhold tenure as a new institutional form. No designer specified the outcome; it emerged from marginal bargaining adjustments under changed price conditions. This illustrates that path dependence is not rigidity — it is *constrained* change.

## The Constitutional Transplant Failure
The most powerful evidence for path dependence: multiple Latin American countries adopted constitutions modeled directly on the US Constitution after independence — same formal rules, radically different outcomes. Without the underlying informal constraints, organizational skills, and prior institutional evolution that made the US constitution work, the same written rules failed consistently. As North puts it: "The imaginative federal schemes and courageous attempts at decentralization had one thing in common after the first few years of republican independence: they were all tried but none worked." Formal rules are not sufficient conditions for outcomes.

## ### ⚠️ Contradiction
Path dependence as a theory risks becoming unfalsifiable: if every outcome is explained by its history, what would count as evidence against it? North's response is that path dependence makes specific predictions — discontinuous events (revolutions, military defeat, constitutional moments) should produce lasting divergence, while incremental change should show persistence. This is testable but rarely tested rigorously in the NIE literature.

## PhD Application
- **Zamindari/Ryotwari legacy**: Colonial land administration instantiated revenue-extraction logic into Indian land registries. DILRMP inherits that registry architecture and its presumptive-title logic, even as it adds digital infrastructure.
- **Resistance to reform**: Patwari-based record-keeping, manual mutation procedures, and the absence of conclusive title are not technical failures — they are path-dependent institutional arrangements that serve the interests of the organizations that have adapted to them.
- **Tribal tenure persistence**: [[Khuntkatti]] and related customary tenure systems survive formal legal override because the informal constraints that embed them are more persistent than statutory rules. See [[Informal Constraints]].

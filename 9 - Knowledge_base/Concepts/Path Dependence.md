# Path Dependence

## Standard Definition
In the context of New Institutional Economics, **Path Dependence** is the theory that "history matters" because the present and future are constrained by the continuity of a society's institutions. It explains why inefficient institutional frameworks can persist for millennia despite competitive pressures.

## Mechanisms of "Lock-In"
North (1990) identifies four self-reinforcing mechanisms that create institutional persistence:
1. **Large Setup/Fixed Costs**: Creating an institutional framework requires significant initial investment.
2. **Learning Effects**: Organizations develop specialized skills and knowledge tailored to the existing rules.
3. **Coordination Effects**: Benefits an individual receives from an institution increase as other individuals or organizations also adopt it (network externalities).
4. **Adaptive Expectations**: If people expect an institution to persist, they invest in ways that make it more likely to persist.

## Scholarly Debate
- **Efficient Convergence (Neoclassical)**: Standard trade theory suggests that competition should weed out inferior institutions, leading to convergence among economies.
- **Persistent Divergence (North)**: North argues that the recursive relationship between institutions and the organizations that rely on them creates a "lock-in" that makes radical change extremely costly. Even discontinuous changes (revolutions) are often tempered by the persistence of **[[Informal Constraints]]**.

## PhD Application
- **Historical Property Rights**: This concept is vital for understanding why colonial land tenure systems (like the Zamindari or Ryotwari systems in India) left institutional "residues" that constrain modern land governance reforms.
- **Resilience of Customary Law**: Explains why tribal land rights ([[Khuntkatti]]) persist as a powerful political and social force despite formal legal overrides by the state.

# DILRMP (Digital India Land Records Modernization Programme)

## Standard Definition
The **Digital India Land Records Modernization Programme (DILRMP)** is India's primary central-sector scheme for computerising and modernising land records. Launched as the National Land Records Modernization Programme (NLRMP) in 2008, it was renamed DILRMP in 2016. It is a merger of two earlier schemes: Strengthening of Revenue Administration and Updating of Land Records (SRA&ULR), and Computerization of Land Records (CLR).

## Key Components
1. **Computerisation of Land Records (RoR)**: Digitising ownership, location, dimensions, and encumbrances.
2. **Survey/Re-survey**: Conducting accurate cadastral surveys and creating digital maps for all properties.
3. **Computerisation of Registration**: Streamlining land transaction registration and integrating it with digitised records.
4. **Modern Record Rooms**: Establishing digital record centres at tehsil, taluk, circle, and block levels.
5. **Capacity Building**: Training revenue officers and staff.

## Stated Benefits
- Increased efficiency and transparency; reduced fraud and human error.
- Improved land-use planning through accessible spatial data.
- Enhanced property rights and reduced transaction costs.
- Empowerment of landowners through online record access.

## DILRMP 2.0 (2021–26) — New Components
In 2021 guidelines extended the programme to 2025–26, adding:
- Linkage of Aadhaar number with land records database.
- Computerisation of Revenue Courts and integration with land records.
- Target: 100% basic digitisation by 31 March 2023; full DILRMP completion by 31 March 2026.

## Achievements (as of 2024)
- Computerisation of land registrations: 94% complete nationwide.
- 9 crore land parcels with Bhu-Aadhaar (see [[Bhu-Aadhaar]]).
- Bhu-Aadhaar rolled out in 26 states; 9 states still implementing.
- Map digitisation nationally: ~68% of cadastral maps digitised; ~50% georeferenced; ~71% linked to RoR.

## Scholarly Debate

### ⚠️ Critique: Digitisation ≠ Reform
Kapoor et al. (2024) argue DILRMP's core failure is that it has digitised the *form* of land records without addressing the *substance* of ownership. India's legal framework remains built around registered sale deeds, not land titles. Transactions are registered; the land title itself is not. Previous unclear or erroneous entries are simply migrated into digital form, perpetuating the [[Presumptive vs Conclusive Title]] problem.

### ⚠️ Critique: Erroneous Data Legitimised
- In Gujarat (2018), the government halted DILRMP after widespread complaints; two-thirds of villages had already been processed with errors. Each rectification requires a new survey, creating a cascade of RoR modifications.
- Haryana Police (2022) flagged that digitised thumb impressions on the Jamabandi portal were exploited for fraud — raising data privacy concerns.
- Tamil Nadu CAG Audit (2022) found: 61% of sampled villages had significant discrepancies between manual and computerised land area figures; ~3.22 lakh private parcels incorrectly classified as government land; resurvey work incomplete in multiple districts.

### ⚠️ Critique: Exclusion of Marginalised Communities
Gurumurthy et al. (2022) document that DILRMP:
- Ignores customary tenure rights of tribal groups, indigenous peoples, and marginal farmers.
- Fails to recognise collective land claims in common lands (see [[Communal Rights]]).
- In Coorg, communal *jamma* land rights were recorded as individual rights during digitisation, enclosing the commons.
- In Jharkhand (2016), tribal religious and burial spaces were included in a government land bank portal; data entry errors by private agencies (2008–2016) stripped farmers of their land records overnight.
- Limits "farmer" definition to landowners, excluding sharecroppers, leaseholders, and landless agricultural labourers.

### ⚠️ Bromley's Formalization Critique Applied
Bromley (2008) refutes the assumption that formalising land records automatically creates development — jobs, planned urbanisation, agricultural asset creation, and supporting infrastructure are prerequisite conditions. DILRMP's neo-liberal framing treats land purely as an economic asset, ignoring socio-cultural dimensions (see [[Daniel Bromley]]).

## Structural Problem: Multi-Department Fragmentation
Land information remains fragmented across three departments with no automatic synchronisation:

| Department | Documents | Officers |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| Revenue | RoR, Mutation Register | Collector → Tehsildar → Patwari |
| Survey & Settlement | Village maps, City survey maps | Deputy Inspector → Town Surveyor |
| Registration & Stamp Revenue | Sale deed, Encumbrance certificate | Registrar → Sub-Registrar |

DILRMP has not resolved this silo structure — sale deeds are stored in the registration department, maps in the survey department, and tax receipts in the revenue department.

## PhD Application
DILRMP exemplifies [[Implementation Failure (Land Reform)]] at a programmatic scale. It represents a technocratic, apolitical solution applied to a deeply political problem — consistent with the neo-liberal push (World Bank, donor agencies) to use technology to bypass redistributive politics. The program's failure to address ownership — only digitising possession records — means the [[Record-Reality Gap]] persists in digital form.

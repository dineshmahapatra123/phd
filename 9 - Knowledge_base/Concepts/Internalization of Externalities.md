# Internalization of Externalities

## Standard Definition
The process of altering incentives so that a person or organization takes into account the effects of their actions on others. In property rights theory, this involves creating rights that bring the costs and benefits of resource use to bear on the decision-maker.

## Scholarly Debate
- **[[Harold Demsetz]] (1967)**: Proposes that property rights develop specifically to **internalize externalities** when the gains of doing so become larger than the costs of internalization.
    - **Trigger**: Changes in relative prices, technology, or new market opportunities (e.g., the commercial fur trade) increase the magnitude of externalities.
    - **Mechanism**: Privatization allows an owner to "internalize" the future value of a resource, creating an incentive to manage it sustainably (e.g., husbanding game instead of overhunting).
- **[[Elinor Ostrom]]**: Argues that internalization does not require private property. It can be achieved through communal rules, monitoring, and sanctioning within a group-owned resource.

## The Coase Connection
Demsetz (1967) builds directly on Coase (1960): in a world of zero transaction costs, the output mix that results when property rights are exchangeable is *efficient* and *independent* of who is assigned ownership (ignoring wealth effects). This is the Coase Theorem. Demsetz's contribution is to explain *why* property rights exist and *how* they evolve — the Coase Theorem takes rights as given; Demsetz asks where they come from.

## The Future Generations Problem in Communal Property
Demsetz identifies a specific and underappreciated failure of communal property beyond simple overuse: future generations cannot represent their interests in current resource-use decisions. A private owner acts as a **broker** between present and future — maximising the present value of rights by taking expected future supply/demand into account. Under communal property, there is no such broker. Future generations must "speak for themselves," but have no living agent to place their claims. This creates a systematic bias toward *over-exploitation in the present* that goes beyond the standard free-rider problem.

## Non-Obvious Examples
Demsetz argues economists recognise externalities selectively. Two under-recognised cases from the paper:
- **Military draft**: Taxpayers benefit by not paying the full voluntary-recruitment price for soldiers — an externality caused by *forbidding* negotiation. A "buy-him-in" or "let-him-buy-his-way-out" property right system would internalise the full cost of military labour.
- **Serf-to-free-man transition** (feudal Europe): Serfs could in principle offer payment for freedom based on expected returns to being free — internalising the cost of slavery if voluntary negotiation were permitted.

Both cases show that *prohibiting* property right adjustment is what precludes internalisation — not just the absence of rights.

## PhD Application
This is the core "efficiency" explanation for why new land titling systems are introduced. It provides a benchmark against which to measure political or colonial "capture" models. The future-generations problem is directly relevant to India's land tenure crisis: the presumptive titling system generates a bias toward present revenue extraction over long-term investment incentives — precisely the broker-less communal dynamic Demsetz describes.

## Measurement/Methods
- **[[Comparative Institutional Analysis]]**: Comparing resource outcomes (depletion rates, investment levels) under different property regimes.

# Institutional Efficiency

## Standard Definition
The capacity of a political-economic framework to reduce the "friction" of exchange, thereby capturing the maximum potential gains from specialization.

## The Northian Paradox: Inefficient Property Rights
- **Revenue Maximization**: Rulers often produce inefficient property rights because they are easier to monitor and tax, compared to efficient rights which may have high collection costs.
- **Political Survival**: Rulers cannot "afford" efficient rights if those rights offend powerful constituents or interest groups whose support is necessary for maintaining power.
- **Example**: Monopolies granted to guilds in Colbert's France.

## Scholarly Debate
- **Historical Lesson**: History is not a unilinear path to efficiency. Political systems have an "inherent tendency" to produce stagnation through suboptimal rights. 
- **North vs. Neoclassical Theory**: Neoclassical theory assumes that competition will automatically lead to efficient outcomes. North's 1987 paper introduces **Political Institutions** as a constraint that blocks efficiency gains.

## PhD Application
Explains why [[Eminent Domain]] or specific land tenure laws in India might remain "inefficient" for growth but "efficient" for political survival or state revenue collection. 

## Measurement/Methods
- **Absence of Finance**: Checking for missing credit and banking institutions.
- **Transacting Sector Growth**: Measuring the percentage of the economy devoted to transacting (North & Wallis).
- **Nonmarket Transaction Costs**: Measuring the cost of "queueing," "red tape," and "bribing officials" in third-world countries.

# Systematic vs Sporadic Registration

## Standard Definition
A foundational policy design choice in land registration programs:
- **Systematic registration**: Bulk registration of all parcels in a defined area in a single campaign. Involves on-site verification with neighbours, community consultation, and simultaneous boundary demarcation.
- **Sporadic registration**: Demand-driven; individual landholders apply for registration when they choose. Higher per-unit cost; unequal coverage; higher equity risk.

## Scholarly Debate

- **[[Gershon Feder]] & Nishio (1999)**: Argue systematic registration is superior on both efficiency and equity grounds:
    - **Economies of scale** reduce cost per parcel.
    - **Wider information diffusion**: Systematic campaigns inform all potential beneficiaries simultaneously, including the poor, reducing information advantage for wealthier actors.
    - **Lower land-grabbing risk**: Sporadic registration concentrates benefit among those who apply first (typically wealthier and better-connected), creating opportunity for land grabbing. Systematic registration removes this first-mover advantage.
    - **Thailand model**: First-time registration is **subsidized**; costs are recovered through fees on subsequent transfers. This decouples access from ability to pay at registration.

## ⚠️ Contradiction: Sporadic as Default
In most developing countries (including India), registration is sporadic — initiated by private demand for transactions (sale, mortgage). This entrenches the information asymmetry Feder critiques. The Indian system's mutation-lag problem ([[Mutation Lag]]) reflects precisely the weaknesses of a non-systematic, transaction-triggered updating system.

## PhD Application
The systematic vs sporadic distinction is a direct policy lever for the India land formalization debate. Systematic registration is a prerequisite for achieving the equity goals of programmes like DILRMP. Connects to the implementation failure literature ([[Implementation Failure (Land Reform)]]).

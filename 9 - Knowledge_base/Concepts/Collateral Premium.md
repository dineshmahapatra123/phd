## Standard Definition
The portion of a land tract's market price that results from its ability to serve as collateral for credit. This premium arises because secure land titles reduce information asymmetry in credit markets, allowing owners to access cheaper and more abundant credit.

## Formal Model Derivation (Feder & Feeny 1991)
Feder and Feeny derive the collateral premium from a two-period optimization model. In equilibrium, the market price of titled land equals:

**Land price = Discounted net output stream + Collateral premium**

Where the collateral premium is the additional value that accrues because: (a) titled land can serve as collateral, enabling the owner to access formal credit at lower rates; and (b) this credit access increases the owner's ability to acquire capital inputs, raising expected output.

**Key model result**: Land prices are *negatively related* to the riskiness of land rights. As the probability of land loss rises:
- Demand for land falls (lower expected return)
- Equilibrium land price falls
- Credit supply per unit of land falls (riskier collateral)
- Capital investment per unit of land falls
- Output per unit of land falls
- Social welfare falls below the optimum

The model confirms that risk to land rights generates a cascade of market failures — not just in the land market but in the credit market and factor markets simultaneously.

## The Social vs. Private Welfare Divergence
A subtle but important result: **individuals are willing to support more spending on property security than is socially optimal**. The reason:

When a farmer loses land, it is an asset loss to the individual — but **society does not lose the land**. The land remains productive under whoever next holds it. The individual counts the full asset value in their willingness to pay for security; society only counts the temporary output disruption during the transition. This divergence means:
- Private demand for security programs exceeds socially optimal levels.
- This can lead to over-investment in security measures (gated communities, private guards) and under-investment in productivity.
- The implication for land policy: private willingness to pay for titling programs should be treated with caution as a measure of social value — it overstates it.

Caveat (Feder & Feeny): if secure title expands the land market and allows land to move from low-productivity to high-productivity farmers, there is a countervailing social benefit that can restore alignment.

## Land Reform Implication
Because land prices include the collateral premium, a landless beneficiary receiving titled land under a reform programme faces a fundamental financial problem:

- Full market price = agricultural yield value + collateral premium
- The beneficiary can service debt only from agricultural yield
- The collateral premium component cannot be paid from farm revenues — it represents access-to-credit value that the beneficiary does not yet have

**Conclusion**: Landless beneficiaries with no equity cannot be expected to compensate former owners at full market price from farm revenues alone. Land reform that requires market-price compensation is therefore structurally designed to fail for the intended beneficiaries — they will either default or be unable to acquire land in the first place.

## Thailand Empirical Evidence
The Thailand case study (Feder and others 1988a, 1988b) found:
- Titled land commanded significantly higher market values than untitled squatter land in comparable locations.
- Most of the gap was attributable to credit access differences, not to differences in actual tenure security (squatters' eviction risk was low).
- Titled farmers had larger credit volumes, more land improvements, more intensive input use, and higher output per unit of land.

The finding that **credit access > tenure security** in explaining the collateral premium has a policy implication: in contexts where eviction risk is already low (strong political cost of displacement), the priority reform is not tenure security per se but making land **legible to formal financial institutions** — which requires conclusive title, not just physical security.

## PhD Application
In India, land prices in peri-urban and agricultural areas routinely exceed agricultural yield value by multiples — often 10–50× in peri-urban zones. The collateral premium (and speculative premium) explains much of this gap. However, because India's title is presumptive, the collateral premium is partially captured by formal banks through demanding additional documentation (registered sale deed, encumbrance certificate, revenue records) — high transaction costs that disadvantage smaller borrowers. A shift to conclusive title would lower the information cost of accessing the collateral premium, making formal credit more accessible to smaller landholders. Hedonic price modelling comparing titled and untitled land prices while controlling for quality and location.

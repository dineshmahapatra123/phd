## Standard Definition
The portion of a land tract's market price that results from its ability to serve as collateral for credit. This premium arises because secure land titles reduce information asymmetry in credit markets, allowing owners to access cheaper and more abundant credit.

## Scholarly Debate
Feder and Feeny (1991) argue that because land prices include this collateral premium, purchasers may not be able to pay for land solely from the income generated by farming; they must also possess equity. This has significant implications for land reform: landless beneficiaries cannot easily compensate former owners at full market price using only farm revenues.

## PhD Application
Explains why land in India often sells at prices far above its agricultural yield value, and why land market participation is restricted to those with existing capital.

## Measurement / Methods
Hedonic price modeling comparing titled and untitled land prices while controlling for quality and location.

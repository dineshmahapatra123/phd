# Collateral Value of Land

**Collateral Value** refers to the capacity of property rights (specifically land titles) to serve as a guarantee for loan repayment in formal financial markets (Feder & Nishio, 1998).

## **The Mechanic: Information Asymmetry**
Lenders typically have less information than the borrower regarding loan repayment. Fixed assets, such as land, are used as collateral to align incentives.
- **Titles are Essential**: To mortgage land, the lender must be assured the borrower is indeed the owner.
- **Impact on Credit**: Secure titles reduce **transaction costs** and information asymmetry, allowing farmers to obtain credit at a lower cost and higher volume.

## **Link to Productivity**
Access to institutional credit (facilitated by collateral) enables:
- Increased investment in land improvements.
- Higher usage of variable inputs (seeds, fertilizer).
- Ultimately, higher **productivity per unit area**.

---
**Key Study**: The rural Thailand survey (Feder et al., 1988) found that titled farmers had access to **52–521% more institutional credit** than untitled farmers.
**Source**: [[The benefits of land registration and titling_ economic and social perspectives]]

## Indian Evidence: Patta Passbook Failure
Wadhwa (2002) documents a direct illustration of the collateral problem in India: state governments spent large sums issuing Patta Passbooks to farmers as proof of land rights. Financial institutions universally rejected these as collateral because they are derived from presumptive records — not conclusive title. The scheme "will continue to be of little utility until passbooks issued to land holders reflect guaranteed title to land." This corroborates Feder's theoretical claim with a negative Indian case.

**GDP-Level Estimate**: McKinsey (2001) estimates India loses **1.3% of annual GDP** from unclear land titles — primarily through suppressed agricultural credit, development land lock-up, and litigation costs.

## Standard Definition
The cultural values, customs, and informal norms that legitimise institutional arrangements and constrain human behaviour. These codes evolve slowly — more slowly than specific institutional arrangements, though faster than constitutional orders.

## The Congruence Problem (Feder & Feeny 1991)
Feder and Feeny identify the **congruence problem** as the core mechanism of institutional failure in developing countries: formal Institutional Arrangements fail when they are not congruent with the Normative Behavioral Codes of the population they govern.

Two categories of congruence failure:
1. **Alienability norms**: Formal law may permit the sale of land; social norms may treat certain land as inalienable (clan heritage, ancestral holding, tribal commons). Formal titling programs that assign individual alienable titles to land held under communal norms do not produce functioning markets — they produce legal instruments that communities refuse to enforce, or that are captured by elites.
2. **Registration compliance norms**: Formal law may require mutation of land records upon transfer; community norms may treat possession and social recognition as sufficient — formal mutation is seen as unnecessary expense and bureaucratic exposure. The result is systematic non-compliance (see [[Mutation Lag]]).

## Normative Codes vs. Informal Constraints: Connection to North
Feder and Feeny's Normative Behavioral Codes correspond closely to Douglass North's [[Informal Constraints]] — both refer to the cultural and cognitive rules that persist after formal rules change. The key shared insight: **formal institutional change is relatively easy; normative change is generational**. This is why:
- The same land registration law can produce dramatically different outcomes in different cultural contexts (North's "same-rules/different-outcomes" evidence).
- Reform designed solely at the Institutional Arrangements layer will be absorbed and neutralised by resistant Normative Behavioral Codes unless those codes are also addressed.

## Empirical Cases
- **Sub-Saharan Africa (Feder & Feeny citing Place & Hazell 1993)**: Formal freehold titling programs in Ghana, Kenya, and Rwanda failed to produce expected land market development because community norms treated ancestral land as inalienable regardless of formal title. Informal land rights continued to determine actual access.
- **Thailand shift cultivation**: Tribal communities in upland Thailand had normative codes around rotational land use that were inconsistent with fixed individual title assignment. The formal titling program reached lowland sedentary farmers readily; upland communities remained outside the formal system because the Institutional Arrangement did not match their normative codes.
- **India patwari norm**: The patwari (village revenue official) is normatively positioned as a gatekeeper — farmers approach the patwari for assistance rather than the patwari proactively maintaining records. This normative expectation is stable across states and decades, despite formal laws requiring proactive record maintenance. It is the underlying reason why the dual-notification-chain in mutation systematically fails.

## The Evolution Rate Problem
Normative codes change over generations, not years. This creates a structural mismatch with the political cycle:
- Reform proposals are designed over months; legislation takes years; normative change requires decades.
- Politicians who launch land registration reforms receive credit for the announcement; the failure emerges after the political cycle ends.
- This mismatch is a structural reason why land record reform produces "nothing happened" outcomes even when technically well-designed.

## PhD Application
The [[Record-Reality Gap]] in Indian land records is, in substantial part, a normative codes problem. Farmers do not mutate because they do not believe formal records are the legitimate basis of tenure — possession and community knowledge are. DILRMP's digitisation does not address this: you cannot solve a normative codes problem with a technical interface upgrade. Baxi's incremental approach (conclusive titles for SC/ST beneficiaries and ceiling-surplus allottees first) is normatively smarter — it targets communities with stronger formal-system legitimacy beliefs. See [[Implementation Failure (Land Reform)]], [[Mutation Lag]].

## Measurement / Methods
Ethnographic studies, cultural analysis, and surveys on social legitimacy of laws.

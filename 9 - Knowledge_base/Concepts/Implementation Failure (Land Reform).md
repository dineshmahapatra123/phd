# Implementation Failure (Land Reform)

## Standard Definition
**Implementation Failure** in land reform refers to the systematic gap between formally enacted or recommended land policy and its actual execution on the ground. In India, this pattern is so persistent that the Planning Commission Task Force (1973) noted: "In no sphere of public activity since independence has the hiatus between precept and practice been as great as in the domain of land reforms."

## The "Nothing Happened" Pattern (Wadhwa's Chronicle)
Wadhwa (2002) documents a recursive failure cycle across multiple institutional levels over 13 years (1989–2002):

| Year | Event | Outcome |
|------|-------|---------|
| 1989 | Preliminary study submitted to Planning Commission | Seminar planned, then election called — postponed indefinitely |
| 1990 | Cabinet Secretary writes to Law Ministry | Referred to rural development, then to expert group — never constituted |
| 1991 | Committee of Secretaries approves pilot project in principle | High-level inter-ministerial group proposed — never formed |
| 1991–92 | PM Narasimha Rao announces land record reform from Red Fort (twice) | No follow-up action |
| 2001 | Madhav Godbole committee recommends pilot in Maharashtra | Chief Minister approves — bureaucracy stalls |
| 2002 | National Commission on Working of Constitution unanimously recommends Torrens | Not acted upon |

## Structural Causes
1. **Absent reform lobby**: Unlike labour law reform (championed by multinationals and industry), land law reform has no organised constituency. Diffuse beneficiaries (poor farmers, small urban owners) cannot overcome collective action problems.
2. **Vested interests in ambiguity**: Title uncertainty creates rents for lawyers, revenue officials, and intermediaries — a constituency with incentive to block reform.
3. **Bureaucratic self-preservation**: Administrative apparatus dedicated to managing litigation and disputes has no incentive to resolve the underlying cause.
4. **Operational complexity**: Transition from presumptive to conclusive title requires resolving millions of disputed parcels — a problem departments prefer to defer.
5. **Minutes manipulation**: The Maharashtra case reveals how departments can "indirectly overrule" political decisions by selectively recording minutes — a form of bureaucratic veto.

## Baxi's Incremental Alternative
Upendra Baxi (former Vice-Chancellor, Delhi University) proposed a politically feasible sequencing to overcome implementation failure: rather than comprehensive national reform, begin with **proclamation of conclusive titles for beneficiaries already protected under the Ninth Schedule** — ceiling-surplus land allottees, patta holders, dam oustees, anti-poverty programme beneficiaries, ST/SC communities. His argument: "This will have immediate political acceptance, upon which we can build the grand design."

This is the only serious alternative reform pathway identified in Wadhwa (2002). It directly addresses the **lobby asymmetry** problem by targeting a constituency (weaker sections) with existing political salience, rather than attempting universal reform against entrenched interests.

**PhD Application**: Baxi's approach maps onto the incremental institutional change literature — creating "islands of conclusive title" within a predominantly presumptive system, then expanding outward. This is analogous to special economic zones in institutional reform.

## PhD Application
This concept is central to understanding why **institutional reform** in property rights systems fails even when the diagnosis is correct and politically endorsed. It illustrates the gap between formal institutional change (de jure reform) and actual institutional practice (de facto continuity) — a core theme in [[Path Dependence]] and [[Informal Constraints]] literature.

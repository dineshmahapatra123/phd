# State as a Discriminating Monopolist

## Definition (North 1989)
The theory that the state, acting as a "Discriminating Monopolist," creates property rights that maximize its own revenue and political survival rather than economic efficiency.

## The Inefficiency Paradox
North (1989) explains why political systems have an inherent tendency to produce inefficient property rights:

### 1. The Fiscal Constraint (Monitoring vs Efficiency)
- The revenue a ruler can raise is often greater with an **inefficient** structure of property rights that is **easy to monitor and tax**.
- A more "efficient" structure (e.g., decentralized private property) may have much higher collection and monitoring costs for the central ruler.

### 2. The Political Constraint (Survival vs Risk)
- Rulers prioritize their own security.
- Creating "efficient" property rights might offend powerful interest groups (constituents) whose support the ruler needs to stay in power.
- Even if a ruler understands the benefits of efficiency, they "cannot afford" to implement it if it jeopardizes their political life.

## Evolutionary Consequences
Because the state provides the framework (the "Grid") for all other organizations, the decision of the "Discriminating Monopolist" to prioritize revenue over efficiency leads to long-term **stagnation or decline**, as seen in North's comparison between Spain and England.

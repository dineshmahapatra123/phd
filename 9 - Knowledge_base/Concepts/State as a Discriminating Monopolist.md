# State as a Discriminating Monopolist

## Definition (North 1989)
The theory that the state, acting as a "Discriminating Monopolist," creates property rights that maximize its own revenue and political survival rather than economic efficiency. The state is a monopolist because it holds the sole legitimate coercive power in the territory; it is *discriminating* because it offers different constituent groups different bargains — "protection and justice" in exchange for tax revenue — based on each group's bargaining power and opportunity costs.

## The Bargaining Model
North (1989) formalizes the state's behavior as follows:
- The ruler offers constituent groups the reduction of internal disorder and protection of property rights.
- Since different constituent groups have different opportunity costs and different violence potential, the ruler strikes different bargains — charging higher implicit prices to groups with fewer alternatives.
- Economies of scale exist in the provision of law and enforcement: total revenue rises as the ruler provides more public goods, but the division of gains between ruler and constituents depends on their relative bargaining power.
- At the margin: if the ruler's violence potential rises (or constituents' opportunity costs fall), the bargain shifts in the ruler's favor. The serf-to-copyhold evolution is the reverse — a demographic shock (Black Death) raised serfs' outside options and shifted the bargain toward copyhold rights.

## The Bureaucracy as Agency Problem
The ruler cannot personally monitor and collect from all constituents. This requires a hierarchical structure of agents — a bureaucracy. The inherent consequences of agency theory then apply: agents have their own interests, monitoring agents is costly, and rent dissipates at every level of the hierarchy. North's analysis of Spanish colonial governance illustrates this: controlling the Indies was "an elaborate story in agency theory," with distance amplifying monitoring costs until the agency structure itself became the primary source of economic stagnation.

## The Two Constraints on Efficiency

### 1. The Fiscal Constraint (Monitoring vs Efficiency)
The revenue a ruler can raise is often greater with an **inefficient** structure of property rights that is easy to monitor and tax than with an efficient structure with high monitoring and collection costs. Example: Spain's Mesta guild (sheep-herders) provided the Crown reliable revenue through an easily-monitored monopoly arrangement — at the cost of suppressing arable agriculture and generating soil erosion.

### 2. The Political Survival Constraint
Rulers cannot "afford" efficient property rights if efficiency offends powerful interest groups whose support is necessary for political survival. Even rulers who understand the efficiency argument are constrained: survival dictates a different course than optimality.

## The Alcabala Spiral
Spain's fiscal trajectory illustrates the endgame of the discriminating monopolist model. As military costs of empire outstripped revenue, the Crown raised the alcabala (internal sales tax) from 1.2% to 10% and repeatedly went bankrupt — resolving each crisis through seizure of private properties and financial assets. The result was systematic destruction of property right security: investors faced expropriation risk at the moment the fiscal crisis peaked, exactly when they most needed institutional stability. Long-run stagnation followed.

## Evolutionary Consequences
Because the state provides the framework within which all other organizations operate, the discriminating monopolist's choice to prioritize monitorable-but-inefficient rights locks the economy into a stagnation path. The organizations that adapt to this framework develop skills and investments tailored to it (rent-seeking, clientization, bribery) — reinforcing it through path dependence. See [[Path Dependence]], [[Institutions vs Organizations]].

## PhD Application
India's land registry exemplifies the discriminating monopolist logic: presumptive title systems generate recurring registration and mutation fees (measurable, collectable), while a conclusive title system would reduce state discretion and eliminate the rents that accrue to local revenue officials (patwaris). The fiscal and survival constraints operate simultaneously — DILRMP's failure to adopt conclusive title reflects both the measurement cost advantage of the existing system and the political cost of offending officials whose compliance the reform requires. See [[DILRMP]], [[Implementation Failure (Land Reform)]], [[Political Markets (Transaction Cost Theory)]].

# Extraction Institutions

## Standard Definition
**Extraction Institutions** are socio-economic or legal frameworks designed by colonial or ruling powers to systematically exploit land and labor for the wealth accumulation of the metropole. 

## Scholarly Debate
- **Foundational Theory**: Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001, 2012) argue that where settler mortality was high, Europeans established extractive instead of inclusive institutions, leading to long-term underdevelopment.
- **The Land Grab Mechanism**: Frankema (2010) systematically investigated colonial land policies across former European colonies and found that the redistribution of land from natives to settlers contributed to long-run and persistent asset inequality. The mechanism: dispossession → smallholder landlessness → structural inequality in factor markets → persistent underdevelopment.
- **Beckert's War Capitalism**: Beckert (2014) describes how the British forcibly reorganized land and agricultural production through "war capitalism" — locals were dispossessed and impoverished in service of metropolitan trade imperatives.
- **Banerjee & Iyer (2005)**: Demonstrated that the Indian zamindari system led to transfer of vast tracts of land to a few landlords, leaving cultivators with insecure tenure — an effect that continues to generate regional disparities in agricultural investment, productivity, and poverty.
- **Ecological Enclosure**: In India, the declaration of forests as state property stripped tribals of livelihoods (see [[Khuntkatti]], [[Ulgulan]]); forced poppy/tea cultivation further entrenched extraction.
- **Continuity**: Post-independence India retained the LAA 1894 until 2013, preserving the eminent domain doctrine. The "public purpose" standard remains the cover for what Ghosh and Deodhar (2025) identify as a continuous extraction logic.

## The Opium-Tea-Indenture Nexus: The Specific Economic Machine (Ghosh & Deodhar 2025)
The extraction institutions of colonial India were not random — they were driven by a specific economic imperative: correcting Britain's trade imbalance with China.

**The chain of causation**:
1. High demand for Chinese tea, silk, and porcelain; China accepted only silver
2. British silver dwindling (Spanish controlled Latin American supply)
3. EIC solution: mass opium cultivation in Bihar and UP, forcing China to accept opium in exchange → Opium Wars (1839–42; 1856–60)
4. By mid-19th century, opium revenue ranked second only to land revenue in British India's budget
5. To reduce dependence on Chinese tea: discovery of wild tea species in Assam + smuggled Chinese varieties → conversion of northern Bengal and Assam to tea plantations

**Land governance as enabler**: "Rules, laws and regimes that emerged from organized land systems were used to enforce poppy cultivation contracts on farmers that had clear land titles." Farmers who failed to supply adequate opium quantities or grew food crops lost their land. They became landless labourers or were arrested and transported as indentured labour.

**The indenture supply chain**: Between 1834 and 1920, approximately 1.3 million indentured labourers were transported to British colonies (Mauritius, South Africa, the Caribbean). 85% were from India — dispossessed land farmers filling the labour vacuum after chattel slavery was abolished. India was, as Ghosh and Deodhar put it, the colonial empire's "cost-minimising concoction for the two most vital economic inputs — land and labour": investing in land assessments and assigning absolute rights to zamindars secured both land productivity and a continuous supply of legally bound cheap labour.

## PhD Application
This concept allows for a critical critique of colonial legal shadows in modern land governance, specifically how "Public Purpose" is often a mask for old extractive practices. The continuity runs through: eminent domain (LAA 1894 → LARR 2013) → forced displacement → landlessness → vulnerability. DILRMP's extension of cadastral coverage to unrecorded tribal and forest lands, without recognising customary rights, continues the colonial pattern of using land records as an instrument of state control rather than rights protection.

# Extraction Institutions

## Standard Definition
**Extraction Institutions** are socio-economic or legal frameworks designed by colonial or ruling powers to systematically exploit land and labor for the wealth accumulation of the metropole. 

## Scholarly Debate
- **Foundational Theory**: Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001, 2012) argue that where settler mortality was high, Europeans established extractive instead of inclusive institutions, leading to long-term underdevelopment.
- **The Land Grab Mechanism**: Frankema (2010) identifies the redistribution of land from natives to settlers as a chief driver of persistent asset inequality.
- **Ecological Enclosure**: In India, the declaration of forests as state property (stripping tribals of livelihoods) and forced poppy/tea cultivation (Beckert's "war capitalism") are prime examples.
- **Continuity**: Even after independence, some argue these extractive logics persist through the continued use of the **Eminent Domain** principle in land acquisition.

## PhD Application
This concept allows for a critical critique of colonial legal shadows in modern land governance, specifically how "Public Purpose" is often a mask for old extractive practices.

# Ownership Forms Typology

## Standard Definition
Demsetz (1967) identifies three idealized forms of ownership that exhaust the logical space of who holds the right to exclude:

| Form | Who holds exclusion right | Key characteristic |
|------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **Communal** | All community members jointly | The community denies the state *and* individuals the right to interfere with any person's exercise of the communally-owned right |
| **Private** | Individual owner | The community recognises the owner's right to exclude all others from exercising the owner's private rights |
| **State** | The state | The state may exclude anyone, provided it follows accepted political procedures for determining who may not use state-owned property |

This is the foundational typology — simpler and chronologically prior to the [[Schlager-Ostrom Taxonomy]], which decomposes these categories into five graduated rights (access, withdrawal, management, exclusion, alienation).

## The Logic of the Typology
Each form produces a different externality problem:

- **Communal ownership** → No exclusion possible → Costs of individual use not borne by user → Over-exploitation and under-investment (see [[Internalization of Externalities]]). The "pay-to-use" system is ruled out and negotiating costs make a "pay-him-not-to-use" system unworkable.
- **Private ownership** → Exclusion concentrated in one person → Most externalities internalized directly; remaining externalities (affecting neighbours, not general public) can be resolved by bilateral negotiation at low cost.
- **State ownership** → Exclusion subject to political process → Susceptible to rent-seeking and political inefficiency; Demsetz explicitly brackets this form as outside his main analysis.

## Why Private Tends to Dominate
Demsetz's core argument: private ownership reduces the *number of parties* who must negotiate to resolve residual externalities. Under communal ownership, any externality requires agreement with *every* community member (prohibitively costly). Under private ownership, most externalities only affect a few adjacent owners — negotiation is bilateral or multilateral among a small group, making internalization cheap.

**Key quote**: "An increase in the number of owners is an increase in the communality of property and leads, generally, to an increase in the cost of internalizing."

## Scholarly Debate
### ⚠️ Contradiction: Ostrom
[[Elinor Ostrom]] (2000) rejects the communal/private binary. She shows that the communal form — when properly governed — is neither equivalent to open access nor inferior to private ownership. Her Swiss Alpine and Japanese forest cases demonstrate that communal arrangements can out-perform private ownership where variance is high and monitoring costs are low at the group level. See [[Communal Rights]] for full debate.

### ⚠️ Contradiction: State Ownership Understated
Demsetz largely brackets state ownership. Post-colonial critiques (including [[Ghosh & Deodhar]] via [[In the name of eminent domain_ a historical and colonial perspective to land governance and land struggles in India]]) show that state ownership — combined with [[Eminent Domain]] — is the dominant form in Indian land history and cannot be bracketed as a residual category.

## PhD Application
The typology establishes the benchmark against which India's land tenure evolution is measured. India's history moves from communal ([[Samudaya]]) → state/revenue extraction (Mughal Zabt, British) → incomplete transition toward private ([[Presumptive vs Conclusive Title]]). The failure to reach the private-with-conclusive-title stage is the central institutional puzzle of the PhD.

# Presumptive vs Conclusive Title

## The Core Distinction
- **Presumptive Title (Fiscal)**: Historically found in India. Land records are prepared for revenue collection. An entry is *prima facie* evidence of possession for tax purposes but not a definitive document of title. It can be successfully challenged in court.
- **Conclusive Title (Proprietary)**: The standard in [[Torrens System]] jurisdictions. The act of registration itself *creates* the title. The state certifies ownership and guarantees its accuracy, making it "impregnable against attack."

## The "Record-Reality Gap" in India
Wadhwa (1989) argues that India's current system is "fiscal in nature." Revenue records often lag behind ground truth (e.g., showing deceased owners or unregistered transfers). This disharmony leads to:
- **Retrospective Investigation**: Buyers must verify a recursive chain of private documents to find the "ultimate source."
- **Institutional Insecurity**: Title to land becomes incidental to tax payment rather than a direct state guarantee.

## The Legislative Basis for Presumptive Title: Transfer of Property Act and Registration Act (Wadhwa 1989)
India's presumptive system is not merely administrative practice — it is built into two foundational statutes:

**Transfer of Property Act (1882)**: Section 55(1)(b) obliges the seller to produce "documents of title" for the purchaser's inspection. But these documents are *private* — not certified by the state. They constitute a chain of past transactions, each of which must be individually validated. The state registers the *transaction* (the deed), not the *title* itself.

**Registration Act (1908)**: Provides for compulsory or voluntary registration of *documents*, not of *titles*. The registering officer "is not supposed to concern himself with the validity of the document." Registration is purely a record of the existence of a document; it confers no state guarantee of its substantive validity.

The combined effect: a buyer who complies with both Acts — inspecting documents and registering the transaction — still has no state guarantee of clear title. He has only a chain of private acts, each of which may be defective for external reasons (forgery, undiscovered wills, want of consent, incompetence of a party). The state is a passive archivist, not an active guarantor.

## The Chain-of-Title Problem: "No Transaction Can Ride Higher Than Its Source"
Wadhwa (1989) identifies the structural defect at the heart of deed-based systems: "It is elementary that no successive transaction can ride higher than the original and ultimate source." If any link in the chain of deeds is defective — through forgery, invalid consent, undiscovered will, or adverse claim — all subsequent transactions derived from it are contaminated. A buyer who investigates "to the best of his ability" may still acquire a defective title because the source itself is weak.

This creates a transaction-cost asymmetry: the cost of title investigation is borne privately by each successive buyer; the social cost of cumulative uncertainty compounds over time; and no private investment in investigation can make a defective source title good. Only a public act — state registration of title — can break the chain and create a fresh, indefeasible starting point. This is precisely what the Torrens system provides and what India's deed-based system cannot.

## Proposed Reform
Wadhwa advocates for revisional surveys to convert presumptive titles into conclusive ones, starting with undisputed cases to ensure a "smooth and orderly" transition.

## Fiscal Origins (Path Dependence)
India's land records were designed by the colonial state for **revenue extraction**, not property rights protection. The presumptive character is not a design flaw — it was fit-for-purpose in a static agricultural economy where the state only needed to identify who owed tax. The problem is that this system has persisted unchanged into a dynamic market economy where land has acquired negotiable value and serves as collateral — contexts for which it was never designed. This is a textbook case of [[Path Dependence]].

## Collateral Consequences
The Patta Passbook Scheme — distributing land record booklets to farmers — fails entirely because passbooks derived from presumptive records are rejected by financial institutions as collateral. "Patta passbooks do not satisfy [the] requirement [of authentic evidence of a clear marketable title] because information contained in them is derived from land records and entries in land records do not confer conclusive title." (Government of India, 1989). See [[Collateral Value of Land]].

## Updated: Wadhwa (2002) Evidence
Wadhwa's 2002 EPW article (the full report, vs. the 1989 preliminary study) adds: McKinsey estimate of 1.3% annual GDP loss; the ITDC hotel cases; Maharashtra pilot failure; and the explicit political economy argument that vested interests block reform. See [[Implementation Failure (Land Reform)]].

## Updated: NITI Aayog Model Act (2019) and DLT Alignment
NITI Aayog drafted a Model Act and Rules for Conclusive Land Titling in 2019, allowing objections to title registration within three years of notification. The draft failed to gain legislative traction — consistent with the [[Implementation Failure (Land Reform)]] pattern. Kapoor et al. (2024) argue that [[Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT)]] offers a path to operationalising all three Torrens principles (mirror, curtain, indemnity) digitally, making conclusive titling technically more feasible — but only if upstream data quality problems (see [[DILRMP]]) are first resolved.

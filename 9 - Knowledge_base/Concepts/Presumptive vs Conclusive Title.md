# Presumptive vs Conclusive Title

## The Core Distinction
- **Presumptive Title (Fiscal)**: Historically found in India. Land records are prepared for revenue collection. An entry is *prima facie* evidence of possession for tax purposes but not a definitive document of title. It can be successfully challenged in court.
- **Conclusive Title (Proprietary)**: The standard in [[Torrens System]] jurisdictions. The act of registration itself *creates* the title. The state certifies ownership and guarantees its accuracy, making it "impregnable against attack."

## The "Record-Reality Gap" in India
Wadhwa (1989) argues that India's current system is "fiscal in nature." Revenue records often lag behind ground truth (e.g., showing deceased owners or unregistered transfers). This disharmony leads to:
- **Retrospective Investigation**: Buyers must verify a recursive chain of private documents to find the "ultimate source."
- **Institutional Insecurity**: Title to land becomes incidental to tax payment rather than a direct state guarantee.

## Proposed Reform
Wadhwa advocates for revisional surveys to convert presumptive titles into conclusive ones, starting with undisputed cases to ensure a "smooth and orderly" transition.

## Fiscal Origins (Path Dependence)
India's land records were designed by the colonial state for **revenue extraction**, not property rights protection. The presumptive character is not a design flaw — it was fit-for-purpose in a static agricultural economy where the state only needed to identify who owed tax. The problem is that this system has persisted unchanged into a dynamic market economy where land has acquired negotiable value and serves as collateral — contexts for which it was never designed. This is a textbook case of [[Path Dependence]].

## Collateral Consequences
The Patta Passbook Scheme — distributing land record booklets to farmers — fails entirely because passbooks derived from presumptive records are rejected by financial institutions as collateral. "Patta passbooks do not satisfy [the] requirement [of authentic evidence of a clear marketable title] because information contained in them is derived from land records and entries in land records do not confer conclusive title." (Government of India, 1989). See [[Collateral Value of Land]].

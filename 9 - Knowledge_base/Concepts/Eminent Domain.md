# Eminent Domain

## Standard Definition
Derived from the Latin phrase meaning "supreme lordship," the principle of **Eminent Domain** allows a sovereign state to unilaterally confiscate private property for public use, provided there is a public purpose. It reflects the belief that ultimate ownership of land rests with the state or God.

## Scholarly Debate
- **Divine/Sovereign Ownership**: Rooted in the medieval belief that ultimate ownership of land rests with God, and by extension, the King as God's representative (Psalms 21:1). 
- **Historical Codification**: The principle began its formal codification with the **Magna Carta** (13th century). Later, the **Papal Bull** of 1493 (Pope Alexander VI) legitimized European colonial conquests by sanctifying the right to rule "undiscovered" lands.
- **Colonial Application**: Used by British colonial powers (LAA 1894) to institutionalize coercion for infrastructure expansion (railways, dams) while often undervaluing compensation and ignoring customary rights. 
- **Extractive Institutionalism**: [[Ranjan K. Ghosh]] and [[Satish Y. Deodhar]] (2025) argue that Eminent Domain was a tool for "war capitalism" (Beckert) to correct trade imbalances via forced plantations (Opium/Tea).

## PhD Application
This concept is critical for understanding the legal-economic transition of land from communal resource to a quantifiable, taxable, and tradable asset in the Indian context.

## Standard Definition
A situation where transacting parties (buyers/sellers or lenders/borrowers) have unequal knowledge regarding the validity of land rights, boundaries, or encumbrances. This asymmetry increases transaction costs and risk, potentially leading to market failure or segmentation.

## The Development-Asymmetry Paradox (Feder & Feeny 1991)
Feder and Feeny identify a counter-intuitive dynamic: **asymmetric information about land rights increases with economic development**, not decreases. The mechanism:
- In subsistence communities with stable populations, everyone knows everyone's claims — informal tenure is enforced through social knowledge; asymmetry is low even without formal records.
- As development proceeds, transactions occur between **non-local parties** (urban buyers, distant banks, migrant workers) who lack access to the social knowledge network. Asymmetry rises precisely as the volume and distance of transactions increases.
- The implication: the communities that most need formal land records are precisely the ones undergoing rapid development — a transitional demand that informal systems cannot meet but formal systems have not yet been built to serve.

## Credit Market Segmentation: The Dominant Mechanism
The Thailand case study (Feder and others 1988a, 1988b) produced a finding that challenges naive land reform advocacy: **most of the productivity and investment gap between titled and untitled farmers was explained not by tenure insecurity but by differential access to formal credit**.

Key findings:
- **Formal credit institutions refuse untitled land as collateral** — not because eviction risk is high, but because information asymmetry about title validity makes formal lenders unwilling to accept it. The information cost of verifying an informal claim is prohibitive for institutional lenders operating at scale.
- **Two groups of squatters, one with official acceptance** (but no legal title to transact): both groups performed equally — neither could pledge land as collateral. This isolates the credit access mechanism from the tenure security mechanism.
- **Area dominated by informal credit**: where dominant lenders are local (small traders, moneylenders) with dense social knowledge, the gap between titled and untitled performance was smallest — because information asymmetry was lowest in the informal credit market.

This finding reframes the policy question: it is not primarily about making farmers feel secure; it is about making land **legible to formal financial institutions** that cannot replicate the social knowledge of local communities.

## Informal Credit as Partial Substitute
In the absence of formal title, borrowers and lenders develop informal mechanisms to reduce asymmetry:
- **Physical title deposit**: the landowner deposits the title document with the creditor as collateral — the title cannot be used to transact or mortgage elsewhere while the debt is outstanding. An informal Torrens-like mechanism: title controls access to credit without formal state registration.
- **Local moneylender social knowledge**: local creditors who have lived in a community for generations have private information about claims that substitutes for public records.
- **Limit**: these informal substitutes cannot scale beyond community boundaries and cannot connect farmers to institutional credit at competitive rates. The interest rate premium in informal credit markets reflects, in part, the cost of producing information about tenure security that formal land records would provide at near-zero marginal cost.

## Market Segmentation Consequence
Asymmetric information segments land markets between titled and untitled zones:
- Titled land can attract buyers from anywhere — price reflects full market valuation including the [[Collateral Premium]].
- Untitled land transacts only among parties with common social knowledge — effective buyer pool is restricted; price is discounted.
- The discount is not simply an expected loss from tenure insecurity; it includes an **information discount** — the premium a buyer demands for accepting the cost of verifying a claim that formal title would certify at near-zero cost.

This segmentation is self-reinforcing: lower prices discourage investment; lower investment reduces productivity; lower productivity reduces political pressure to invest in titling programs.

## PhD Application
India's presumptive title system creates endemic information asymmetry in both land markets and credit markets. The 7/12 extract (Record of Rights) explicitly states it does not confer title — it is evidence of possession, not ownership. Formal banks cannot accept it as conclusive collateral. The CanFin Homes testimony (Wadhwa 2002) confirms: conclusively titled land functions "equivalent to a fixed deposit receipt or company scrip" — immediately legible to institutional lenders without further investigation. DILRMP's digitisation of 7/12 extracts does not resolve the asymmetry because the underlying documents remain presumptive. See [[Record-Reality Gap]], [[Presumptive vs Conclusive Title]], [[Collateral Premium]].

## Measurement / Methods
Analysis of land dispute incidence, transaction costs, and credit market segmentation. The Thailand study used a quasi-experimental design comparing titled farmers, squatters with official acceptance, and squatters without — across three regions with different dominant credit regimes.

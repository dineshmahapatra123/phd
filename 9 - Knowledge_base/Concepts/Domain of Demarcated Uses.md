# Concept: Domain of Demarcated Uses

## Standard Definition
The **Domain of Demarcated Uses** is the socially and legally defined set of permitted actions — "uses" — that can be performed with a specific resource. Alchian and Demsetz (1973) use this term to describe the "menu" of possible actions that can be partitioned among different right-holders. What is owned is not the resource itself but a right to perform specific actions within this domain.

**Absolute ownership** of a particular use is achieved when the probability is "1" that the owner's choice dominates the decision process governing that use. Ownership is thus a matter of degree, not a binary — one may have absolute right to pick apples from a tree but no right to prune it.

## How the Domain Is Partitioned
The domain can be partitioned in two dimensions:
1. **Across uses**: Different parties hold different sticks from the same bundle (e.g., right to till vs. right to mine vs. right of easement over the same parcel).
2. **Across decision processes**: The exercise of a particular right may require collective decision-making (e.g., majority vote determines how a publicly owned facility is used), even if the nominal right is individually held.

## The Domain Is Never Fixed: Emergence of New Uses
Alchian and Demsetz emphasise that the domain of demarcated uses is not static — it expands and contracts as technology, values, and economic conditions change. Two historically important cases:

### The Radio Frequency Case (1926–1938)
When radio broadcasting began, no one held the right to prevent others from broadcasting on the same frequency — frequencies were open access, a domain use that had not yet been demarcated. In 1926, the Holland telephone company relayed foreign radio programmes without compensating copyright owners, exploiting the absence of any demarcated right to cross-border broadcast authorization. This triggered the Berne Convention (1928), which demarcated a new right: copyright owners gained the sole right to authorize any communication of broadcast material to citizens of signatory countries. By 1938, the US Federal Radio Commission treated unauthorised relay of broadcast signals as illegal. A new technology created a new use; institutional adjustment demarcated the corresponding right.

### 12th-Century English Land Law
North (cited in Alchian & Demsetz 1973) notes that a relative rise in the value of land in 12th-century England generated pressure to convert the existing right structure into one that allowed for exclusive ownership and transferability. By the 13th century, England developed an extensive body of land law, initiated enclosure, and established the right to alienate land. Rising resource value made demarcation of previously undefined uses economically worthwhile — exactly the mechanism Demsetz (1967) formalises in [[Internalization of Externalities]].

### The Branding Case
Where unbranded animals are communal property but branded animals are private, there is a race to brand — converting the communal domain into the private domain at the cost of branding. Branding is low cost and socially beneficial (identification purpose anyway), so the conversion is efficient. This illustrates how the instability of communal-before-capture / private-after-capture resolves when conversion costs are low: the domain shifts from communal to private without social waste. See [[Open Access (Property Right)]].

## PhD Application
India's land records system is a domain demarcation problem. The colonial-era Record of Rights demarcated agricultural uses (cultivation, tenancy) but left many uses — mineral rights, forest rights, tribal communal rights — undemarcated or assigned to the state by default. DILRMP and Bhu-Aadhaar attempt to extend demarcation to all parcels, but do so without resolving the contested customary uses that tribal communities exercise under undemarcated rights. Demarcating the parcel boundary without demarcating the use-rights within it replicates the problem at a finer scale. See [[DILRMP]], [[Bhu-Aadhaar]], [[Khuntkatti]].

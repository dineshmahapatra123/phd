# Concept: Communal Rights


## Standard Definition
**Communal Rights** refer to a property right structure where individuals have the right to use a scarce resource but lack the right to exclude others from using it. Usage is typically determined on a "first-come, first-served" (FCFS) basis.

## Scholarly Debate
- **[[Armen Alchian]] & [[Harold Demsetz]] (1973)**: Define communal rights as a system where individuals have the right to use but lack the right to exclude. 
    - **The Capture Rule**: Communal systems often function as "communal-before-capture, private-after-capture." This creates a race to capture resources (e.g. hunting, fishing, pollution) where individuals do not internalize the full social costs of their use.
    - **Incentive Problem**: Because current restraint benefits others rather than the restrainer, communal systems are prone to overuse and depletion when resources become scarce.
- **[[Elinor Ostrom]] (2000) Rebuttal**: Argues that Alchian, Demsetz, and Hardin (1968) suffer from a **"Troika of Confusions,"** most notably conflating **Open Access** (no rules) with **Common Property** (rules enforced by a group).
- **### ⚠️ Contradiction**: Ostrom demonstrates that communal systems are *not* inherently inefficient. In contexts like Swiss Alpine grazing or Japanese forests, communal property can outperform private property due to risk-sharing, economies of scale, and lower transaction costs for large-area monitoring. 
- **Incentive Resolution**: Contrary to the Alchian-Demsetz view, communal regimes often develop sophisticated monitoring and graduated sanctioning rules that internalize social costs effectively within the group.
- **[[Gershon Feder]] & [[David Feeny]] (1991)**: Align with the distinction between open access and communal property. They argue that communal rights represent an efficient arrangement for small communities where land is scarce but investment opportunities (requiring private tenure) are limited. These systems economize on transaction costs because the group can readily detect and exclude outsiders.

## The Future Generations Problem (Demsetz 1967 — NEW)
Demsetz (1967) adds a subtler critique of communal property beyond overuse: under communal ownership, *future generations have no living agent to place their claims on the present resource*. A private owner acts as a broker between present and future — their wealth depends on correctly anticipating future supply and demand. No such broker exists in a communal system. Current users are therefore systematically biased toward over-exploitation in the present, even if they genuinely care about future welfare, because the institutional mechanism for time-discounting across generations is absent. This is distinct from the Hardin open-access problem — it applies even when current users are cooperating and well-intentioned. See [[Internalization of Externalities]].

## The Swiss Alpine Dual-Regime (Netting via Ostrom 2000)
The most powerful empirical refutation of the "communal = inefficient" equation. In Swiss Alpine villages, the *same individuals* simultaneously held private parcels for agriculture (hay, grain, vegetable plots on flat land) and communal property rights over the alpine grazing pastures — for multiple centuries. They chose the property regime appropriate to the resource characteristics:
- Flat agricultural plots: low variance, high returns from intensification, easy boundary maintenance → private parcels
- Steep alpine pastures: high variance in grass availability, low returns from intensification, strong economies of scale → communal property

This is not a case of communal property because private rights were impossible to define. It is a case of rational institutional selection: the same rational actors simultaneously used both regimes because each was efficient for its specific resource type. Netting (1976, 1981) documents this case; Ostrom uses it to demonstrate that the choice between communal and private governance is a functional response to resource attributes, not a sign of institutional underdevelopment.

## State-Created Open Access: US Fishing Union and the Sherman Antitrust Act (Ostrom 2000)
Ostrom documents a critical inverse case: communal governance destroyed not by resource scarcity but by state action. US fishers who organised to exclude non-group members from productive fishing grounds were prosecuted under the Sherman Antitrust Act for "restraint of trade." The state, by treating coordinated exclusion as an illegal cartel, converted *de facto* common property (governed exclusion zone) into *de jure* open access. The resulting pattern was exactly what Hardin predicted — but the cause was not the absence of governance; it was the state's active destruction of the governance that existed. This is the inverse of the nationalization pathway: rather than creating formal rights without enforcement capacity, the state here dissolved informal rights that had been effectively enforced.

## Africa Evidence: Proprietorship Is Sufficient for Investment (Ostrom 2000, citing Place & Hazell 1993)
Studies of smallholder agriculture in Ghana, Kenya, and Rwanda (Place & Hazell 1993) found that farmers who held proprietor-level rights — the right to use, manage, and exclude, but not to sell — invested in land improvements and achieved productivity levels statistically indistinguishable from those with full alienation rights. This directly challenges the standard property rights prediction that security of *alienation* is required for long-term investment. If proprietors perform as well as owners, then the policy prescription to privatize (convert communal to individual freehold) for investment incentives is not supported by the evidence. The binding constraint is exclusion security, not alienation. See [[Schlager-Ostrom Taxonomy]].

## PhD Application
Useful for analyzing "open access" scenarios in resource management or public infrastructure where exclusion is either technically difficult or legally prohibited. The future-generations problem is relevant to tribal CPR regimes in India (see [[DILRMP]]) where conversion of communal rights to individual records removes the institutional broker that managed inter-generational resource allocation. The Africa proprietorship evidence is directly applicable to India: the push to convert tribal communal forest rights to individual freehold titles under the Forest Rights Act, justified on investment grounds, is not supported if proprietor-level collective governance already produces investment-equivalent outcomes. See [[Communal Rights]], [[Schlager-Ostrom Taxonomy]], [[Common-pool resources]].

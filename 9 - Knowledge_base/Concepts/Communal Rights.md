# Concept: Communal Rights


## Standard Definition
**Communal Rights** refer to a property right structure where individuals have the right to use a scarce resource but lack the right to exclude others from using it. Usage is typically determined on a "first-come, first-served" (FCFS) basis.

## Scholarly Debate
- **[[Armen Alchian]] & [[Harold Demsetz]] (1973)**: Define communal rights as a system where individuals have the right to use but lack the right to exclude. 
    - **The Capture Rule**: Communal systems often function as "communal-before-capture, private-after-capture." This creates a race to capture resources (e.g. hunting, fishing, pollution) where individuals do not internalize the full social costs of their use.
    - **Incentive Problem**: Because current restraint benefits others rather than the restrainer, communal systems are prone to overuse and depletion when resources become scarce.
- **[[Elinor Ostrom]] (2000) Rebuttal**: Argues that Alchian, Demsetz, and Hardin (1968) suffer from a **"Troika of Confusions,"** most notably conflating **Open Access** (no rules) with **Common Property** (rules enforced by a group).
- **### ⚠️ Contradiction**: Ostrom demonstrates that communal systems are *not* inherently inefficient. In contexts like Swiss Alpine grazing or Japanese forests, communal property can outperform private property due to risk-sharing, economies of scale, and lower transaction costs for large-area monitoring. 
- **Incentive Resolution**: Contrary to the Alchian-Demsetz view, communal regimes often develop sophisticated monitoring and graduated sanctioning rules that internalize social costs effectively within the group.
- **[[Gershon Feder]] & [[David Feeny]] (1991)**: Align with the distinction between open access and communal property. They argue that communal rights represent an efficient arrangement for small communities where land is scarce but investment opportunities (requiring private tenure) are limited. These systems economize on transaction costs because the group can readily detect and exclude outsiders.

## PhD Application
Useful for analyzing "open access" scenarios in resource management or public infrastructure where exclusion is either technically difficult or legally prohibited.

## Measurement/Methods
Contrastive analysis using the **[[Schlager-Ostrom Taxonomy]]** to distinguish between de jure open access and de facto communal governance.

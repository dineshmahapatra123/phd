# Personal vs Impersonal Exchange

## Standard Definition
A theoretical spectrum defining the structure of social interaction in economic exchange. 

1. **Personal Exchange**: Characterized by small-scale production, local trade, and dense social networks. Participation is constrained by kinship, friendship, and personal loyalty. 
   - **Transaction Costs**: Low (due to high trust and repeat interaction).
   - **Production Costs**: High (due to limited specialization and small market size).
2. **Impersonal Exchange**: Characterized by complex interdependence between strangers across time and space.
   - **Transaction Costs**: Potentially high (due to anonymity and specialization).
   - **Production Costs**: Low (due to massive gains from specialization and technology).

## Scholarly Debate
- **Douglass North (1987)** argues that the "breakdown of personal exchange" is the primary hurdle for developing economies. It requires the rise of the state and informal norms to handle the "breakdown of communities of common ideologies."
- **Neoclassical Economics**: Often assumes exchange is costless (zero transaction costs), implicitly operating in a world where institutions don't matter or the transition to complexity is "automatic and unilinear."

## PhD Application
This concept explains the "Great Divergence" in property rights. Societies stuck in **Personal Exchange** rely on informal, kinship-based property rights which cannot scale. Transitioning to **Impersonal Exchange** requires formal, state-backed property systems ([[conclusive title]], [[Torrens System]]) to provide the "institutional reliability" necessary for strangers to contract.

## Measurement/Methods
- **Institutional Reliability**: The level of confidence in outcomes remote from personal knowledge.
- **Development of Third Parties**: The presence of effective government specification and enforcement of property rights.
- **Evolution of Norms**: Tracking changes from ritual/religious constraints to formal law.

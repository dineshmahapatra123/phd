# Concept: Attenuated Property Rights

**Attenuated Property Rights** occur when the **[[Property Right Bundle]]** associated with a resource is incomplete or constrained by institutional rules, blocking the owner from exercising some uses they would otherwise desire (Alchian & Demsetz, 1973).

## Standard Definition
Attenuation can take two forms:
1. **Exclusion attenuation**: The right to exclude others is absent or weakened — producing open-access or communal-right dynamics.
2. **Exchange attenuation**: The right to transact at market-clearing prices is blocked by price controls, zoning, or regulatory caps.

Both forms raise transaction costs and force allocation onto non-price mechanisms, generating predictable behavioral responses.

## The Behavioral Consequences of Attenuation (Alchian & Demsetz 1973)

### Rent Control — Chicago WWII Data
Effective rent control creates a queue of prospective renters who cannot be rationed by price. The percentage of Chicago apartment-for-rent advertisements specifying "restricted" rental conditions or requiring the renter to purchase furniture rose from a pre-war low of 10% to a wartime high of 90% during WWII rent controls. Landlords, blocked from charging market-clearing rents, shifted allocation to non-price criteria: age (childless adults less likely to damage property) and discriminatory personal characteristics. Attenuation of the exchange right transfers the selection mechanism from price to personal attributes — giving systematic advantage to those with more appealing racial or personal characteristics.

### The Freeway vs Toll Road Contrast
A communal right to use a freeway raises transaction costs in two ways simultaneously. First, anyone wishing to reduce congestion cannot exclude non-payers from benefiting (free-rider problem). Second, any temporary congestion reduction attracts new drivers who were previously on alternative routes — the supply of road space creates its own demand, compounding the cost. A toll road eliminates both problems: new users can only enter if they value the route enough to pay, and no free-rider problem prevents private investment in congestion management.

### The Seal Slaughter Case
The Canadian government set a quota of 50,000 seals, distributed on a first-come-first-served communal basis. Because the first 50,000 animals were free, hunters competed on speed — crushing skulls with clubs and skinning animals alive. The hunting methods were not a reflection of hunter morality but of the institutional design: "It is not the hunters who are to blame but the regulations governing seal hunting." Communal attenuation forces socially costly behavior by structuring incentives toward ruthless speed rather than humane harvesting.

## The Central Theorem: Attenuation → Central Regulation
Alchian and Demsetz (1973) derive a general theorem: the attempt to resolve scarcity problems by *reducing* the scope of private rights must inevitably result in a *more centrally regulated or indoctrinated society*. When private rights are curtailed, individual incentive disappears. The state must then either directly mandate the desired behaviour (order the hunt; require participation) or invest in cultural indoctrination to substitute for the missing private incentive. This is not a claim about the desirability of private property — it is a structural argument about the institutional substitutes required when private incentives are removed.

## ### ⚠️ Contradiction
This theorem is a direct target of Ostrom's critique: she shows that neither private rights nor central regulation is the only solution to communal resource management problems. Communities can design collective rules that internalize costs without privatization or state mandate. The Alchian-Demsetz theorem is correct when applied to *unmanaged open access* but overstates the case when applied to genuinely governed communal property. See [[Communal Rights]], [[Common-pool resources]].

## PhD Application
India's land tenure system is a case of cumulative attenuation: colonial layering of Zamindari, Jagir, and government-easement rights created bundles where no single party held the full complement of use, exclusion, and alienation rights. The result is the pattern Alchian-Demsetz predict: non-price allocation (political favour, caste affiliation, bribery), rising transaction costs (litigation, Record-Reality Gap), and increasing regulatory intervention to substitute for the missing private incentive structure. DILRMP's digitization does not address the attenuation — it is an administrative reform without a rights reform. See [[Record-Reality Gap]], [[DILRMP]], [[Presumptive vs Conclusive Title]].

# Optimal Ownership Scale

## Standard Definition
**Optimal Ownership Scale** is Demsetz's (1967) theory that the size and structure of property ownership — whether individual, joint, or corporate — is determined by minimising the combined cost of:
1. **Negotiating costs**: The cost of reaching agreement among owners about how to use a resource.
2. **Policing costs**: The cost of monitoring and enforcing the agreed-upon use.
3. **Scale diseconomies**: The efficiency losses from owning either too small or too large a parcel.

Ownership will tend toward whichever structure minimises the *sum* of these three costs. This explains both why individual ownership dominates for most resources and why joint or corporate ownership emerges for others.

## The Core Mechanism
When residual externalities exist between parcels — effects that private ownership has not already internalised — owners have two options:

- **Contractual agreement**: Negotiate a binding rule about the external effect (cheaper when few parties are involved and the agreement is easy to police).
- **Consolidation (buy-out)**: Purchase adjacent parcels, eliminating the externality by merging ownership (cheaper when contractual agreements would be complex or difficult to enforce).

Which option is chosen depends on which is cheaper — a standard cost-minimisation problem applied to the *structure* of rights, not just to production.

## The Corporation as Proof Case
Demsetz uses the publicly-held corporation as the most significant "exception" that validates the rule:

1. **Why joint ownership at all?** Economies of scale in large enterprises make single-owner operation impossible; equity capital must be pooled from many shareholders.
2. **Why management control (not shareholder control)?** If all owners participated in every decision, negotiating costs would overwhelm scale economies. *De facto* ownership is therefore concentrated in management — a delegation that minimises negotiating cost.
3. **Why limited liability?** Under partnership law, each shareholder would be exposed to the full debts of the firm — an externality that would deter equity capital provision. Limited liability caps this exposure, reducing the external effect of managerial decisions on shareholders.
4. **Why tradeable shares?** Allows shareholders to exit without destroying the enterprise — an "escape hatch" that reduces the cost of the remaining ownership structure and generates the organised securities market.

Each legal modification (management control, limited liability, share tradability) is an institutional response to a specific externality generated by joint ownership at scale. Together they minimise the total cost of operating large enterprises.

## Implications for Land
The same logic applies to land parcel size. Parcels will tend toward the size that minimises negotiating + policing costs given the external effects at stake. Very small fragmented parcels generate high bilateral negotiating costs; very large consolidated parcels generate scale diseconomies in management. The efficient parcel size balances these.

**India application**: The fragmented, multi-document land record system (sale deeds in registration, maps in survey, tax in revenue — see [[DILRMP]]) creates enormous negotiating costs equivalent to Demsetz's communal-property problem: no one can easily establish clear title because no single record serves as the authoritative "ownership" document. The [[Record-Reality Gap]] is partly a failure to reach optimal ownership scale in the *administrative* sense — no single agency holds consolidated, authoritative records.

## PhD Application
Optimal Ownership Scale provides the efficiency benchmark for evaluating institutional design choices in land administration:
- Is the three-department silo structure the optimal administrative arrangement, or does it generate excessive negotiating costs?
- Would a single unified land agency (as proposed by Kapoor et al. 2024 for [[DILRMP]]) lower the sum of costs?
- At what parcel size does the [[Torrens System]]'s guarantee become cost-effective?

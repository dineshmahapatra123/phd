# Land Liquidity

## Standard Definition
The capacity of land to be efficiently converted into other resources — through sale, lease, exchange, pledge, or mortgage — at low transaction cost. **Land Liquidity** is enabled by land registration, which provides the state-guaranteed information both parties need to transact quickly and securely. Without registration, the resources embedded in land are effectively locked up.

## Scholarly Debate

- **[[Frank F.K. Byamugisha]] (1999)**: Introduces land liquidity as a distinct channel — separate from collateral — through which land registration drives financial development. The mechanism: registration → secure transferability → land transactions at low cost → resources flow freely → deposit mobilization → investment.
    - World Bank (1989): "In most countries, real estate accounts for between half and three-quarters of national wealth."
    - UK (1997): land and buildings = 57% of national wealth.
    - USA (1993): real estate = ~70% of all tangible capital; taxable real property ≈ US$6 trillion.
    - Bangkok (1997): real estate = 45% of Thailand's GDP.

## The Mechanism
1. Land registration provides state-guaranteed ownership information at low search cost.
2. Information symmetry between buyer and seller → transactions conclude quickly and securely.
3. Resources embedded in land become **divisible** — landowners can unlock partial value through leasing, pledging, or partial sale.
4. Freed resources flow into credit markets, stock markets, and insurance markets → deposit mobilization → broader financial development.

## PhD Application
India's unclear land records suppress this liquidity channel entirely. The McKinsey 1.3% GDP estimate ([[Collateral Value of Land]]) captures only the credit suppression effect; the broader liquidity-investment channel is an additional, unquantified loss. Byamugisha's framework gives a theoretical basis for estimating economy-wide losses from India's incomplete land registration.

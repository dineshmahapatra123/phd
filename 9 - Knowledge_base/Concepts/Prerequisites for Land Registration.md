# Prerequisites for Land Registration

## Standard Definition
The institutional and economic conditions that must be present for a formal land registration program to generate net economic benefits. Feder & Nishio (1999) argue that registration is "only a means to an end" — without these prerequisites, investment in registration yields little return.

## Feder's Four Prerequisites

### 1. Functioning Formal Credit Markets
Titling unlocks collateral value only if lenders can enforce collateral contracts. If credit markets are absent or heavily regulated (e.g., usury restrictions, cumbersome enforcement of mortgages), the credit-access benefit of titling does not materialise. This explains why in parts of rural India, titling improved land values but not credit access — formal lenders were absent ([[Collateral Value of Land]]).

### 2. Economic Incentive to Invest in Land
Farmers and urban investors must have market-accessible reasons to intensify land use. If output markets are too distant, land is infertile, or input distribution systems are dysfunctional, titling will not trigger investment even if tenure is secure. Thailand's 19th-century land registration became viable only when foreign trade opened rice markets.

### 3. Insufficient Tenure Security Under Current System
If existing customary systems already provide adequate security and facilitate the volume of transactions typical in the area, formal registration adds little value — and may be disruptive. Sub-Saharan Africa (Migot-Adholla et al., 1991) is the key counter-case: customary tenure was sufficient; formal registration was premature.

### 4. Enabling Regulatory Framework
Laws must have realistic evidence requirements, clear dispute resolution procedures, and incentives to register (priority to registered parcels). Overly restrictive land-use regulations (land committees, approval requirements) can nullify market efficiency gains even where formal title exists.

## PhD Application
This framework is a diagnostic checklist for evaluating India's land titling programmes. India partially satisfies prerequisites 1, 2, and 4 in urban areas; rural areas often fail on all four — explaining why DILRMP has generated records without economic impact. Directly confronts the "registration as development" assumption in Indian policy.

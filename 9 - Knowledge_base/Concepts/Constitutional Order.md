## Standard Definition
Fundamental rules about how society is organised — the "rules for making rules." It provides the overarching framework within which institutional arrangements are created and modified.

## The Three-Tier Hierarchy (Feder & Feeny 1991)
Feder and Feeny situate Constitutional Order as the highest and slowest-changing layer in a three-tier hierarchy:

| Tier | Component | Rate of Change | Political Cost |
|------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 (highest) | Constitutional Order | Decades–centuries | Extremely high |
| 2 | [[Normative Behavioral Codes]] | Generations | High (social resistance) |
| 3 (lowest) | [[Institutional Arrangements]] | Years | Moderate |

The hierarchy has a critical implication for reform: lower tiers cannot durably override higher tiers. An Institutional Arrangement (such as a land registration law) that contradicts the Constitutional Order will either be struck down or systematically evaded. An Institutional Arrangement that contradicts Normative Behavioral Codes will be formally enacted but informally ignored — which is the defining pattern of land reform implementation failure in India.

## What Constitutional Order Governs
Constitutional Order determines:
1. **Whether individual property rights exist in principle**: a constitution that does not recognise private land rights cannot be overridden by legislation creating a land registration system.
2. **The scope of state power over property**: eminent domain powers, the definition of "public purpose," and compensation obligations are constitutional-level decisions.
3. **The distribution of powers** between central and state governments over land administration — a particularly acute constraint in federal systems like India, where land is a State List subject.

## The Slow-Change Problem for Land Reform
Because Constitutional Order evolves slowly, it creates **path dependence** in property rights systems. The colonial-era LAA 1894 was a specific Institutional Arrangement, but the eminent domain doctrine it instantiated was a constitutional-order-level principle inherited from British common law. Post-independence India retained this constitutional order even as it formally changed the political structure. The LARR 2013, which replaced the LAA 1894, is also an Institutional Arrangement — it modifies procedures without altering the constitutional-order principle that the state holds ultimate dominion over land.

**North's parallel**: Feder and Feeny's Constitutional Order corresponds directly to Douglass North's distinction between the "rules of the game" (constitutional) and "plays of the game" (institutional arrangements). See [[Credible Commitment]], [[Path Dependence]].

## PhD Application
India's Constitution (Article 300A, post-44th Amendment 1978) protects against arbitrary deprivation of property but does not guarantee conclusive title or mandate a specific quality of land records. The constitutional order thus creates space for conclusive titling reform but does not require it. This is the reason why forty years of Five-Year Plan commitments and successive government announcements about land record modernisation never produced durable change: they were Institutional Arrangement interventions attempting to create what the Constitutional Order does not mandate. See [[Implementation Failure (Land Reform)]], [[Eminent Domain]].

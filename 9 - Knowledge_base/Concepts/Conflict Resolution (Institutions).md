# Concept: Conflict Resolution (Institutions)

**Conflict Resolution** is the primary function of social institutions (rules, customs, laws) according to **[[Armen Alchian]] & [[Harold Demsetz]] (1973)**. 

## **Standard Definition**
In contrast to the standard textbook framing of economics (What to produce? How? Who gets it?), Alchian & Demsetz argue that the "Economic Problem" is fundamentally a problem of **scarcity-driven conflict**.
- **Scarcity** → Competing claims over resource use.
- **Conflict** → Need for a mechanism to resolve these claims.
- **Institutions** → The specific techniques (Markets, Property Rights, Central Planning) that resolve these conflicts.

## **Scholarly Debate**
- **Reframing Capitalism**: Capitalism is not just a market system, but an **institutional mechanism for conflict resolution** through private property rights and price signals (Alchian & Demsetz, 1973).
- **Incentives and Resolution**: Different property structures (Private, State, Communal) resolve conflicts by creating different incentive structures. The "efficiency" of a resolution is determined by the **Transaction Costs** of identifying, measuring, and enforcing the chosen rights of action.

## **PhD Application**
This framing is critical for understanding the **Evolution of Property Rights (Phase 4-5)**. In the context of colonial and post-colonial India, institutional change (e.g., the **[[Torrens System]]** or **[[Ulgulan]]**) can be analyzed as a shift in the *techniques* used to resolve conflict over land—moving from customary Sabhas to state-guaranteed registers.

# Informal Constraints

## Standard Definition
**Informal Constraints** are the unwritten rules of a society, including customs, traditions, norms of behavior, and codes of conduct. North (1990) argues that they are "part of the heritage that we call culture" and are often more important—and more persistent—than formal laws.

## Core Characteristics
- **Evolved Constraints**: They emerge spontaneously over time rather than being created by deliberate policy.
- **Asymmetry of Change**: Formal rules (laws, constitutions) can change overnight, but informal constraints change extremely slowly and are "impervious to deliberate policies."
- **Internal Enforcement**: They are often enforced through social taboos, reputation, and internalized belief systems.

## Where Informal Constraints Come From (North 1990)
Informal constraints come from **socially transmitted information** — the heritage we call culture. Culture provides a language-based conceptual framework for encoding and interpreting the information the senses present to the brain. It is transmitted across generations through teaching and imitation, not through formal rule-making. This means informal constraints carry enormous inertia: they are embedded in habitual behavior, not in conscious calculation, and they change only as culture changes — which is a very slow process.

This has a direct implication: formal rules can change overnight, but the informal normative substrate that gives them meaning cannot. The Russian Revolution — the most complete formal institutional transformation North knew of — nonetheless could not extinguish the informal constraints of Russian society. Japanese culture survived the US occupation after WWII. Post-revolutionary American society remained much as it had been in colonial times. Discontinuous formal change is always incomplete.

## Scholarly Debate
- **Institutional Complementarity**: Informal constraints can either support formal rules (making them low-cost to enforce) or contradict them (making them very high-cost or impossible to enforce).
- **The Same-Rules / Different-Outcomes Problem**: The most powerful evidence for the independent causal weight of informal constraints: the same formal constitutional and property rights rules imposed on different societies produce radically different outcomes. Multiple Latin American countries adopted US-inspired constitutions after independence; all failed. North's explanation: formal rules are necessary but not sufficient — the informal constraint substrate is the decisive variable.
- **Formal Overrides**: Modernizing states often attempt to override informal land tenure with formal titles, but North argues this often fails because the underlying informal norms (e.g., communal sharing, ancestral rights) remain the "active" constraints for local actors.

## PhD Application
- **Customary Land Rights**: This explains the persistent tension between the formal **[[Torrens System]]** of titling (conclusive) and the informal, ancestral claims to land in indigenous contexts.
- **Resistance to Titling**: Explains why technical "solutions" like cadastre and titling often create more conflict rather than less, if they violate the "informal rules of the game" in the village.

## Empirical Grounding: Stateless Societies
North (1990) uses anthropological work to show that informal constraints are sufficient to produce stable social order even without a state:
- **The Nuer (Evans-Pritchard / Bates)**: Despite potential for theft and disorder, the Nuer lived in relative harmony without formal institutions. Order was maintained through kinship-based deterrence: because a feud once started would harm all family members, family members policed each other. An iterated prisoner's dilemma resolved cooperatively through informal enforcement.
- **The Tonga (Colson)**: Communities do not leave members free to "explore every possible avenue." Rules define "what it is people can expect from certain of their fellows" — reducing ambiguity, limiting demands, enabling people to order their lives around a set of priorities regarded as legitimate. Rules "do not solve all problems; they only simplify life."
- **Shasta County (Ellickson)**: Even in a modern US setting, rural residents resolving livestock trespass disputes almost never resorted to legal redress. They used an elaborate structure of informal constraints instead. This directly challenges the law-and-economics claim that formal legal doctrine is the operative constraint in everyday life.

## Ideology as Transaction Cost Variable (North 1987)
North (1987) makes an underappreciated argument: **ideology is the fourth variable** in transaction costs, not a background condition. The "ideology premium" is the cost people are willing to incur rather than free-ride — a measure of their belief in the fairness and legitimacy of institutional arrangements. This premium is influenced by education, propaganda, and symbols, and it varies across individuals and societies.

Critically, specialization creates **ideological fragmentation**: as individuals develop different skills, occupations, and experiences, they form divergent perceptions of fairness and justice. The result is that, ceteris paribus, contracting costs rise as markets expand — because the common normative consensus that enabled cheap informal enforcement in personal-exchange communities dissolves. Informal constraints weaken not from neglect but from the structural dynamics of modernization itself.

This frames a deep tension: the same economic forces that create demand for impersonal exchange (specialization) also destroy the informal ideological substrate that made personal exchange cheap. The transition to formal law is therefore not merely institutionally demanding — it requires replacing an entire normative architecture.

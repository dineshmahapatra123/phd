# Informal Constraints

## Standard Definition
**Informal Constraints** are the unwritten rules of a society, including customs, traditions, norms of behavior, and codes of conduct. North (1990) argues that they are "part of the heritage that we call culture" and are often more important—and more persistent—than formal laws.

## Core Characteristics
- **Evolved Constraints**: They emerge spontaneously over time rather than being created by deliberate policy.
- **Asymmetry of Change**: Formal rules (laws, constitutions) can change overnight, but informal constraints change extremely slowly and are "impervious to deliberate policies."
- **Internal Enforcement**: They are often enforced through social taboos, reputation, and internalized belief systems.

## Scholarly Debate
- **Institutional Complementarity**: Informal constraints can either support formal rules (making them low-cost to enforce) or contradict them (making them very high-cost or impossible to enforce).
- **Formal Overrides**: Modernizing states often attempt to override informal land tenure with formal titles, but North argues this often fails because the underlying informal norms (e.g., communal sharing, ancestral rights) remain the "active" constraints for local actors.

## PhD Application
- **Customary Land Rights**: This explains the persistent tension between the formal **[[Torrens System]]** of titling (conclusive) and the informal, ancestral claims to land in indigenous contexts.
- **Resistance to Titling**: Explains why technical "solutions" like cadastre and titling often create more conflict rather than less, if they violate the "informal rules of the game" in the village.

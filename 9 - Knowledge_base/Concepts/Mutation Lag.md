# Mutation Lag

## Standard Definition
**Mutation Lag** is the delay — often indefinite — between the registration of a land transaction (deed) and the consequential update of the official record-of-rights (mutation). In India's dual-track system, registering a document and mutating the revenue record are separate administrative acts that frequently become decoupled.

## Mechanism
Under India's system:
- The registering authority records the *transaction* but has no mandate to verify title or update the revenue record.
- A separate revenue official (patwari/talathi) is supposed to update the record-of-rights upon receiving intimation from the registration office.
- In practice: intimations are not sent, or are sent but not acted upon unless the party physically approaches the revenue official.
- Result: a property may be sold 5 times with all deeds registered, while the revenue record still shows the original owner from 30 years ago.

## Punjab Revenue Department's Own Admission (1990)
"If a certain property undergoes five successive transactions, the revenue records would not go to guarantee the title-land in favour of the final purchaser if the preceding four transactions are not reflected in the revenue records." — Deputy Secretary, Government of Punjab.

## Scale of the Problem
- Millions of mutation cases pending across India at any given time.
- In the Pune computerisation case: records show buildings as "vacant land" 30 years after construction.
- A famous school in Pune, built in 1963, still shows as vacant land in the Property Card as of 2002.

## Torrens Solution
Under the [[Torrens System]], there is **no registration without mutation** — both acts are performed simultaneously at a single point of contact. This structurally eliminates mutation lag.

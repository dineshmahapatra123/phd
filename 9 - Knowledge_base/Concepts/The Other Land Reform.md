# The Other Land Reform

## Definition
The **"Other Land Reform"** is a term coined by **[[Daniel Bromley]]** (1989) to describe the institutional reform of non-private (public domain) lands at the extensive economic margin, particularly in Africa and South Asia.

## Core Thesis
While conventional land reform focuses on the redistribution of large private estates to the landless (the "first" land reform), the "other" land reform focuses on:
1.  **Institutional Fixes**: Converting [[Open-access Regime]] (res nullius) into [[Common-property Regime]] (res communis).
2.  **Recognition of Pluralism**: Acknowledging that privatization is not the only path to productivity.
3.  **Management Capacity**: Building the rules, duties, and rights necessary for sustainable collective use of common-pool resources (forests, grazing lands).

## Economic Rationale
Bromley argues that the administrative costs of privatization (fencing, title insurance, individual record keeping) often exceed the marginal gains in productivity for low-value lands at the extensive margin. In such cases, common property is the **economically efficient** choice, provided the institutional framework is robust.

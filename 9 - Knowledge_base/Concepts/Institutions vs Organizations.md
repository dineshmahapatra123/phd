# Institutions vs Organizations

## The Fundamental Distinction (North 1993)
North (1993) defines **Institutions** as the "rules of the game" and **Organizations** as the "players." Using a sports analogy (e.g., American football or a team sport), he distinguishes between the framework and the strategic actors within that framework.

### 1. Institutions (The Rules)
- Consist of **formal rules** (laws, constitutions) and **informal constraints** (conventions, codes of conduct).
- They define the **opportunity set** for organizations.
- They are generally **incremental and path dependent** due to network externalities and network complementarities within the institutional matrix.

### 2. Organizations (The Players)
- Consist of individuals bound by a common purpose to achieve an objective (e.g., political bodies, economic firms, social clubs).
- They are the **initiators of institutional change**.
- Incentivized by the rule-matrix, they invest in **skills and knowledge** to survive.

## The Dynamics of Change
Institutional change results from the continuous interaction between these two layers:
1. **Skill Acquisition**: Competition (scarcity) forces organizations to acquire skills.
2. **Revised Evaluations**: These skills lead organizations to revise their perceptions of opportunities/gains.
3. **Pressure for Reform**: Organizations then exert pressure to alter formal rules or gradually shift informal norms.

## The Pay-Off Rule (Pirates vs Producers)
North provides a critical insight into why **inefficient institutions persist**:
- If the highest rate of return in an economy comes from **piracy or redistribution**, organizations will invest in skills that make them better pirates (e.g., corruption, rent-seeking).
- If the highest rate of return comes from **productive activities**, organizations will invest in innovation and efficiency.
- Evolutionary change is NOT always equate to progress; it is simply change toward the highest payoff defined by the matrix.

## The Five Propositions on Institutional Change (North 1993)
North enumerates five propositions that define his theory of how institutions evolve:
1. **Continuous interaction**: The continuous interaction between institutions and organizations is the key to institutional change.
2. **Scarcity drives competition**: Competition forces organizations to invest continuously in knowledge to survive.
3. **Mental models shape action**: The framework agents use to interpret new information determines the direction of change. Ideologies (subjective mental constructs) mediate between environment and action — two agents facing the same facts can draw opposite conclusions.
4. **Economies of scale, complementarities, and network externalities** of an institutional matrix create path dependence — change is incremental and constrained by existing institutions.
5. **Intended vs Realized Change**: The perceived pay-off structure drives intentional change, but outcomes are frequently unintended because the institutional matrix is complex and actors have limited rationality. History is the accumulation of these discrepancies.

## PhD Application: Land Registries
In the context of **Digital Land Records**, the Registry itself is an Institution (Rule-matrix). The local land administrators (patwaris), brokers, and landowners are Organizations (Players). If the institutional matrix (rules for title transfer) favors corruption or obfuscation, the Organizations will invest in skills (bribery, forgery) to flourish under those rules, resisting any "digitalization" that shifts the payoff structure toward productivity or transparency.

The Five Propositions also explain DILRMP's repeated implementation failures: intended rationality (state designs efficient digital registry) diverges from realized outcomes (patwari resistance, data quality failures, Tamil Nadu CAG findings) because the reform does not alter the underlying pay-off structure that makes opacity valuable to incumbents.

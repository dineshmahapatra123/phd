# Common-property Regime

## Definition
A **Common-property Regime** is a well-defined set of institutional arrangements concerning who may make use of a resource, who may not, and the rules for how the accepted users shall conduct themselves. In legal terms, it is **res communis**.

## Key Characteristics (Bromley, 1989)
- **Defined Group**: A well-defined group of authorized users.
- **Defined Resource**: A well-defined resource that the group will manage and use.
- **Rights and Duties**: Members have both rights and duties with respect to use rates and maintenance. Non-members have a duty to abide by exclusion.
- **Private Property for the Group**: Bromley argues that common property is essentially "private property for the group" since all others are excluded from use and decision-making.

## Distinction from Open Access
Unlike [[Open-access Regime]], a common-property regime has a management unit that can exclude outsiders and regulate internal use. The "Tragedy of the Commons" usually occurs due to the breakdown of these institutional mechanisms, effectively converting common property into open access.

---
> [!IMPORTANT]
> The failure to distinguish between *res communis* (common property) and *res nullius* (open access) is the root cause of the "inevitability" argument in the Tragedy of the Commons literature.

# Credible Commitment

## Definition
The fundamental problem of binding players (including the state) to agreements across space and time. A commitment is credible when it is belief-consistent that the party will honor the agreement even when defection is in their short-term interest.

## The Shepsle (1991) Framework
North (1993) adopts Kenneth Shepsle's distinction between two types of credibility:
- **Motivational Credibility**: The commitment is **incentive-compatible**. The players *want* to honor it at the time of performance because the gains from cooperation exceed the rewards of defection. It is self-enforcing.
- **Imperative Credibility**: The player **cannot act otherwise**. Discretion is disabled or performance is coerced (e.g., Ulysses binding himself to the mast to hear the Sirens without jumping).

## The Neoclassical Story of Growth
North uses a "Wealth Maximizing Absolute Ruler" model to illustrate the tradeoff of credible commitment:
1. **The Tradeoff**: A ruler can extract more revenue by granting rights to constituents (increasing their productivity), but this increases his security risk as constituents gain resources to overthrow him.
2. **The Dilemma**: Constituents will not invest or innovate if they fear the ruler will renege and confiscate accumulated wealth.
3. **The Solution**: Creating imperative credibility by binding the sovereign to honor property rights — North's paradigm case is the **1689 Glorious Revolution** in England.

## The 1689 Glorious Revolution as Watershed
North (1993) identifies 1689 as the institutional turning point where England "shackled" the sovereign's discretion over taxation and property. Before 1689, the Crown could arbitrarily revoke property rights, default on debt, and vary regulations. The constitutional settlement created:
- A Parliament with genuine power over taxation
- An independent judiciary protecting property rights from Crown interference
- A credible state debt market (enabling the National Debt and the Bank of England)

This single institutional shock allowed English interest rates to fall dramatically, capital to mobilize for industry, and complex long-term contracting to emerge. The contrast with Spain — where no equivalent shackling occurred — explains divergent industrialization paths. See [[Spain vs England Institutional Divergence]].

## The Law Merchant: Pre-State Credible Commitment
Before state-enforcement of contracts, medieval merchants solved the credibility problem through the **Law Merchant** — a private, decentralized adjudication system embedded in merchant fairs and trade networks. Key features:
- **Community Enforcement**: A merchant who cheated and lost arbitration was ostracized from future trade (multi-period reputation mechanism).
- **Voluntary Compliance**: No state compulsion. Credibility derived from the value of ongoing trading relationships (motivational credibility — self-enforcing).
- **Institutional Innovation**: The Law Merchant is North's example that private institutional solutions can precede and sometimes substitute for state enforcement.

The limitation: the Law Merchant worked only for *small networks with repeat dealings*. As markets expanded beyond the reach of personal reputation, the state became necessary for impersonal contracting at scale. See [[Personal vs Impersonal Exchange]].

## The Temporal Variable
Time is critical for credibility:
- **For the Ruler**: Reputation can only be established over a long horizon. Short horizons (fiscal crises, war) make confiscation attractive.
- **For Constituents**: Learning and uncertainty mitigation require long-term observation of institutional stability.

## PhD Application: Land Governance
In the transition from **Presumptive to Conclusive Title**, the state's guarantee of land title is an exercise in **Imperative Credibility**. If the state retains arbitrary power to alter the registry or eminent domain without "shackled" procedures, the title remains presumptive because the commitment to property rights is not imperatively bound. India's DILRMP faces exactly this problem: digital records without legislative reform to conclusive title leave the state's property guarantee motivationally rather than imperatively credible.

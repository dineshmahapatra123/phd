# Credible Commitment

## Definition
The fundamental problem of binding players (including the state) to agreements across space and time. A commitment is credible when it is belief-consistent that the party will honor the agreement even when defection is in their short-term interest.

## The Shepsle (1991) Framework
North (1993) adopts Kenneth Shepsle's distinction between two types of credibility:
- **Motivational Credibility**: The commitment is **incentive-compatible**. The players *want* to honor it at the time of performance because the gains from cooperation exceed the rewards of defection. It is self-enforcing.
- **Imperative Credibility**: The player **cannot act otherwise**. Discretion is disabled or performance is coerced (e.g., Ulysses binding himself to the mast to hear the Sirens without jumping).

## The Neoclassical Story of Growth
North uses a "Wealth Maximizing Absolute Ruler" model to illustrate the tradeoff of credible commitment:
1. **The Tradeoff**: A ruler can extract more revenue by granting rights to constituents (increasing their productivity), but this increases his security risk as constituents gain resources to overthrow him.
2. **The Dilemma**: Constituents will not invest or innovate if they fear the ruler will renege and confiscate accumulated wealth.
3. **The Solution**: Creating imperative credibility by binding the sovereign (e.g., 1689 Glorious Revolution in England) to honor property rights.

## The Temporal Variable
Time is critical for credibility:
- **For the Ruler**: Reputation can only be established over a long horizon. Short horizons (fiscal crises, war) make confiscation attractive.
- **For Constituents**: Learning and uncertainty mitigation require long-term observation of institutional stability.

## PhD Application: Land Governance
In the transition from **Presumptive to Conclusive Title**, the state's guarantee of land title is an exercise in **Imperative Credibility**. If the state retains arbitrary power to alter the registry or eminent domain without "shackled" procedures, the title remains presumptive because the commitment to property rights is not imperatively bound.

# Concept: Political Markets (Transaction Cost Theory)

## Standard Definition
North (1993) applies transaction cost economics to political exchange to explain why inefficient economic institutions persist. Political markets — the exchange of promises for votes, regulations, or favors — are structurally prone to far greater inefficiency than economic markets, because the measurement and enforcement problems are qualitatively worse.

## Why Political Markets Fail (North's Five Structural Reasons)
1. **Measurement Complexity**: What is being traded in political markets (policy outcomes, ideological commitments, regulatory packages) is far harder to measure than a commodity. Constituents cannot easily monitor whether a politician has delivered on a complex policy promise.
2. **Voter Ignorance**: Voters have little incentive to become informed because their individual vote is unlikely to be decisive. This is the rational ignorance problem — the cost of information exceeds its expected benefit.
3. **Periodic Enforcement**: The only enforcement mechanism for political contracts is the periodic election cycle. This gives politicians very long windows to defect before being penalized.
4. **Ideological Dominance**: Because information is costly, voters rely on ideological stereotypes and mental short-cuts to evaluate complex policy choices. This allows persistent mismatch between promised and delivered policy.
5. **Institutional Stickiness**: Political institutions, once established, create constituencies that benefit from the current rules. These constituencies resist efficient institutional reform, even when the aggregate gains would be positive.

## Contrast with Economic Markets
| Feature | Economic Market | Political Market |
|---|---|---|
| Measurement of exchange | Relatively clear (prices, quantities) | Ambiguous (policy bundles, promises) |
| Enforcement mechanism | Contract law, repeat dealings | Election cycles only |
| Information cost | Borne by buyer | Socially dispersed / ignored |
| Incentive to be informed | Strong (private benefit) | Weak (rational ignorance) |
| Defection lag | Short | Long |

## The Inefficiency Theorem
The transaction cost argument implies that **inefficient property rights and institutions can persist indefinitely** in political equilibrium, because the political market that should correct them operates with systematically higher transaction costs than economic markets. Efficient institutions do not automatically win out; they must overcome entrenched political constituencies operating through a broken market.

## ### ⚠️ Contradiction
This model implies a deeply pessimistic view of democratic reform pathways — change comes through crises, external shocks, or ideological shifts, not through rational voter demand for efficient institutions. This sits in tension with Ostrom's finding that local communities *can* design efficient institutions from below, without passing through the inefficient political market.

## PhD Application
The persistent failure of India's land record modernization (DILRMP) to deliver conclusive title despite decades of investment is explicable through this framework. State governments face incentives to maintain opaque, discretionary land records (enabling political patronage and revenue extraction) and the political market for reform — voters demanding clean title — is too costly to operate effectively at scale.

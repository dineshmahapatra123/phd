# Institutional Reliability

## Definition (North 1989)
Institutional reliability is the degree of confidence that participants have in the stability and outcomes of an institutional framework, specifically when those outcomes are "increasingly remote from our personal knowledge."

## The Requirement for Impersonal Exchange
In personal exchange (tribal/social networks), reliability is based on repeat dealings and a dense social fabric. However, to achieve modern growth, a society must transition to **impersonal exchange**:
1. **Remote Outcomes**: Well-being depends on specialized interdependence over vast distances in time and space.
2. **Predictability**: Participants must be able to trust that the "rules of the game" will be enforced even when they have no personal knowledge of the other party.

## The Role of the Third Party
North (1989) argues that institutional reliability is only possible through:
- **Third Party Enforcement**: The state (government) that specifies property rights and enforces contracts neutrally.
- **Norms of Behavior**: Informal constraints (belief in the rules) that reduce the "measurement costs" of enforcement.

## PhD Application: Conclusive Titling
In land governance, **Institutional Reliability** is the target of a **Conclusive Titling** system (Torrens). A presumptive system has low reliability because the outcome of a land transaction is subject to "remote" claims and "retrospective" investigations that exceed the individual's personal knowledge or ability to verify.

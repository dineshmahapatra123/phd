# Acts in Law vs Acts of the Sovereign

## Theoretical Framework
Wadhwa (1989) identifies a crucial distinction between private transactions and public certification:
- **Acts in Law**: Private transactions between individuals (e.g., sales, gifts, mortgages, inheritance).
- **Acts of the Sovereign**: The state's public recording and certification of these transactions.

## The Domesday Precedent
Wadhwa cites the **Domesday Book (1085-1086)** as an "Act of the Sovereign" that provided a final, written judgment where "no possible appeal lay." A contemporary comment explained that when an appeal is made to the book, its evidence "cannot be set at naught."

## The Desideratum of Alignment
Wadhwa argues that these two acts must "harmonize with each other." If a private transaction (Act in Law) occurs but the public record (Act of the Sovereign) is not updated, the justifiability of statutory evidence laws falls to the ground. The record fails to be "faithful to the external world."

## The Evidence Act Paradox (Wadhwa 1989)
India's Indian Evidence Act (1872) goes "out of its way" to attach special importance to public records. Sections 32 and 35 create a layered presumption about public documents:
1. Public records are **prepared carefully** after verification of facts.
2. They are **maintained accurately** by reflecting changes whenever those changes occur.
3. They are **faithful to the external world** — a reliable reflection of the transactions they seek to preserve.

The statute was drafted on the assumption that Acts of the Sovereign keep pace with Acts in Law. But India's land records are not continuously updated. Agricultural land is "still recorded in the name of a person who died long ago" while the legal successors are not entered; land "goes on being transferred, without quick consequential mutations in the record." Millions of mutation cases are pending.

The result is an internal legal contradiction: the Evidence Act grants special evidentiary status to public land records on the premise that they are accurate; the records are demonstrably not accurate; therefore the statutory presumption rests on a false foundation. The Act of the Sovereign has fallen behind the Acts in Law, and the evidentiary architecture built on the assumption of their harmony collapses.

**PhD Application**: This is the juristic diagnosis underlying the Record-Reality Gap. It is not merely an administrative problem — it is a structural incoherence in India's legal system. DILRMP's digitisation copies existing presumptive records (including their inaccuracies) into digital form, legitimising rather than resolving the contradiction.

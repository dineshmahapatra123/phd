## Standard Definition
The specific laws, regulations, associations, contracts, and property rights regimes created within the framework of the [[Constitutional Order]]. These arrangements govern the interactions between agents and the allocation of resources.

## The Three-Tier Hierarchy and Evolution Rates (Feder & Feeny 1991)
Feder and Feeny (1991) embed Institutional Arrangements within a three-tier institutional hierarchy:

1. **[[Constitutional Order]]** — fundamental rules about how society is organised; evolves most slowly; requires the greatest political capital to change.
2. **[[Normative Behavioral Codes]]** — cultural values, customs, and informal norms that legitimise arrangements; evolve slowly but faster than constitutional order.
3. **Institutional Arrangements** — the specific laws, regulations, and contracts created within this framework; the most readily modifiable layer.

The critical implication: Institutional Arrangements can be changed by legislation in months, but their effectiveness depends on alignment with the slower-moving Normative Behavioral Codes. Misalignment produces implementation failure — the formal arrangement exists on paper but is not enacted in practice.

## The Evolution of Property Rights: Demand-Side Driver
Feder and Feeny argue that property rights arrangements are not static but evolve in response to changes in **population density**, **technology**, and **political power**. The mechanism:
- Low population density → land abundant → communal or open access arrangements sufficient; transaction costs of defining individual rights exceed benefits
- Rising population density → land scarce → competition → rising returns to exclusion → demand for more defined individual rights
- Technological change (irrigation, mechanization) → increased land value → increased returns to investment security → demand for stronger individual property rights

**Thailand case**: As population density rose in Central Thailand, communal tenure gave way to private title progressively — a historical "natural experiment" confirming the demand-driven evolution thesis.

## Informal Substitutes and Their Limits (Feder & Feeny 1991)
Before formal institutional arrangements emerge, communities develop informal substitutes. Feder and Feeny cite:
- **US Great Plains cattlemen's associations** (pre-formal titling): informal norms and association rules defined range rights and resolved disputes — an effective substitute under low population density and homogeneous community.
- **Barbed wire** (1874 onwards): a technological solution that made physical exclusion cheap enough to substitute for formal legal exclusion — allowed the informal system to survive longer than it otherwise would.

The limits: informal substitutes work within communities with **dense social ties and low information asymmetry** (everyone knows everyone's claims). As markets expand beyond community boundaries, asymmetric information between strangers makes informal systems inadequate — this is where formal institutional arrangements (land records, title systems) become necessary. The transition is not automatic; it requires public sector action.

## The Stratification Critique: Titling as a Regressive Intervention
Feder and Feeny raise a distributional critique largely absent from mainstream titling advocacy. Formal titling programs typically involve **fixed transaction costs** — survey fees, legal costs, registration fees, bureaucratic time. These fixed costs represent a larger share of the value for **smaller and poorer landholders** than for large landholders. The distributional consequence:
- Large farmers capture most of the credit-access gains from titling; small farmers may not qualify for formal credit even after titling.
- Where land markets are underdeveloped, the collateral premium from titling cannot be realised by farmers who have no access to formal financial institutions.
- This means blanket titling programs can widen the gap between landed classes rather than eliminating the credit constraint for the poor.

**PhD application**: DILRMP's computerisation of records does not address this stratification effect — poorer farmers in tribal and peri-urban areas cannot afford legal representation to correct erroneous records; wealthier landowners can. Digitisation of an inequitable system may accelerate stratification.

## Public Goods Rationale for State Provision
Land records and title registration are **public goods** (or more precisely, club goods) that private actors systematically underprovide. Feder and Feeny identify two reasons:
1. **Economies of scale**: A centralized cadastre serves thousands of parcels at lower cost per parcel than any private alternative. The Great Trigonometrical Survey, Torrens registration, modern GIS — all exhibit extreme scale economies.
2. **Complementarity with other public functions**: Land records are complementary to taxation (know what to tax, and who), judicial functions (provide authoritative evidence), and police functions (define what is being protected). A private land-records provider cannot internalise these complementarities.

This is the strongest theoretical argument for why the state must provide land administration infrastructure — it is not a policy preference but a structural necessity. Where the state fails to provide it, as in India's presumptive title system, the gap is filled by costly private substitutes (lawyers, intermediaries, physical possession as evidence).

## PhD Application
DILRMP is the most recent Institutional Arrangement designed to reduce asymmetric information and transaction costs in Indian land markets. Its failure replicates a pattern Feder and Feeny would recognise: the Institutional Arrangement (computerised records) was modified without addressing the slower-moving Constitutional Order (LAA-derived eminent domain powers) or the Normative Behavioral Codes (patwari as gatekeeper, mutation as discretionary act). The stratification critique also applies: the farmers most in need of secure title are the least able to navigate the digitised system.

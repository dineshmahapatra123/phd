# Concept: Property Right Bundle


## Standard Definition
The **Property Right Bundle** is the theoretical conceptualization of ownership not as a monolithic claim over a physical object, but as a "bundle" of divisible, socially recognized rights to perform specific actions with respect to a resource.

## Scholarly Debate
- **[[Armen Alchian]] & [[Harold Demsetz]] (1973)**: Argued that property is not the resource itself, but the *rights of action* associated with it. This bundle is "partitionable" and "circumscribed" (limited by prohibitions). 
    - **The Divisibility Principle**: More than one party can hold an ownership interest in the same resource (Landlord, Tenant, Bank, State). 
    - **Structural Questions**: 1. What is the current bundle structure? 2. What consequences (incentives) follow? 3. How did it emerge?
- **[[Elinor Ostrom]] & [[Edella Schlager]] (1992, 2000)**: Operationalized the bundle by defining five distinct rights specifically for resource management: Access, Withdrawal, Management, Exclusion, Alienation.
- **Divisibility**: This concept dissolves the binary between "state" and "private" property, as many resources are held in bundles where different parties (landlord, tenant, bank, state) hold different "sticks" in the bundle.
- **[[Gershon Feder]] & [[David Feeny]] (1991)**: Define property rights as a bundle of characteristics: **exclusivity, inheritability, transferability, and enforcement mechanisms**. They emphasize that the bundle defines the legitimate uses viewed as exclusive (e.g., hunting, passage, gathering, grazing, cultivation, mining).

## PhD Application
This concept is foundational for analyzing how different institutional arrangements partition decision-making authority. In the context of land administration, it allows for the study of overlapping claims (statutory vs. customary) as distinct bundles of rights.

## Measurement/Methods
Analyzed through **[[Comparative Institutional Analysis]]** and the **[[Schlager-Ostrom Taxonomy]]**, looking at how partitioning the bundle affects incentives, transaction costs, and resource allocation.

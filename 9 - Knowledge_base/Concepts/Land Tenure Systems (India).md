# Land Tenure Systems (India)

## The Core Transition Thesis (Ghosh & Deodhar 2025)
Pre-British land in India was **never a freely tradeable commodity**. Under both Hindu and Mughal systems, land was primarily a source of state revenue and subsistence — not private wealth. The British introduced, for the first time, the concept of land as a marketable asset that could be bought, sold, and mortgaged. This was a radical rupture, not an evolution.

## Ancient India: Trustee State and Prescriptive Cultivator Rights
The Sanskrit word for land is *Vasundhara* — "someone that holds wealth" — but land itself is not among the eight forms of wealth (ashta-lakshmi). This reflects the foundational Indian philosophical view: land creates wealth but is not itself wealth. Kautilya's Arthashastra defines economics as "the science of the attainment and protection of that earth" — land is the ground of livelihood, not an asset to be accumulated.

**Jaimini's doctrine (4th century BCE)**: The right of property is not vested in the king; the king has no right to give away private land even as a religious fee. Kings had to **purchase** private land if they wanted to donate or use it for public works. The Nasik cave inscription (1st century CE) records that the Satavahana emperor Gautamiputra Satakarni, wishing to grant land to a Buddhist monastery, first had to buy the tract from its original possessor for **4,000 karshapanas**. This is the opposite of eminent domain: even the most powerful king had to pay market price.

**Ancient land classification**:
- *Rajya*: State/crown land (king as trustee, not absolute owner)
- *Svātva*: Private property — inheritable, giftable, purchasable
- *Samudāya*: Common property — pastures, water bodies, forests, managed by village assemblies (sabhas)
- *Devasvām*: Temple/endowment lands managed by the priestly class
- *Apratibaddha bhumi*: Inalienable cultivated land — if neglected, another could cultivate it for up to five years, after which the original owner received labour compensation only
- *Vivita/Pasuvana*: Dedicated pasture lands, protected from encroachment by the state
- *Setu*: Common pool tanks, reservoirs, embankments — community assets with collective regulation
- *Vana*: Forests, classified by use (resource forests, elephant forests, spiritual/soma forests)

**Prescriptive possession**: Cultivators who had farmed a piece of land for a long period could not be arbitrarily evicted — an early form of cultivation-based tenure security, documented in Kautilya and some Dharmashastra commentaries. If the king's officials neglected their duty, Kautilya required the king to compensate farmers for undue fines or labour demands.

## Mughal System: Revenue without Ownership, First Formal Land Contracts
The Mughals did not own land in the Western sense. They viewed land revenue as "remuneration of sovereignty" — a share in exchange for state protection, not an ownership claim. The key distinction: *Jagirdars* (temporary, non-hereditary military grantees) vs. *Zamindars* (hereditary revenue intermediaries) — neither was an absolute owner of the land they administered.

**Raja Todar Mal's land classification (late 16th century CE)** — the precursor to modern Indian revenue categories:
- *Polaj*: Most productive; cultivated annually; taxed most heavily
- *Parauti*: Farmed in two-year cycles, then left fallow
- *Chachar*: Fallow for three to four years; taxed in five-year cycles
- *Banjar*: Fallow more than five years

**The Patta-Kabuliyat system**: The first formal land contracts in Indian history, introduced under Todar Mal. *Patta* = deed specifying the size, classification, and revenue obligation of the land. *Kabuliyat* = receipt containing the farmer's signature making the agreement binding. Todar Mal used the average produce of the past ten years as the tax benchmark and set the rate at one-third of this average — payable in cash or kind. This dual-document system made land relationships contractual for the first time, though it remained revenue-focused rather than ownership-constitutive.

## British Colonial: Land as Commodity — A Rupture, Not Evolution
The British introduced **three novel elements** absent from both Hindu and Mughal systems:
1. **Permanent private landownership for non-cultivators**: Permanent Settlement (1793) made zamindars hereditary owners — not just revenue intermediaries — with rights to evict tenants and auction land for revenue defaults. Unlike Mughal zamindars, British zamindars held absolute title.
2. **Freely tradeable land**: Under both Hindu and Mughal systems, land could not be freely sold on open markets. The British made it mortgageable, saleable, and auctionable — converting it from a social/political relationship into a financial instrument.
3. **Three settlement types with distinct logics**:
   - *Zamindari/Permanent Settlement* (1793, Bengal): fixed revenue to zamindars; tenant rights abolished; eviction threat enforced collection
   - *Ryotwari* (1792 onwards, Madras/Bombay): individual cultivators paid directly to British; smaller farmers heavily taxed, driven into debt
   - *Mahalwari* (1822–1833, NW India): village-level revenue collection through headmen; gave village elites power while exposing peasants to high assessments

**The cadastral revolution**: The Great Trigonometrical Survey (1802, Lambton) created the "Great Arc of the Meridian" from Cape Comorin to the Himalayas. Cadastral surveys from end of 18th century onwards assigned each landholding a unique parcel (*khasra*) with mapped boundaries and owner names, and a *khata* ledger with owner-wise information. This transformed land from "a traditional, communally viewed resource into a quantifiable, taxable asset." The patwari/kanungo/tehsildar apparatus built for colonial revenue extraction is the same apparatus DILRMP now digitises.

## The British Transition: Summary
| Dimension | Ancient/Medieval Hindu | Mughal | British |
|---|---|---|---|
| King's role | Trustee, must purchase private land | Revenue sovereign, not absolute owner | Legislative authority to acquire land |
| Peasant rights | Hereditary cultivation rights, prescriptive possession | Retained cultivation rights; dependent on intermediaries | Evictable tenants; rights conditional on rent payment |
| Land transferability | Not freely sellable (family/religious transfer only) | Not freely transferable | Freely buyable, sellable, mortgageable |
| Revenue basis | Share of produce (1/6 to 1/4) | Share of produce (1/3 average) | Fixed money assessments |
| Taxation flexibility | Adjusted for drought/calamity | Somewhat flexible | Fixed; rigidity drove debt |

## PhD Application
Modern India's land record system — the patwari apparatus, the 7/12 extract, the khasra/khata ledger — is a direct inheritance of the colonial cadastral system, designed for revenue extraction not property rights protection. DILRMP digitises this colonial infrastructure without addressing the underlying legal character. Tribal communal land systems (Khuntkatti, Northeast customary tenure) were never recognized by the colonial cadastre and remain outside the formal record.

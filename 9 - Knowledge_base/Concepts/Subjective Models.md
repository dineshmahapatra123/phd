# Subjective Models

## Standard Definition
**Subjective Models** (also known as mental models) are the internal representations of reality that individuals use to process information and make choices. North (1990) argues that because information is incomplete and agents possess bounded rationality, these models diverge between individuals and do not necessarily converge toward a single "efficient" or "true" model.

## Core Characteristics
- **Cognitive Frictions**: Individuals are not fully rational calculators (rejection of neoclassical **Substantive Rationality**).
- **Ideology & Beliefs**: Subjective models are heavily influenced by a society's **[[Informal Constraints]]**, including religion, culture, and social taboos.
- **Persistent Inefficiency**: When individuals act on erroneous subjective models, they may develop and maintain institutional frameworks that are profoundly inefficient but persistent (lock-in).

## Scholarly Debate
- **Neoclassical Rationality**: Assumes that even with diverse models, information feedback (arbitrage) will eventually correct them and lead actors toward efficiency.
- **Northian Subjective Models**: Argues that feedback is often too weak or processed through such biased lenses that divergent and incorrect models can persist for generations.

## PhD Application
- **Perception of Property Rights**: This concept explains why local communities might perceive land titling programs ([[Torrens System]]) as predatory or threatening, even if the state promotes them as wealth-enhancing.
- **Elite Bargaining**: Explains how rulers' subjective models of their own fiscal security can lead to the creation of extractive institutions.

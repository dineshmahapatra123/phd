# Subjective Models

## Standard Definition
**Subjective Models** (also known as mental models) are the internal representations of reality that individuals use to process information and make choices. North (1989, 1990) argues that because information is incomplete and agents possess bounded rationality, these models diverge between individuals and do not necessarily converge toward a single "efficient" or "true" model.

## Core Characteristics
- **Cognitive Frictions**: Individuals are not fully rational calculators — a rejection of neoclassical substantive rationality.
- **Ideology and Beliefs**: Subjective models are shaped by organized ideologies (religions, social and political values) and by experience — which either reaffirms or rejects prior norms.
- **The Ideology Premium**: The key empirical handle. North (1989) frames ideology as the premium people are willing to pay rather than free-ride. This premium is a negatively sloped function of its cost: ideology matters more when acting on it is cheap. The lower the cost of expressing a conviction, the more ideological behavior shapes outcomes.
- **Persistent Inefficiency**: When actors process information through biased subjective models, institutions can persist in inefficient equilibria because the feedback that would correct the model is itself filtered through the same model.

## The Neoclassical Behavioural Defence — and North's Rebuttal (North 1990)
North (1990) systematically dismantles the "classic defence" of neoclassical behavioural assumptions (following Winter). The defence has seven steps: (1) the world is in equilibrium; (2) actors face repeated, similar choices; (3) preferences are stable; (4) actors seize all improvement opportunities or are eliminated by competition; (5) no equilibrium allows non-maximizing behaviour; (6) the world approximates these patterns; (7) parsimony favours exploring optimization equilibria rather than adaptive processes.

North's rebuttal, step by step:
1. Multiple equilibria exist — there is "a continuum of theories that agents can hold and act on without ever encountering events which lead them to change their theories" (Hahn 1987).
2. Actors frequently face unique, non-repetitive choices where information is incomplete and outcomes uncertain.
3. Preferences change over time — the demise of slavery in the 19th century cannot be explained without acknowledging changing perceptions of legitimacy.
4. Information feedback may be too poor for actors to identify better alternatives.
5. Competition may be too muted and signals too confused for the evolutionary mechanism to correct behaviour over relevant timescales.
6. Historical evidence provides overwhelming evidence of behaviour that cannot be characterized as rational non-cooperative wealth-maximization.
7. Neoclassical behavioural assumptions are useful for analysing organized markets but are "the fundamental stumbling block preventing an understanding of the existence, formation, and evolution of institutions."

## The CD Gap (Heiner 1983)
Ronald Heiner's "The Origins of Predictable Behavior" (1983) — cited by North as a key parallel contribution — argues that the gap between an agent's *competence* in deciphering problems and the *difficulty* of selecting optimal alternatives (the "CD gap") is the source of predictable, regularized behaviour. The greater the gap, the more agents impose limited, rule-governed response patterns to manage complexity and uncertainty. Heiner's insight is that uncertainty does not produce erratic behaviour — it produces *institutional* behaviour. Individuals create routines and rules precisely because they cannot compute optima. North absorbs this: subjective models are not just cognitive limitations but are the *driver* of institutional formation.

## Substantive vs Procedural Rationality (Simon)
Herbert Simon's distinction is essential to North's framework:
- **Substantive rationality**: The rational person reaches the decision that is objectively best given their utility function. Assumes unlimited computational power and an accurate model of the world.
- **Procedural rationality**: The rational person goes about making decisions in a way that is *procedurally reasonable* given available knowledge and computational means. Accepts that the decision-maker's subjective representation of the problem — their frame — is constitutive of the decision.

North's institutional economics requires procedural rationality: if decision-makers had substantive rationality, institutions would be unnecessary (zero-cost information processing would make rules superfluous). It is precisely because humans are procedurally rather than substantively rational that institutions — regularized patterns of interaction — are necessary and persistent.

## Three Empirical Cases (North 1989)

### 1. The Voter Paradox
Individual votes almost never decide elections, yet many people vote. Brennan and Buchanan identify this as a puzzle. North's resolution: voters are expressing strongly held convictions at low personal cost — the ideology premium is high because the price of acting on it is nearly zero. This is ideological expression, not instrumental calculation.

### 2. Legislators Voting Their Convictions
Empirical work on legislators shows they frequently vote their own convictions rather than their constituents' revealed interests. The institutional structure of Congress also allows strategic voting that conceals true objectives. Subjective models (ideological commitments) persistently shape legislative outcomes even when observable constituent preferences diverge.

### 3. The Hamilton/Burr Duel
Alexander Hamilton, on the last night of his life, wrote out all rational reasons why he should not duel Aaron Burr — overwhelmingly convincing reasons that proved insufficient to override the code of conduct requiring gentlemanly dispute resolution through dueling. The norm was externally enforced (by the attitudes of others) and internally enforced (by Hamilton's own conception of honor). A perfectly rational man, fully aware of the irrationality of his action, could not act otherwise. This is the subjective model overriding instrumental calculation.

### 4. The Abolition of Slavery
The demise of slavery is not explicable in a pure interest-group model. Most of those who voted for abolition bore no personal cost — they could simply express their abhorrence of one human being owning another at zero price. North's argument: the ideology premium at zero cost is extremely high, and ideological movements can produce major institutional change precisely because they mobilize action where the individual cost of conviction is near zero. The interest-group model cannot explain this; the subjective model framework can.

## The Hierarchy of Norm-Formation
North (1989) distinguishes between two types of informal constraints that shape subjective models:
1. **Derivative norms**: Extensions and elaborations of formal rules — relatively easy to analyze because they follow the logic of the rule structure.
2. **Independent norms**: Codes of conduct, taboos, standards of behavior shaped by ideology and experience, which can persist even when formal rules change. These are the "tenaciously surviving" norms — integral parts of habitual behavior — that form the deepest layer of institutional stability.

## ### ⚠️ Contradiction
Neoclassical economics assumes that information arbitrage eventually corrects divergent models. North argues feedback is too weak and too ideologically filtered for this correction to occur reliably. However, the subjective model framework is difficult to operationalize: if mental models are unobservable, how does one test when they — rather than material interests — are doing explanatory work? This is a persistent methodological gap in the NIE program.

## PhD Application
- **Land titling resistance**: Local communities may perceive DILRMP and Bhu-Aadhaar as predatory even when framed as wealth-enhancing, because their subjective model of state-land relations is shaped by centuries of extractive colonial administration. The reform's credibility problem is not informational — it is ideological.
- **Elite subjective models**: Land revenue officials' subjective models of their own role (gatekeeper rather than registrar) shape their resistance to digitization, independently of explicit rent-seeking calculation. See [[Informal Constraints]], [[Institutions vs Organizations]].

# Transaction Costs

## Definition (North 1989)
Transaction costs are the costs involved in the exchange process, separate from the traditional costs of production. They are the fundamental "key to the performance of economies" and the primary obstacle to realizing the gains from trade (Adam Smith).

## The Neoclassical Critique
North (1989) builds on **Ronald Coase (1960)** to argue that the standard neoclassical model only holds under the "severely restrictive assumption of zero transaction costs." In a world of positive transaction costs, **institutions matter** because they define the structure of exchange.

## Two Components of Transaction Costs
North decomposes transaction costs into two primary drivers:

### 1. Measurement Costs
- **Attributes vs Entities**: Individuals receive utility from the various attributes (dimensions) of a good or service rather than the entity itself.
- **The Problem**: It is costly to measure all separable dimensions (quality, quantity, weight, reliability).
- **Impact**: Measurement technology (human senses, standardization) directly influences the ability to define property rights.

### 2. Enforcement Costs
- **Imperfect Enforcement**: Because measurement is costly, enforcement is typically imperfect.
- **Agency Problems**: Rules are enforced by agents (police, judges, foremen) whose interests are not identical to the principals.
- **Opportunism**: Measurement costs create "slack" that allows players to gain by cheating, shirking, or opportunistic behavior.

## The Growth Paradox
Western growth is characterized by high **aggregate** transaction costs but low **per-transaction** costs. As specialization increases, the "transaction sector" grows (from 1/4 of GNP in 1870 to 1/2 in 1970 in the U.S.), but institutions make these exchanges reliable enough to occur across vast distances.

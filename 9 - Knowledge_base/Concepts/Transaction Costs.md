# Transaction Costs

## Definition (North 1987)
Transaction costs are the costs involved in the exchange process, separate from the traditional costs of production. They are the fundamental "key to the performance of economies" and the primary obstacle to realizing the gains from trade (Adam Smith). In a world of zero transaction costs, **institutions do not matter** — Coase (1960) showed this. North's project is to explain why, in a world of positive transaction costs, the institutional structure of property rights determines economic performance.

## The Neoclassical Critique
North (1987, 1989) builds on **Ronald Coase (1960)** to argue that the standard neoclassical model only holds under the "severely restrictive assumption of zero transaction costs." In a world of positive transaction costs, **institutions matter** because they define the structure of exchange. Growth is not automatic or unilinear; it depends on the institutional framework that resolves the four fundamental cost variables.

## The Four Variables of Transaction Costs (North 1987)
North (1987) offers a more complete decomposition than the standard two-factor version. There are **four** fundamental variables, not two:

### 1. Measurement Costs
- **Attributes vs Entities**: Individuals receive utility from the various attributes (dimensions) of a good or service rather than the entity itself.
- **The Problem**: It is costly to measure all separable dimensions (quality, quantity, weight, reliability of performance).
- **Impact**: Measurement technology directly influences the ability to define and enforce property rights. In a world of perfect and costless information, measurement — and therefore transaction costs — would be unnecessary.

### 2. Exchange Process / Market Scale
- In **personal exchange** (kinship, repeat dealings), informal constraints substitute for formal contracts: "a handshake suffices for even complicated exchange."
- In **impersonal exchange** with strangers across time and space, these informal substitutes vanish. Contracts must be precisely specified and elaborate safeguards devised.
- The size and scale of the market is therefore a direct cost variable: expanding markets raise the transaction costs of specification and enforcement per exchange.

### 3. Enforcement
- In a world of perfect enforcement, a neutral third party impartially evaluates disputes and awards compensation. Opportunism would never pay.
- In reality, measurement difficulty makes it hard to determine whether a contract has been violated. A coercive third party (state) is therefore **essential** but not sufficient: the state can also be captured and used to raise transaction costs in favour of powerful constituencies. See [[State as a Discriminating Monopolist]].

### 4. Ideology
- **The most underappreciated variable.** Neoclassical economics treats preferences as constant. North argues ideological attitudes are **not constant** and directly affect the cost of contracting.
- The "ideology premium" is the cost individuals are willing to incur rather than free-ride — a function of their beliefs about the justice and fairness of institutional arrangements.
- Ideology can be influenced by education, propaganda, and symbols. Politicians, employers, and labor leaders invest heavily in shaping it because it determines compliance costs.
- Specialization creates a specific pathology: as individuals have different experiences, they develop **divergent perceptions of fairness**, so ceteris paribus contracting costs rise because common normative consensus disappears. See [[Informal Constraints]].

## The Growth Paradox
Western growth is characterized by high **aggregate** transaction costs but low **per-transaction** costs. As specialization increases, the "transaction sector" grows (from roughly 25% of GNP in 1870 to 50% in 1970 in the US — North & Wallis 1986), but institutions make these exchanges reliable enough to occur across vast distances. The paradox: more transacting is a sign of *success*, not inefficiency, if each transaction is cheap and reliably enforced.

## Measuring Transaction Costs: The National Income Problem
Standard GDP accounting conceals transaction costs in three ways (North 1987):
1. Transaction costs may be so high that production simply does not occur — the cost is invisible in the data.
2. Transaction costs are not separated from production costs in national accounts.
3. Nonmarket transaction costs (queueing, red tape, permit costs, bribery of officials) go through no market and are therefore unmeasured entirely.

This last category is particularly important for developing economies: the true cost of the bazaar/suq economy or of India's land registry system lies largely in nonmarket transaction costs (time, uncertainty, corruption), which GDP cannot capture.

# Concept: Common-pool resources (CPR)

## Standard Definition
A **Common-pool resource** is a type of economic good characterised by two structural attributes (Ostrom 2000):
1. **High Cost of Exclusion**: It is difficult or costly to exclude potential beneficiaries via physical or legal barriers.
2. **High Subtractability**: Consumption by one individual subtracts from what is available to others.

CPRs are defined by the nature of the *resource*, not by the property regime governing it. A CPR can be governed as private property, state property, communal property, or left as open access. The property regime is a separate analytical variable.

## The Resource System / Resource Units Distinction (Ostrom 2000)
Every CPR has two analytically separable components:
- **Resource system** (the stock or facility): the lake, forest, fishery stock, groundwater basin, irrigation canal, or mainframe computer that generates benefits over time.
- **Resource units** (the flow): water, timber, fish, fodder, processing time drawn from the system.

Effective governance requires two distinct sets of rules — rules limiting *access to the system* and rules limiting the *amount, timing, and technology* of withdrawal of units from the system. Many governance failures occur because only one set of rules is specified. A fishery quota (unit rule) without entry limits (system rule) still depletes the stock; irrigation water allocation (unit rule) without maintenance obligations (system rule) lets the canal deteriorate.

## The Troika of Confusions (Ostrom 2000)
Ostrom identifies three terminological confusions that have systematically distorted the debate:
1. **Common property ≠ Open access**: Open access means no one has the legal right to exclude anyone. Common property means a defined group has the right to exclude non-members. These produce opposite incentive structures and are routinely conflated in the "Tragedy of the Commons" literature.
2. **CPR ≠ Common property regime**: The resource type (CPR) does not determine the property regime. Calling something a "common property resource" wrongly implies it must be governed communally.
3. **Resource system ≠ Resource units**: Rules governing the stock and rules governing the flow are different and must both be specified.

## When Communal Property Is Conditionally Efficient: Netting's 5 Attributes
Netting (1976, 1981) studying Swiss Alpine peasants identified five resource attributes that make communal proprietorship the efficient governance form:
1. Low value of production per unit of area
2. High variance in availability of resource units on any one parcel
3. Low returns from intensification of investment
4. Substantial economies of scale from utilizing a large area
5. Substantial economies of scale in building shared infrastructure (roads, retaining walls, processing facilities)

When these conditions hold — as in steep Alpine pastureland — communal property outperforms private parcellisation. When they do not — as in the flat agricultural plots of the same Swiss villages — private ownership is efficient. The *same individuals* used both regimes simultaneously for multiple centuries, choosing the appropriate form for each resource type.

## The 7 Participant Conditions for Successful Communal Governance
Ostrom (2000) synthesises empirical research into seven participant-level conditions that predict whether communal governance will succeed:
1. Accurate, low-cost information about resource condition and expected flows
2. Shared understanding of the benefits and risks of status quo vs. change
3. Generalised norms of reciprocity and trust as initial social capital
4. Relatively stable group membership
5. Long time horizons — participants expect to live there (and their offspring to inherit), reducing discount rates
6. Collective-choice rules between extremes of unanimity and bare majority
7. Relatively accurate and low-cost monitoring and sanctioning arrangements

## The Nationalization → Open Access Pathway
Ostrom (2000) documents a critical policy failure pattern in developing countries: as concern for natural resource protection mounted in the 1960s, many governments nationalised all land and water not recorded as private property. This eliminated the legal standing of local communal governance institutions — but national governments lacked the resources to monitor and enforce their new formal ownership. The result: resources that had been under *de facto* common property (governed by local users) became *de jure* government property but *de facto* open access. Evidence from Thailand, Niger, Nepal, and India all show that state takeover of locally governed forests produced worse outcomes than the communal regimes they replaced.

## ### ⚠️ Contradiction with Demsetz / North
Demsetz (1967) and North (1990) treat common property as characterised by under-investment and over-exploitation, predicting that rising resource values should trigger a shift toward private rights. Ostrom's framework shows this is only true of *open access*, not of *governed communal property*. Communal proprietorship can outperform private property precisely when the resource has the attributes Netting identifies — and the policy prescription (privatise to solve the commons) is wrong when it destroys working communal governance institutions. See [[Communal Rights]], [[Open Access (Property Right)]].

## PhD Application
India's "wastelands" and forest commons were, in many cases, governed communal property systems before colonial-era land settlement. The colonial state's failure to record and recognise these systems as property converted them to open access — the opposite of the intended rationalisation. DILRMP continues this pattern by digitising parcel-level records without addressing the question of which users hold which rights over communal lands. See [[DILRMP]], [[Bhu-Aadhaar]], [[Land Tenure Systems (India)]].

# Deeds vs Title Systems

The choice between a **Deeds Registration** and a **Title Registration** (Torrens) system is a fundamental decision in land administration, impacting cost-effectiveness, risk distribution, and legal certainty (Deininger & Feder, 2009).

## **1. Deeds Registration System**
A public repository where documents for evidence of land transactions are lodged and archived.
- **Mechanism**: Recording provides public notice and assigns priority (registered deeds take priority over unregistered ones).
- **Legal Weight**: Does **not** guarantee the legal validity of the underlying transaction.
- **Risk**: The residual risk of verifying ownership remains with the transacting parties (due diligence).
- **Cost**: Generally cheaper to set up and operate.

## **2. Title Registration System (Torrens)**
The register itself serves as the primary evidence of ownership. It is built on three core principles:
1. **Mirror Principle**: The registry is an exact reflection of reality.
2. **Curtain Principle**: Interested parties do not need to search the chain of historical documents; they can rely on the registry as definitive.
3. **Assurance Principle**: The state indemnifies damages incurred due to errors in the registry (Assurance Fund).

## **Comparative Analysis**

| Feature | Deeds System | Title System |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **State Guarantee** | None. | Government indemnifies against errors. |
| **Transaction Cost** | Low for state, high for parties (due diligence). | High for state, low for parties. |
| **Optimal Context** | Low land values; weak state capacity. | High land values; robust state capacity. |
| **Robustness** | High (e.g., Netherlands, South Africa). | Low if regulatory framework is weak. |

## **Key Insights from Deininger & Feder (2009)**
- **Subsidized Failure**: Shifting from deeds to title in developing countries often fails (e.g., Sri Lanka) because it requires complex legal/institutional frameworks that aren't sustained.
- **Technological Convergence**: With computerization and parcel-based indexing, the gap between the two systems is narrowing.
- **Recommendation**: Title systems should only be pursued if land values are high enough to justify the state's increased operational costs.

# Comparative Institutional Analysis

## Description
A research method that compares different institutional arrangements (e.g., communal, private, state ownership) to evaluate their relative efficiency in resource allocation and externality management. 

Instead of looking at a "market failure" against a theoretical optimum, this method compares **attainable** institutional alternatives, taking into account their unique transaction costs.

## Strengths/Weaknesses
- **Strengths**: 
    - **Real-world focus**: Evaluates how institutions actually function, rather than assuming zero transaction costs.
    - **Dynamic perspective**: Can explain the *emergence* of new rules based on shifting resource values.
- **Weaknesses**:
    - **Functionalist bias**: May assume that the existing arrangement is the "most efficient" simply because it survived.
    - **Data-intensive**: Requires historical or anthropological data (e.g., Demsetz's hunt records) to establish the link between resource value and rights change.

## PhD Relevance
This is the primary method for comparing **Pre-Colonial** vs. **Colonial** land rights in your thesis. It allows you to model whether colonial "enclosure" were driven by economic efficiency (Demsetzian) or by political extraction.

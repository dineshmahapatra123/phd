# Low-cost Land Certification

**Low-cost Land Certification** is a pragmatic land administration methodology that prioritizes rapid, participatory, and affordable tenure security over high-precision, expensive titling (Deininger & Feder, 2009).

## **Key Characteristics**
1. **Participatory Adjudication**: Involves high levels of community participation in identifying boundaries.
2. **Simplified Surveying**: Uses field measurements or textual descriptions rather than high-precision GPS/Graphical records in the initial phase.
3. **Speed and Scale**: Designed to cover large populations quickly (millions of households).
4. **Upgradability**: Information is maintained in a coherent format that allows for future upgrading to more formal systems as land values rise.

## **Case Study: Ethiopia (2003–2005)**
- **Scale**: Registered 20 million plots (6 million households) in less than three years.
- **Cost**: Less than **US$1 per parcel**.
- **Impact**: 
    - 80% of respondents perceived increased security (especially regarding compensation for state land takings).
    - Significant reduction in land conflicts.
    - Improved bargaining power for women (using photographs on certificates).
- **Sustainability**: A modest fee of $0.65 is sufficient to finance a computerized update system.

## **Comparison with Traditional Titling**
| Feature | Traditional Titling | Low-cost Certification |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cost per Parcel** | US$20–$100 | < US$1 |
| **Precision** | High (Centimeter-level) | Low (Metes and bounds) |
| **Pace** | Slow (Decades for national coverage) | Rapid (Years for national coverage) |
| **Accessibility** | Often biased toward elites | Pro-poor and inclusive |

## **Prerequisites**
- Clear legal recognition of the certificates.
- Mechanisms for updating the registry (to avoid "deformalization").
- Community-based conflict resolution mechanisms.

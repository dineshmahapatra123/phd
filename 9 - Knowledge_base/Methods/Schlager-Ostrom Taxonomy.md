# Method: Schlager-Ostrom Taxonomy

## Description
A matrix developed by Schlager and Ostrom (1992) and applied in Ostrom (2000) for analyzing property-rights regimes by mapping **five specific rights** across **five classes of rights holders**. The core insight: "property rights" is not a binary (owned vs. unowned) but a structured bundle, and different institutional arrangements assign different slices of that bundle to different actors.

## The Five Rights
1. **Access**: The right to enter a defined physical area.
2. **Withdrawal**: The right to extract resource units (fish, water, timber).
3. **Management**: The right to regulate use patterns and transform the resource.
4. **Exclusion**: The right to determine who has access and withdrawal rights.
5. **Alienation**: The right to sell or lease management and exclusion rights to others.

## The Five Classes of Rights Holders

| Class | Access | Withdrawal | Management | Exclusion | Alienation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Authorized Entrant | ✓ | | | | |
| Authorized User | ✓ | ✓ | | | |
| Claimant | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | | |
| Proprietor | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | |
| Owner | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |

Rights are cumulative: each level adds to the previous. A proprietor has all rights except alienation; an owner holds the complete bundle.

## Empirical Cases (Ostrom 2000)

### Authorized Entrants
Visitors to national parks or seasonal licence-holders who may enter and observe but cannot extract — the most attenuated position. Any extraction converts them to unauthorized users.

### Authorized Users (Withdrawal only)
Forest foragers who may collect deadwood or non-timber products from state forests under licensed permits but have no say in management. Standard position of tribal communities under the Indian Forest Act before the Forest Rights Act 2006.

### Claimants (Management without Exclusion)
Ostrom cites **Jambudwip, India fishers**: fishing communities that had developed rules governing the positioning of boats and gear (management rights) to reduce conflict, but who could not prevent outsiders from entering the fishery (no exclusion right). The result is a partial governance institution — the group can order itself internally but cannot defend the resource perimeter. Claimants face chronic pressure because their investment in management benefits entrants they cannot exclude.

### Proprietors (Exclusion without Alienation)
**Swiss Alpine communities (Netting)**: mountain pasture communities that held full governance rights — managing access, setting stocking rates, maintaining infrastructure, excluding outsiders — but held these rights collectively and could not sell or lease the exclusion right to outside parties. The community as a whole was the proprietor; no individual could convert the collective exclusion right into a private asset.

The proprietor position is the critical intermediate category. Most long-lived communal governance institutions studied by Ostrom operate at this level: they have enough rights to invest, manage, and exclude, but not the alienation right that would allow conversion to private individual property.

### Owners (Full Bundle)
Swiss Alpine villages with share-based systems where individual families could buy and sell shares in the communal resource — transferring management and exclusion rights through market mechanisms while maintaining communal governance of the underlying pasture. Rare; requires a mature institutional structure to prevent share concentration from destroying the communal governance.

## The ITQ Privatization Case
Individual Transferable Quotas (ITQs) — widely promoted as the privatization solution to fishery over-exploitation — are revealed by the taxonomy as a *withdrawal-right* allocation only. ITQs give holders the right to extract a defined quantity (withdrawal) but do not assign management, exclusion, or alienation rights over the fishery system itself. Calling ITQ schemes "privatization" conflates the withdrawal right with the full bundle. An ITQ regime without exclusion rights still faces entry problems; without management rights, the community cannot adapt rules to ecological change.

## The Proprietorship Productivity Finding
Studies in Ghana, Kenya, and Rwanda (Place & Hazell 1993, cited in Ostrom 2000) found that farmers at the **proprietor** level of the taxonomy — holding access, withdrawal, management, and exclusion rights but not alienation — achieved land investment and productivity outcomes statistically indistinguishable from full owners. The binding constraint for investment is *exclusion security*, not *alienation rights*. This finding challenges the standard argument that communal governance must be converted to freehold to incentivize investment.

## Strengths/Weaknesses
- **Strengths**: Precise identification of who holds which authority; dissolves the private/common binary; enables cross-regime comparison at the rights level; the five-class structure maps onto real institutional variation rather than forcing a binary.
- **Weaknesses**: Assumes relative clarity in institutional rules; does not account for layered or hybrid regimes where formal and customary rights coexist and conflict; does not capture temporal dynamism (rights that vary seasonally or are renegotiated annually).

## PhD Relevance
Primary analytical tool for deconstructing India's land tenure complexity. The Record of Rights assigns cultivating rights (access + withdrawal) to occupancy tenants but reserve management and exclusion rights for the state — claimant-level rights at best. Forest communities under the Forest Rights Act gain access and withdrawal over minor forest produce but rarely achieve proprietor-level exclusion. DILRMP's digitization addresses the record of withdrawal rights without touching the distribution of management and exclusion rights — a taxonomy-level diagnosis of why administrative modernisation does not resolve tenure insecurity.

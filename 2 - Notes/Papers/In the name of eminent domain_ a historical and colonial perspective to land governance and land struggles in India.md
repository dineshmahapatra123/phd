# Claude Primer
**"In the Name of Eminent Domain": A Comprehensive Review**

**Ghosh & Deodhar | IIMA Working Paper WP No. 2025-06-02 | June 2025**

---

## Overview and Central Argument

This is a historically sweeping paper that traces the evolution of land governance in India from ancient philosophical traditions through the colonial Land Acquisition Act (LAA) of 1894. The paper's central argument is that the LAA 1894 was not an isolated legislative event but the culmination of centuries of ideological, structural, and economic forces — rooted in the European doctrine of **eminent domain** — that progressively dismantled India's communal, decentralized land systems and replaced them with an extractive, commodified regime designed primarily to serve British imperial interests. The paper also establishes that this extraction was fundamentally connected to Britain's global trade strategy (the opium-tea-silver circuit) and the indentured labour system — making land governance in colonial India a node in a global capitalist network.

---

## Part I: Land Struggles — The Backdrop

The paper opens with a striking historical claim: the world's first recorded organized non-violent struggle against forced displacement caused by an irrigation project took place in 1921, under the leadership of Pandurang Bapat (titled _Senapati_), who championed the rights of tenant farmers against the Mulshi Dam near present-day Pune. Mahatma Gandhi opposed the use of the LAA 1894 for this compulsory acquisition, calling it "satanic" and contrary to the spirit of Indian civilization.

But this non-violent protest was preceded by a long chain of armed uprisings:

### Tilak Manjhi (1750–1785)

Tilak Manjhi is identified as the first known tribal leader to fearlessly oppose British attempts to control tribal lands in the Rajmahal region of present-day Bihar and Jharkhand. Born in 1750, he organized Adivasis into an armed force using guerrilla tactics, attacked British commissioner Augustus Cleveland in 1784 with a poisoned arrow, and was eventually captured and executed in 1785 — tied to a horse's tail, dragged, and hanged from a Banyan tree in Bhagalpur. He is positioned as India's first armed rebel against the British.

### The Santhal Rebellion / Hul (1855)

The Santhal rebellion came as a direct response to the complete disruption of traditional communal land ownership due to the Permanent Settlement of 1793. Under this, Zamindars were empowered to collect revenues and impose arbitrary taxes; moneylenders charged exorbitant interest, pushing farmers into debt and bonded labour. The Murmu brothers — Sido, Kanhu, Chand, and Bhairab — formed an army of thousands and launched unprecedented attacks on British establishments and Zamindar homes. Crucially, this rebellion, though quelled by brute force, led to the enactment of **The Santhal Pargana Tenancy Act, 1876** — one of India's first tenancy protection laws, making it illegal for Santhals to transfer land to non-Santhals. Its legacy continues today.

### Birsa Munda (1895–1900)

Birsa Munda led an uprising against British land alienation policies in the Chotanagpur region of Jharkhand. Under the Munda system of _Khuntkatti_ (ownership vested in the clan that first cultivated the land), land was communally held. When the British introduced Zamindari, this system was broken and Mundas became tenants or landless labourers, forced into wageless labour (_Beth Begari_). Forests were declared state property, prohibiting tribals from collecting produce, practicing shifting cultivation, or grazing cattle. His capture and death in Ranchi jail led to the **Chotanagpur Tenancy Act of 1908**.

### Khasi Rebellion (1829–1833)

The Khasi Rebellion was led by U Tirot Sing Syiem of Nongkhlaw against British plans to construct a road through the autonomous Khasi Hills to connect the Brahmaputra Valley with Sylhet. This required forceful land acquisition that threatened the Khasi's traditional land ownership. On 4 April 1829, Tirot Singh attacked the British garrison, killing several soldiers including two officers. He was eventually captured and imprisoned in Dacca, where he died in 1834, his last words being: "Better die a commoner in prison than a slave king on the throne."

### The Legislative Response to Resistance

The paper makes an important structural point: since these uprisings made it very difficult for the British to acquire land, they institutionalized acquisition through the Land Acquisition Act (LAA) of 1894 — a legislative embodiment of the colonial government's continuing exercise of coercion essential for their expansion and control. The LAA thus emerged not despite resistance but _because_ of it.

## Part II: The Doctrine of Eminent Domain — Its Origins

The roots of the coercive LAA 1894 lay in the principle of _Eminent Domain_, which originated in Europe during the Dark Ages. The term derives from the Latin phrase meaning "supreme lordship," reflecting the belief that ultimate ownership of land rested with God. Medieval kings, claiming to be God's representatives on earth, leveraged this belief to assert absolute control over land. The codification of Eminent Domain began with the Magna Carta in the 13th century. Later, the Papal Bull issued by Pope Alexander VI in 1493 legitimized the Spanish conquest of lands in Latin America. Similar principles guided Portuguese and other European colonial conquests in Africa and Asia.

The paper grounds this in comparative colonial studies: Frankema (2010) shows colonial land redistribution caused persistent asset inequality; Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson (2001/2012) argue that "extractive institutions" were established wherever European mortality was high; Beckert (2014) describes how the British forcibly reorganized land through "war capitalism"; Banerjee and Iyer (2005) demonstrate that the Zamindari system created regional disparities in agricultural investment and poverty that persist today.

## Part III: Land Systems in Ancient India

### Philosophical Foundations

This section is particularly rich and original, drawing on Sanskrit texts and ancient treatises. The paper makes a counterintuitive opening claim: the Sanskrit word for land is _Vasundhara_ — "one that holds wealth" — yet land is conspicuously absent from the eight forms of wealth (_Ashta Lakshmi_) mentioned in ancient Indian treatises. This suggests land was considered something that _created_ wealth but was not wealth in itself.

Kautilya (circa 4th century BCE) in the _Arthashastra_ defined economics as: "The source of the livelihood of men is wealth, in other words, the earth inhabited by men." Narada (circa 2nd century CE) in his _Dharmashastra_ stated: "A householder's living space and his field are considered as the two fundamentals of his existence. Therefore, let not the king upset either of them."

Crucially, Jaimini, founder of Purva Mimamsa (circa 4th century BCE), clearly stated that the right of property is not vested in the king and that the king has no right to give it away as a fee. Kings had to _buy_ lands from private owners, even for donations or public works. This is evidenced by the Nasik cave inscriptions: Gautamiputra Satakarni, the Satavahana emperor (circa 1st century CE), wanted to grant land to a Buddha Bhikkhu Sangha but first had to purchase it from the original possessor for 4,000 _karshapanas_.

### Four-fold Classification of Property

Ancient India recognized four categories of land:

1. **Rajya** (State Property): Land and resources directly owned or controlled by the king — including agricultural land, forests, mines, and trade routes. Kautilya recommended the king maintain control over uncultivated lands, pastures, and forests. However, the king's role was more that of a trustee than an absolute owner.
2. **Svātva** (Private Property): Land owned by individuals or families, heritable, giftable, or purchasable. The state protected private property rights in exchange for taxes.
3. **Samudāya** (Common Property): Resources such as pastures, grazing lands, water bodies, forests, and collectively managed agricultural land. Access was based on social norms and customs; communities managed these resources collectively, often under village councils (_sabhas_).
4. **Devasvām** (Temple Property): Managed by the priestly class through endowments called _agrahāras_ or _brahmadeyas_. Temples held large tracts often leased to peasants.

### Detailed Land Sub-classifications under Kautilya

The paper enumerates specific land types with great granularity:

- **Taxable lands** (_Karadas_) vs. **tax-exempt** (_Bhaga Parihara_) — each transferable only within its category
- **Apratibaddha Bhumi** (inalienable arable land) — if neglected, others could cultivate it for up to five years; original owner compensated upon return
- **Vivita/Pasuvana** (pasture lands) — protected from encroachment for livestock
- **Setu** (common pool resources like tanks, reservoirs, embankments) — strictly regulated for fair access
- **Anirjita Bhumi** (waste land) — for new settlements or border defense
- **Vana** (forests) — further classified into _dravyavana_ (productive), _hastivana_ (elephant/royal), and _mrigavana_ (for ascetics)

### Tenure and Security

Arable land received the highest security. Lifetime tenure was awarded only for arable land to taxpayers, but ownership was secure only as long as owners cultivated and maintained the land. If land was not cultivated or was neglected, the king could revoke individual rights. If someone prepared non-arable land for cultivation, it could not be taken away. However, neglect over 10–20 years could result in forfeiture, with exceptions for minors, the elderly, and those affected by calamities.

On tenancy: Agricultural tenancy was prevalent even in ancient India. Many farmers were tenants or sharecroppers paying a portion of produce as rent. The Arthashastra states that between one-fifth to one-half of produce would be shared with the sharecropper depending on input share. Even tenants who did not own land had clear rights — if they had cultivated a piece of land for a long period, they could not be arbitrarily evicted, a practice referred to as "prescriptive possession."

### Revenue and Taxation

Different terms were used for taxes: _bali_ was a voluntary offering; _bhaga_ was a compulsory share of produce (often one-sixth). The _Manusmriti_ recommended the king take no more than one-sixth of the harvest. Kautilya, however, permitted up to one-fourth or one-third during economic strain or war.

### Inheritance and Gender

Inheritance laws favoured male descendants. As per the Dharmaśāstras, land was inherited by male descendants with the eldest son receiving the largest share. Daughters were generally excluded, though widows had rights to manage land in the absence of a male heir. Later, the Mitakshara school (dominant in most of India) recognized joint family property, while the Dayabhaga school (Bengal) allowed individual ownership upon the death of the family head.

## Part IV: Medieval India — Hindu Kingdoms and Mughal Rule

### Hindu Kingdoms (7th–12th Century CE)

The _samanta_ system became prominent under medieval Hindu kings — land was granted to vassals in exchange for military service and increasingly divided among powerful landlords and regional chieftains.

The paper highlights the **Uttiramerur inscriptions** (Chola period, circa 10th century CE) to illustrate decentralized governance: village assemblies (_sabhas_) exercised substantial autonomy; land granted to Brahmins and temples was exempt from _kadamai_ (land tax) and _vetti_ (forced labor). The paper argues, drawing on Sanyal (2008, 2012, 2016), that unlike European feudalism, India had an absence of serfdom and land rights were relatively fluid. Chola inscriptions reflect a participatory model where land taxes funded public works like irrigation and massive temple-building projects.

### Mughal Rule (1526–1857 CE)

Under Mughals, land governance was organized around revenue extraction and centralized control rather than feudal ownership. The Mughal state did not own land in the Western sense — they viewed land revenue as a "remuneration of sovereignty" in return for state protection. They divided land into _jagirs_ (assigned), _khalisas_ (crown), and _zamindaris_ (landlord-controlled).

The Mughal nobility (_mansabdars_) were frequently transferred to prevent them from consolidating local power, making them highly dependent on the emperor. During the later Mughal period, kings tried to convert all land into _khalisa_ (crown land) to reduce dependence on zamindars and jagirdars, but this was never fully successful. Jagirdars, being temporarily assigned, had no interest in land improvement, so rural development was ignored in favour of revenue collection.

### Raja Todar Mal's Revenue Reforms

Raja Todar Mal, a minister under Akbar, systematized land revenue. He instituted the _Zabt_ or _Bandobast_ system with systematic land surveys, which evolved into the _Dahsala_ system by the end of the 16th century. The basic unit of length was the _Ilahi Gaj_ (roughly 29–32 inches), with 3,600 square Ilahi Gaj equalling a _Bigha_, which became the standard area measurement. By 1602, the imperial map had expanded to 15 Subahs, 187 Sarkars, and 3,367 Mahals.

Land was categorized into four quality types: _Polaj_ (most productive, cultivated annually, taxed most heavily); _Parauti_ (farmed in two-year cycles, left fallow for a year); _Chachar_ (fallow for three to four years, taxed in a five-year cycle); and _Banjar_ (fallow for more than five years).

Todar Mal used the average produce of the past ten years as a benchmark and taxed one-third of this average. A dual-document system was introduced: _Patta_ (a deed specifying size, classification, and revenue obligation) and _Kabuliyat_ (a receipt with the farmer's signature making the agreement binding). This is notably the first formal land contract system in Indian history.

## Part V: Colonial British Land Governance

### The Three Pillars of British Land Tenure

After the Battle of Plassey (1757), Battle of Buxar, and the Treaty of Allahabad (1765) — which granted revenue collection rights of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa to the EIC — the British restructured Indian land into three fundamental tenure systems:

**1. Permanent Settlement (1793) — Zamindari System** Lord Cornwallis made zamindars hereditary owners of large estates on the condition they pay fixed annual revenues to the British. Tenant rights were eliminated and peasants paid rent to zamindars or faced eviction. Failure to pay revenues meant ownership was revoked and land auctioned — a fear that pushed zamindars to collect revenue aggressively, leading to tremendous exploitation of peasants.

**2. Ryotwari System (1792 onwards)** Introduced by Captain Alexander Read and Sir Thomas Munro in Madras, Bombay, Berar, Assam, and parts of Coorg, individual cultivators (_ryots_) were made landowners and paid revenue directly to the British based on productivity assessments. While seemingly more equitable, small landowners were heavily taxed, often driving them into indebtedness with moneylenders.

**3. Mahalwari System (1822–1833)** Introduced in North-Western Frontier, British Punjab, and Central India by Holt Mackenzie, revenue was not collected from individuals but from villages or estates (_mahal_). Village headmen (_lambardars_) collected and paid revenue to the British based on periodic productivity assessments, giving local village elites power while keeping peasants at risk of high taxes.

### Scientific Land Measurement and Cadastral Surveys

In 1802, William Lambton initiated the Great Trigonometrical Survey (GTS) using triangulation and a massive half-ton theodolite to measure angles between distant points. The first baseline stretched 7.5 miles near Madras, measured using a 100-foot foldable chain corrected for temperature changes. This eventually created the "Great Arc of the Meridian" — a continuous chain of measured triangles from Cape Comorin to the Himalayas. Heights of 79 Himalayan peaks, including Mount Everest, were measured using this innovation. The survey took 70 years and is considered one of the greatest scientific achievements in history.

Cadastral surveys (documents demarcating land boundaries with dimensions, location, ownership, and tenure) were introduced in the late 18th century. They led to clear mapping of administrative units like _parganas_ and creation of village-level (_maujawar_) records. Each landholding was assigned as a _parcel_ or _khasra_ with the owner's name and boundaries. A _khata_ was a ledger containing owner-wise information of all landholdings and their types. _Tehsildars_, _Kanungos_, and _Patwaris_ managed this system.

## Part VI: The LAA 1894 — Origins and Implications

### Pre-1894 Legislation

The paper traces a legislative genealogy:

- **Bengal Regulation I (1824)**: First formal law enabling acquisition for public works (roads, canals, military) — highly coercive, no compensation guarantees
- **Act VI of 1857**: Used by EIC to seize land for railways, plantations, and government buildings — some compensation but local rights not protected
- **Land Acquisition Act, 1870**: First to allow civil court involvement in compensation disputes — but still defective

### The 1894 Act

The LAA 1894 brought all previous laws together into a common framework for acquiring land for "public purposes." Compensation was provided but was often meagre. Zamindars negotiated for higher compensation, but small farmers and _ryots_ were generally given inadequate amounts. The law did not recognize customary landholding practices of rural and indigenous communities, making their lands vulnerable to seizure.

The 1894 Act was not just an institutional mechanism but a tool to sustain British colonial rule and extract maximum economic surplus. Through it, the British extended land for tea, coffee, and indigo plantations in Bengal, Assam, and South India, and acquired land for railways and ports to enable large-scale, low-cost exports of raw materials (cotton, jute, minerals) to mainland Britain — feeding the Industrial Revolution.

## Part VII: Comparative Summary — Three Eras of Land Governance

The paper provides a sharp comparative table across three eras, which can be summarized as follows:

|Feature|Ancient/Medieval Hindu|Mughal Empire|British Colonial|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Ownership**|Mix of private, state, temple, and common|Continued earlier patterns; land as state revenue source|Permanent private rights to Zamindars via Permanent Settlement (1793)|
|**Revenue**|By local chieftains or village assemblies; ~1/6th of produce|By Jagirdars, Zamindars, and officials|Direct taxation via zamindari, ryotwari, mahalwari|
|**Peasant Rights**|Hereditary cultivation rights, owed produce/taxes|Taxed but retained hereditary rights|Became landless tenants or indebted; could be evicted|
|**Tax Flexibility**|Adjusted based on harvest and monsoons|Flexible, crop-dependent|Fixed and rigid; caused rural distress|
|**Transferability**|Mostly hereditary, not freely sellable|Not freely transferable|Freely bought, sold, mortgaged|

The paper's key historiographical argument: it was the British who brought a tectonic shift by legalizing private landownership of non-cultivators through the Permanent Settlement of zamindars — an extractive institution previously absent in Indian governance. Unlike Mughal zamindars who merely acted as intermediaries, British zamindars now held absolute ownership rights. Failure to pay taxes meant confiscation and auction of land. This led to the emergence of absentee landlords with little interest in agricultural development.

## Part VIII: The Economics of Colonial Land Grabs — The Opium-Tea-Labour Circuit

This final section is the most analytically ambitious part of the paper, situating Indian land governance within Britain's global economic strategy.

### The China Trade Problem

The British East India Company was a commercial venture that transformed into a sovereign power. Its land policies in India had more to do with correcting Britain's trade imbalance with China than with India itself. There was high demand for Chinese tea, silk, and porcelain. China only accepted silver, which the British obtained from Latin America. As Britain's silver reserves dwindled due to Spanish control and depleting mines, the EIC turned to cultivating opium in large swathes of North and Central India — forcing China to buy opium to balance trade.

When China resisted the opium trade due to its devastating social impact, the British forced trade through the **Opium Wars** — the First Opium War (1839–42) and the Second (1856–60). Opium became the British Empire's most lucrative economic activity. By the mid-19th century, revenues from opium ranked second only to land revenues in British India's budget.

Most land in Northern Bihar and Eastern Uttar Pradesh was designated by the British Opium Department for exclusive poppy cultivation; growing food crops was banned.

### The Tea Strategy

To reduce dependence on Chinese tea, Britain sent covert botanical missions to steal Chinese tea plants and cultivation knowledge. When Robert Bruce discovered wild tea species in Assam, it became a game changer. Combined with smuggled Chinese varieties, large parts of Northern Bengal and Assam were converted into tea plantations. This freed Britain from Chinese dependence — making it both a distributor and a producer of tea, improving its balance of payments and stopping its silver drain.

### The Indentured Labour System

Opium and tea required effective land administration. Organized land systems were used to enforce poppy cultivation contracts on farmers with clear land titles. Farmers who failed to supply adequate quantities or grew food crops had their land seized. They either became landless labourers or were arrested and sent as _coolies_ or indentured labour to British plantations in Mauritius, South Africa, and the Caribbean. Between 1834 and 1920, approximately 1.3 million indentured labourers were transported to various British colonies, of whom 85% were from India.

Although there was legal colour to indentures, these contracts were extremely coercive with only minimal rights. India became the biggest catchment of indentured labour — land-evicted peasants ensuring an unbroken, low-cost supply chain. This system was a "legal" substitute for chattel slavery, which had been abolished in the first half of the 19th century.

### The Closing Argument

The paper ends with a powerful synthesis: as scholars like Dadabhai Naoroji, R.C. Dutt, and Amitav Ghosh have shown, the British imperial project in India turned into a tightly integrated system of economic extraction where land governance, agricultural control, and global trade strategy converged to serve the singular purpose of sustaining Britain's global dominance — all in the name of eminent domain. By investing in land assessments and assigning absolute rights to intermediaries like zamindars, the British created a cost-minimizing concoction for the two most vital economic inputs — land and labour.

## Critical Assessment — Strengths and Gaps

**Strengths:**

- Unusually long historical arc (4th century BCE to 1894) gives genuine comparative depth
- Strong use of primary Sanskrit texts (Arthashastra, Manusmriti, Dharmashastra, Upanishads, cave inscriptions) to establish pre-colonial land philosophy
- The opium-tea-silver-indentured labour nexus is the most analytically original contribution — connecting Indian land governance to global capitalism
- The resistance movements section humanizes the otherwise institutional narrative
- The comparative table across three eras is pedagogically excellent

**Gaps / Limitations (worth noting for your research):**

- The paper is explicitly a _prequel_ — it ends at 1894 and does not deal with post-independence land reforms, LARR 2013, or contemporary issues like Special Economic Zones
- Odisha and tribal regions (your focus) get limited specific treatment — Chotanagpur and Santhal Pargana are mentioned but not Odisha's distinct land history (the Orissa Estates Abolition Act, OPDR, etc.)
- The paper relies significantly on Sanyal (2008/2012/2016) for the "decentralized, non-feudal" argument about Hindu kingdoms — a perspective some historians contest
- Gender is touched upon only in the inheritance section and not integrated into the broader argument
- The authors acknowledge using OpenAI tools for proofreading

## Relevance to Your Research (Land Rights, Odisha, DILRMP)

For your PhD work, this paper is most useful as:

1. **Historical grounding** for how cadastral systems, khasra-khata records, and the patwari-kanungo-tehsildar hierarchy emerged as _colonial instruments_ — directly relevant to your interest in land records modernisation (DILRMP, Bhulekh, BhuNaksha)
2. **The critique of "conclusive titling"** — the paper implicitly shows that British permanent settlement created "conclusive" private titles that dispossessed tribals; this historical irony is important when evaluating whether today's DILRMP push for conclusive titling repeats or corrects colonial patterns
3. **Tribal land alienation genealogy** — Khuntkatti, Santhal Pargana Tenancy Act, Chotanagpur Tenancy Act all appear here, providing the deep historical roots of the protective legislation that PESA and the Forest Rights Act later built upon
4. **The doctrine of eminent domain** remains the legal basis for land acquisition in India today, making this historical critique directly relevant to contemporary agrarian distress debates

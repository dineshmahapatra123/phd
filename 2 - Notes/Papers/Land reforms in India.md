# Claude Primer

A Comprehensive Chapter-by-Chapter Review  
Land Reforms in India: A Survey of Policy, Legislation and Implementation*  
P. S. Appu (Vikas Publishing House, New Delhi, 1996)

---

## Preliminary: Author, Purpose, and Method

P. S. Appu's *Land Reforms in India* is, in the Indian literature on agrarian change, very nearly without parallel. There exist prolific state-wise studies, a large body of journal scholarship, and competent treatments of the subject as part of wider agrarian histories (Gunnar Myrdal's *Asian Drama* being the most ambitious). ==What has been absent, as Appu himself notes in the Preface, is "a comprehensive all India survey covering the three programmes" — the abolition of intermediary interests, tenancy reform, and ceilings on agricultural holdings.== This book fills that void.

What makes the work exceptional is not merely its scope but its authorial positioning. Appu served as Land Reforms Commissioner under the Government of India for approximately five years from 1970 to 1975, and subsequently as Additional Secretary in the Ministry of Agriculture, and later as Finance Secretary and Chief Secretary of Bihar. He drafted the Planning Commission's Task Force Report on Agrarian Relations in 1972–73 and was a direct participant in the Chief Ministers' Conferences that redesigned ceiling legislation. The book is thus simultaneously a scholarly survey and a memoir of state power — or, more precisely, of the systematic failure of state power in the face of landed interest.

The book was facilitated by a Jawaharlal Nehru Fellowship, which gave Appu both financial support and access to official materials otherwise unavailable to non-officials. The result is a work of unusual density and authority. In the Preface, Appu writes with characteristic plainness: "My aim in writing this book has been to fill that void in the literature on Indian land reform." He has done so comprehensively.

## Introduction: The Global Setting and the Indian Problem

The Introduction serves both as a comparative manifesto and a statement of method. Appu opens by situating Indian land reform within the broadest possible historical frame — from imperial China's attempts in the seventeenth century BC, through Solon's Athenian experiments in the sixth century BC, to the Gracchi brothers' failed redistributions in Rome two centuries later. His point is not antiquarianism but pattern recognition: "All through history, measures of land reform have been controversial, often accompanied by political conflict and violence. Because of the stiff opposition by vested interests most attempts at reform could not produce the expected results."

The successful programmes, Appu argues, have all involved revolutionary or "specially compelling" circumstances: China's land redistribution of 1949–52, the MacArthur-administered Japanese reforms of 1946–48 (whose "principal objective was to destroy the base of Japanese feudalism"), and the Taiwanese reforms instituted after the Kuomintang's retreat from the mainland. In each case, the political conditions that normally protect landed property were temporarily suspended. "Measures of land reforms introduced in India after independence," Appu observes flatly, "belong to this category" of reforms undertaken in normal circumstances — and those, by historical reckoning, "generally failed to produce the expected results."

The Introduction then defines its terms. The Encyclopaedia Britannica definition of land reform — covering redistribution, tenure security, rent regulation, and agricultural credit — is cited, while Appu distinguishes it from the broader concept of "Agrarian Reforms," which would additionally encompass cooperatives, extension, and rural services. The Indian Five Year Plans defined the specific measures under "Land Reforms" as the Abolition of Intermediary Interests, Tenancy Reform, Ceilings on Agricultural Holdings, and the Consolidation of Holdings. This study covers the first three.

The description of the agrarian structure at independence is among the sharpest writing in the book. Appu enumerates the pathologies with clinical precision: parasitic intermediaries between State and cultivator; extreme inequality in land ownership; concentration of land in the hands of "indolent land owners" belonging to upper castes who shunned physical labour; insecure tenancies "on extortionate terms"; and a "preponderance of miniscule uneconomic holdings." The correspondence between the Hindu caste hierarchy and the agrarian structure — upper castes as landowners, middle castes as cultivators, Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes as agricultural labourers — is identified as a "unique feature" of the Indian agrarian structure.

The constitutional framework is clarified with precision. Under Entry 18 of the State List, land is exclusively a State subject. The Centre's only constitutional basis for intervening is Entry 20 of the Concurrent List (Social and Economic Planning) — the nominal basis for the Planning Commission's advisory role. Appu's judgment is pointed: "Despite the lack of Constitutional powers, the Government of India, if it so chose, could have played a more active role in respect of Land Reforms because for several years after independence the same political party was in power both at the Centre and in the States, and the Central leadership enjoyed immense prestige and authority." That it chose not to use this authority becomes the central argument of the entire study.

## Chapter 1: Tenurial Conditions Before Independence

This is the most historically elaborate chapter and among the most important. Its argument is that the pre-independence tenurial structure was not an organic Indian formation but largely an artifact of British colonial intervention — one that deliberately destroyed a long-standing system of cultivating rights for purposes of imperial political economy. Understanding this is prerequisite to understanding why post-independence reforms were so urgent, and why they ultimately disappointed.

### Ancient and Medieval Tenures

Drawing on Kautilya's *Arthashastra*, the *Manusmriti*, the Buddhist *Jatakas*, and Baden Powell's monumental *The Land Systems of British India*, Appu establishes that the dominant tenure in ancient India was essentially raiyatwari. The king was not the owner of the soil; he was entitled only to a share of the produce — customarily one-sixth — as consideration for his protection of subjects. "According to Manu, the first clearer has the right to the land, just as the hunter who first wounded the deer in the chase." The parasitic intermediary had not yet appeared.

The Mughal period broadly preserved the cultivator's rights. Akbar's revenue minister Todar Mai introduced a sophisticated ryotwari system based on measurement, soil classification, and cash settlement — described by one historian as "a decided improvement on the arbitrary methods of the preceding years." However, Akbar's successors abandoned his methods, resorting to jagirdars and assignees who extracted as much as they could. Under Aurangzeb, the standard state demand reached half the produce. Bernier, writing at the close of Shahjahan's reign, recorded that "land was tilled by only compulsion and irrigation works were badly neglected," culminating in Aurangzeb's instruction "to his assessors to flog the peasantry into cultivating the fields."

The advent of revenue farming during the empire's decline set the stage for the creation of the Zamindar class. As Baden Powell describes a process Appu quotes at length, what began as carefully contracted arrangements became uncontrolled exploitation: "No one in fact knew (or cared) what was actually wrung out of the villagers, so long as the contract sum was paid into the treasury."

### The Permanent Settlement and its Consequences

The British chapter of this history is Appu's central focus. The East India Company — which he notes began its imperial career as "a petty revenue farmer" in Bengal, acquiring Zamindari rights over three villages in 1698 for Rs. 1,300 — obtained the Dewani of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa from Emperor Shah Alam in 1765 and proceeded to raise land revenue from £818,000 in 1764–65 to £3,091,000 by the Permanent Settlement of 1793. 

The Permanent Settlement's political purpose is documented through primary sources. Lord Cornwallis, far from making an innocent administrative mistake, deliberately created a new landlord class. The evidence is conclusive: ==Lord William Bentick's speech of November 1829 stated that the Permanent Settlement, "though a failure in many other respects and in most important essentials, has this great advantage at least, of having created a vast body of rich landed proprietors deeply interested in the continuance of the British Dominion and having complete command over the mass of the people==." R. Palme Dutt's verdict is even sharper: "The purpose of the Permanent Settlement was to create a new class of landlords, after the English model as the social buttress of British rule."

For the cultivators, the consequences were catastrophic. Millions of raiyats who had enjoyed "permanent and heritable rights for generations were either rendered landless or reduced to the status of tenants-at-will." The Land Revenue Commission of Bengal summed up the additional pathology of subinfeudation: =="One of the most serious defects of the system is the excessive amount of subinfeudation it has encouraged... in some cases as many as 50 or more intermediate interests have been created between the Zamindar at the top and the actual cultivator at the bottom... an incubus on the working agricultural population, which finds no justification in the performance of any material service so far as agricultural improvements are concerned."==

==The Permanent Settlement also created an administrative vacuum. Because revenue had been fixed in perpetuity with no need for annual assessment, no revenue administration was established below the Sub-Divisional level in permanently settled areas. The consequence was corrupt and unreliable land records — a deficiency that would haunt every subsequent attempt at reform.==

### Regional Diversity: Avadh, Raiyatwari, and the Extent of Damage

Appu traces the damage systematically across regions. In Avadh, Lord Canning's 1858 decision to settle with the Talukdars — whom he privately acknowledged were "men distinguished neither by birth, good service or connection with the soil, who having held office under the native government... had abused the authority confided to them... had become the nominal proprietors and the actual possessors of the villages" — replicated the Bengal tragedy with eyes open. The UP Zamindari Abolition Committee's retrospective verdict bears full quotation: "The necessity for regulating the relations of landlords and tenants was recognized before the introduction of the Permanent Settlement in 1793. It is an amazing fact that effective protection was not given to tenants until the Congress Government passed the U.P. Tenancy Act in 1939, nearly a hundred and fifty years afterwards."

In the Raiyatwari areas of Madras and Bombay, the initial assessments were so exorbitant that the Madras Board of Revenue itself described them as effectively binding the cultivator "to the plough, compelling him to till land acknowledged to be over-assessed, dragging him back to it if he absconded, deferring their demand upon him until his crop comes to maturity, then taking from him all that could be obtained, and leaving him nothing but his bullocks and seed grain." The government's share was originally pitched at one-half of dry land produce and three-fifths of irrigated land — treating owner-cultivators as de facto tenants-at-will.

The chapter closes with two invaluable analytical frameworks. ==First, the statistical portrait: by 1905, per capita availability of food grains was 200.2 kg per annum (549 grams per day); by 1945 it had fallen to 152.2 kg per annum (417 grams per day) — a 24 per cent decline. Harold Mann's survey of a Deccan village found the average holding had fallen from 40 acres in 1771 to just 17 acres by 1915. Second, Daniel Thorner's three-class typology — Maliks (rentiers), Kisans (working peasants), and Mazdurs (labourers) — is laid out at length and endorsed as capturing the essential character of the Indian agrarian structure. In Thorner's formulation, which Appu quotes extensively: "This complex of legal, economic and social relations, uniquely typical of the Indian countryside served to produce an effect which I would like to call that of a built-in 'depressor'. Through the operation of this multifaceted 'depressor', Indian agriculture continued to be characterized by low capital intensity and antiquated methods."==

## Chapter 2: Abolition of Intermediary Interests

### The Character of Intermediary Exploitation

Chapter 2 opens with the most sustained empirical documentation of intermediary exploitation in the book. The intermediaries — Zamindars, Talukdars, Jagirdars, Malguzars, Janmies — controlled approximately 55–60 per cent of all privately owned agricultural land at independence. Their legal profits were enormous: in permanently settled Bihar districts, landlords' profits were 7.7 times the land revenue; in Madras, 4.5 times; in UP, more than one and a half times. In the thirties and forties, ==Appu records, "rent averaged 40–60 per cent of the crop in kind for India as a whole, but sometimes it was as high as 80 per cent of the crop==."

Over and above the legal rent sat a superstructure of illegal exactions (*abwabs*). =="The signing or renewal of tenancy was invariably an occasion to extort a year's or even two year's rental as a bonus. Religious festivals like Holi, Durga Puja, Diwali etc. were occasions for compulsory contributions to the Zamindar. Similarly domestic events like birth, marriage and death in the Zamindar's household called for special levies." Tenants supplied fodder for Zamindar's horses, funds for repairs to his mansion, and contributions "if the Zamindar extended hospitality to the Governor or other dignitaries or sent his offspring abroad for education." The UP Zamindari Abolition Committee found that "in some ilaqas illegal exactions amounted to as many as fifty in number" and that "crops ready for reaping were either forcibly taken away by the Zamindar's men or burnt down.== The tenant was sometimes made to stand in the sun with a stick between his legs." P. N. Driver estimated that such exactions normally amounted to about 25 per cent of the rent and in exceptional cases to 100 per cent or more.

Beyond economic exploitation lay social subjugation: "They enjoyed no security of life and property; the Zamindar could do what he pleased with the person and property of his tenants." Appu records without sensationalism that in some remote areas "rapacious Zamindars could even enforce the notorious medieval custom of Jus Primae Noctis (the right of the first night)."

The economic indictment is completed by noting that the intermediaries made not the slightest positive contribution to agricultural production. "The sole concern of the parasitic groups of intermediaries was to extort the largest possible share of the produce of the land without making any contribution whatsoever to agricultural production. In fact the system had absolutely no redeeming features."

### Policy and the Congress Dilemma

The evolution of Congress policy on Zamindari abolition reveals what Appu calls the fundamental "dichotomy" at the heart of the party's agrarian politics. ==The Congress was a party whose "leadership was almost entirely middle and upper-middle class. Most leaders owned land which they did not cultivate personally. And the solid rural base of the party comprised medium and small land owners and superior tenants." Its progressive agrarian rhetoric, therefore, had always to be read against this material base==.

The 1935 Allahabad conference under Sardar Patel was already insisting on "reasonable compensation" to Zamindars — a formulation that would shape everything that followed. The 1946 election manifesto committed to removal of intermediaries "on payment of equitable compensation." After independence, the Kumarappa Agrarian Reform Committee (1949) gave detailed proposals for abolition — but the AICC never formally adopted the report. Appu notes: "It would not have been in the best interests of the Congress Party to alienate that class. Those leaders were ever willing to subscribe to vague resolutions calling for social justice or an egalitarian social order... But they would have never subscribed to concrete proposals that would have adversely affected the interests of the rural rich."

### Legislation: The UP Act and its Progeny

==The UP Zamindari Abolition Act (1951), widely regarded as a model, created four new tenurial categories — Bhumidhar (absolute rights), Sirdar (permanent and heritable but not transferable), Asami (limited occupancy), and Adhivasi (transitional) — and brought approximately 15 million superior tenants into direct relationship with the State. But it allowed all 2 million ex-intermediaries to retain their home farms (*sir* and *khud-kasht*) as Bhumidhars with absolutely no ceiling==. A Sirdar wishing to acquire Bhumidhari rights had to pay ten times his annual rent as a lump sum — described by Appu as "a needlessly harsh provision." The definition of "personal cultivation" was broad enough to include cultivation "through share-croppers, servants, hired labour etc."

Jammu and Kashmir's law stood as the radical exception: a ceiling of 22.5 acres, no compensation to Zamindars, and "tiller" defined as a person who "actually tilled the land with his own hands."

In all other states, the legislative process was marked by what Appu calls "extreme dilatoriness." Daniel Thorner's description of the process — the mandatory inquiry committee, the deliberate drafting, the legislative committee referrals — is quoted with evident approval as showing that the delays were not accidents but reflections of "the half-hearted attitude of the ruling class to land reform." Thorner's warning still stands: "The holders of superior rights take advantage of delays to put through paper partitions of joint family properties, to suborn Village officers into falsifying crucial records, to intimidate tenants or crop-sharers into registering themselves as farm servants or wage labourers."

Total compensation paid to ex-intermediaries nationally came to Rs. 670 crores — "a heavy burden on the state exchequer" that "served no useful social purpose" since the Zamindars did not invest the proceeds productively.

### Implementation: Courts, Resistance, and Patwaris

==Judicial intervention brought implementation to a standstill in Bihar: the Patna High Court struck down the Bihar Land Reforms Act, necessitating the very first amendment to the Indian Constitution (First Amendment Act, 1951), which protected land reform legislation from judicial review on compensation grounds. Even then, Bihar landlords refused to hand over rent rolls and records, and obtained stay orders from the Calcutta High Court to avoid compliance.== "Many of these cases dragged on for several years and all the while the landlords continued in legal possession of their lands."

In Rajasthan, where the Jagirdari system covered over 80 per cent of the land, H. D. Malaviya records that "the feudal Jagirdars, incensed at the abolition of their heartless and inhuman exploitation, did their worst to defeat the implementation of the abolition legislation and took recourse to the most lawless activities and, like the barbarians of the middle ages, even burnt and pillaged the villages." Daniel Thorner concluded that in Rajasthan "Jagirdari had to be abolished because the Jagirdars were 'intermediaries'. But the Jagirdars had to live, so they were enabled to acquire Khud-Kasht lands and thereby to become cultivators. Now, as owner of Khud-Kasht lands, they are to be permitted to have their lands cultivated by tenants, i.e., to become once again intermediaries. Thus the full circle has been completed; Rajasthan is back where it started."

==The Revenue Department, entrusted with implementation, was structurally unsuited to the task==. The UP Zamindari Abolition Committee's description of the Patwari — the lowest revenue functionary — is devastating: =="a dishonest Patwari has therefore considerable opportunities for extortion. Most of them make good use of the opportunity... The proprietors being the most powerful as well as the wealthiest class in the village the Patwari finds it an advantage to keep on their side as against the cultivators, an alliance that is strengthened by the powers of nomination possessed by the land holders when the post falls vacant." Thorner's field visit confirmed that "the mischief which the patwaris perpetrated was not only great, it was also irreparable."==

### Results: Benefits and the Unreached Bottom

The reform's most significant achievement was eliminating the top layers of parasitic intermediaries and bringing approximately 25 million erstwhile tenants into direct relationship with the State. Myrdal's assessment is cited approvingly: "Though most persons who became owners or government tenants had enjoyed some protection under earlier laws, it had not always been effective... A further effect of these reforms has been to alter the power structure in rural areas." The emergence of backward-caste leaders like Laloo Prasad Yadav as Chief Minister of Bihar or Mulayam Singh Yadav in UP is attributed directly to the social transformation set in motion by abolition. About 6 million acres of wasteland and forest also vested in government and were distributed.

But the most damning verdict is on those left behind: "The bottom layer of the rural society consisting of agricultural labourers, share-croppers and other tenants-at-will received little benefit from the reform. As a matter of fact, there was only a deterioration in the condition of share-croppers and other tenants-at-will. Millions of them lost access to the land they had been cultivating." Myrdal observed: "sub-tenants, that is under-privileged tenants and share-croppers, drew no benefits from these reforms... A considerable number of them were socially and economically degraded when the Zamindars took over land for personal cultivation."

## Chapter 3: Tenancy Reform

### The Policy Framework: Three Guidelines and Their Erosion

With the abolition of intermediary interests, approximately 25 million superior tenants became landowners. But beneath them persisted a vast sub-tenantry of share-croppers and tenants-at-will in informal, oral, unrecorded arrangements terminable without notice. In Punjab alone, before partition, 15.26 million out of the cultivated area of 31.7 million acres were held by tenants-at-will. By the 1961 Census, 82 per cent of all tenancies in India were estimated to be insecure.

Three authoritative guidelines were laid down across the Five Year Plans: rent should not exceed one-fifth to one-fourth of the gross produce; tenants should be accorded permanent and heritable rights subject to a limited right of resumption by landowners; and in respect of non-resumable land, ownership rights should be conferred on tenants. Appu's analysis of what happened to each guideline is merciless.

The First Plan, cautious about alienating small and medium landowners (the Congress's rural base), allowed large landowners to resume land up to the ceiling limit for "personal cultivation" and gave small and medium owners a five-year period of resumption. As Appu notes, this meant "even a provision as in the Maharashtra law that the right of resumption should be exercised in strict chronological order beginning with the latest tenancy, is of little avail." The Third Plan finally acknowledged that "the impact of tenancy legislation on the welfare of tenants has been in practice less than was hoped for." The Fourth Plan (1969–74) called for declaring all tenancies non-resumable — by which point most tenants had already been evicted.

### Five Structural Loopholes

Appu identifies five structural defects in the tenancy laws that systematically undermined all nominal protections.

**First: exclusion of share-croppers.** In UP, it was expressly legislated that "any arrangement whereby a person is entitled to a right merely to share in the produce grown on the land in consideration of such person assisting or participating with the tenure holder in actual performance of agricultural operation is not a lease." West Bengal refused to classify bargadars (share-croppers) as tenants until 1970. Since share-cropping was the dominant form of tenancy, the bulk of the sub-tenantry was immediately outside the law's protection.

**Second: the right of resumption.** In states including Assam, Bihar, Haryana, Orissa, Punjab, and Rajasthan, landowners could resume land right up to the ceiling limit. Since the owner could choose which plots to resume, "all tenancies were rendered insecure and the tenants were harassed." The Planning Commission's Task Force observed in 1973: "The legal protection given to tenants has often been ineffective... The provision for the resumption of tenanted land by landowners resulted in ejectment of many tenants."

**Third: definition of "personal cultivation."** In every tenancy law, this term was defined to include cultivation through servants, hired labourers, or any member of the family — without even requiring the owner to reside near the land. The Second Plan's suggestion that the landowner "should also be required to put in personal labour in cultivating the land was incorporated only in the laws of Manipur and Tripura." As Appu writes: "Absentee landowners have had no difficulty at all in showing that they have been 'personally cultivating' their land."

**Fourth: voluntary surrenders.** "Many a landowner has successfully circumvented the tenancy laws and regained possession of tenanted land by 'persuading' his tenants to give up tenancy rights 'voluntarily'. The persuasion was, of course, often accompanied by threats; and, in some cases, even the use of brute force." In Punjab, the Planning Commission noted that out of 5,83,400 tenants who existed in 1955, all but 80,250 had been deprived of tenancy rights by 1964.

**Fifth: land records.** Most tenancies were oral, informal, and unrecorded. The subordinate functionaries of the Revenue Department "often act in collusion with the landowners." "Even when the rules provided for recording the rights of the share-croppers in the revenue records, usually no entry was made in the official registers as the employees of the Revenue Department were invariably under the sway of the landowners."

### State-by-State Performance: The Outliers

The comparative state analysis reveals the importance of political will and peasant organisation. **Maharashtra** conferred ownership rights on approximately 14.92 lakh tenants — perhaps 57 per cent of eligible tenants — a respectable result by national standards, though Dantwala and Shah's study found that "for every two cases of effective purchase there was one case of ineffective purchase" due to landlord pressure. **Gujarat** performed similarly, with 12.63 lakh tenants acquiring ownership rights.

**Kerala** achieved the best performance in the country. The State Bureau of Economics and Statistics found that 99 per cent of Kanam tenancies and 92 per cent of Verumpattom tenancies were eventually converted to ownership. A particularly notable feature was the conferment of ownership rights on 2,84,203 *Kudikidappukars* (hutment dwellers) — landless agricultural labourers — over 10 cents of homestead land around their huts. "The main reasons for the better implementation of tenancy reform in Kerala were the organization of agrarian struggles under the leadership of the Communist Party, and the general shift of political power in favour of weaker sections."

**West Bengal** represents the most dramatic case. Despite early exclusion of bargadars from protection, the 1977 amendment introduced the presumptive principle that any person lawfully cultivating another's land is a bargadar — shifting the burden of proof to the landowner. Operation Barga, launched in 1979, deployed squads of revenue officers into villages to record bargadars' names, backed by Left party workers: "The most important factor was, of course, the unambiguous and determined political direction... The campaign resulted in the recording of the names of 13,25,167 bargardars by June 1985." Appu's comment is pointed: "If only a similar operation had been mounted in the other States, particularly in Bihar, Orissa and Eastern Uttar Pradesh, substantial benefits would have accrued to hundreds of thousands of hapless share-croppers."

### Results and the Revisionist Prescription

The national balance sheet is stark. By 1992, ownership rights had been conferred on or tenancy rights protected for approximately 11 million tenants on 14.4 million acres — roughly 4 per cent of the operated area. From a position in which over half the operated area was under tenancy, the area under tenancy had fallen to about 15 per cent — not because tenants became owners, but because the reform's principal effect was to push tenancies underground.

Appu's revisionist conclusion is the most controversial in the book: he calls for lifting the ban on leasing. "The banning of tenancies and imposing restrictions on leasing out has only led to tenancies being pushed underground... Landowners who are not in a position to cultivate their land, may also prefer leasing out their land to keeping it fallow, for there will be no risk of their losing their land. The result will be greater accessibility of the rural poor to agricultural land, enhanced employment opportunities and increased output." The leases, he specifies, should be strictly regulated — written, fixed-term, with rent ceilings — but the ban itself should go.

## Chapter 4: Ceiling on Agricultural Holdings

### A History of Ambivalence

The ceiling chapter is the most detailed and analytically demanding in the book. Its central argument is that the imposition of ceilings represents "a case of inchoate policy, imperfect legislation and inefficient implementation" — and that all three failures were, at bottom, politically determined.

Gandhi's 1942 statement to Louis Fischer — that in independent India peasants would take the land they cultivated without compensation to landlords — is cited as marking the outer limit of Congress radical thought on the subject, never subsequently approached in policy. After independence, both the Economic Programme Committee (1947) and the Kumarappa Committee (1949) recommended ceiling imposition. Yet the First Plan (1951) postponed action on the grounds that data were unavailable. The UP Zamindari Abolition Committee, headed by Chief Minister Gobind Ballabh Pant, had simultaneously and actively opposed ceilings, arguing that "the results achieved by the redistribution of land would be commensurate with the discontent and hardship resulting from it" — and concluded that "no limit be placed on the maximum area held in cultivation either by a landlord or tenant."

This was the Congress's structural problem in miniature: progressive rhetoric at the top, conservative power at the base. "Several stalwarts among the top leaders of the Congress, and the bulk of the middle level leaders were opposed to progressive measures of reform which would adversely affect men of property who wielded considerable influence in the villages." The A.I.C.C. formally approved the Economic Programme Committee report but never discussed or adopted the Kumarappa Report.

### First Round of Laws: Variations and Vices

The Second Plan (1956–61) finally recommended ceilings, suggesting a level of approximately three "family holdings" — and then left every detail of definition to the states. The result was legislative chaos. Ceiling levels for a family of five on the best land ranged from 10 acres (Kerala) to over 100 acres in some states (Andhra Pradesh allowed one additional family holding for every member beyond five, with no upper limit). Andhra Pradesh's ceiling for a family on the poorest land could reach 324 acres; Bihar's for an undivided Hindu family could expand well beyond the stated limits through provisions for co-parceners. Exemptions multiplied: tea, coffee, rubber, sugarcane farms, "compact, well-managed, mechanised farms," orchards, cattle-breeding farms, religious and educational trusts, and more.

Most laws failed to nullify transfers made in anticipation of legislation. Orissa's original Act contained no provision for disregarding transfers whatsoever. Kerala's law provided for ignoring transfers, but the exceptions were "so numerous as to make the provision totally infructuous." Bihar permitted a landholder to transfer land within six months of commencement of the Act by gift to family members — a provision so permissive it essentially invited legal evasion.

By December 1970, after a decade of implementation, only approximately 1 million hectares of surplus land had vested in State Governments across the country, with only about half distributed. Jammu and Kashmir, with its simple no-compensation law fixed in 1950, had alone distributed 1.8 lakh hectares. West Bengal had vested 3.46 lakh hectares.

### The Second Round: Political Theatre and Partial Corrections

The revival of interest in ceilings after 1969 is one of the most revealing passages in the entire book. Three factors drove it: the Communist-led land-grab movement, Naxalite agrarian unrest (which the Home Ministry's 1969 paper diagnosed as rooted in land reform failure), and — crucially — the Congress split and Indira Gandhi's adoption of populist programmes. Appu is blunt: "As this new interest in land reform was born out of partisan electoral calculations and not out of any genuine political convictions, it lasted for only a short period."

Appu participated directly in the Central Land Reforms Committee meetings of 1971–72 that formulated revised guidelines. He records the drama in detail: his own first months as Land Reforms Commissioner producing a background paper from a mass of unfamiliar literature; the July 1972 controversy over whether privately irrigated land should be treated on par with government-irrigated land (which Ladejinsky called "the most strident of debates, almost unprecedented in the annals of the political history of India"); and the final recommendations reducing ceiling levels to a band of 10–18 acres for irrigated double-cropped land, 27 acres for single-cropped, and 54 acres for dry land.

One admission in this chapter is especially candid. Appu acknowledges his own error in accepting the family-unit framework: "I must confess that in 1970–72 I also made the blunder of accepting without questioning the prevailing conventional wisdom that imposing the ceiling on the family basis would be a step in the right direction." In practice, since land records showed individual not family ownership, the family-unit provision required ascertaining the age of every family member — opening "the flood gates for corruption." A simpler ceiling on individuals, he argues, would have produced considerably better results.

After all the amendments, by October 1992 only 4.961 million acres of surplus land had been distributed nationally — 1.24 per cent of the operated area of the eighteen states covered. Jammu and Kashmir (17.77 per cent), West Bengal (6.715 per cent), and Assam (5.9 per cent) were the only significant performers. The Government of India's own assessment: "Judged by the quantum of land declared surplus, this reform measure has failed to achieve any substantial impact."

The ceiling laws did, Appu concedes, prevent some further concentration of land. Holdings above 10 hectares fell from 4.75 per cent of all operational holdings in 1960–61 to 2.4 per cent in 1980–81. "In the context of the significant growth in agricultural production since the late sixties there would have been a scramble for the purchase of agricultural land resulting in greater concentration in the ownership of land but for the existence of ceiling laws." This is genuine, if modest, credit.

## Chapter 5: Conclusion

### The Balance Sheet

The Conclusion is the most politically charged writing in the book and among the most direct in any Indian policy study. Appu opens with the objectives as formally stated in the Third Five Year Plan — to remove agrarian-structural impediments to production and to eliminate exploitation and social injustice — and then assembles the evidence that both objectives were largely unmet.

Of the three programmes, abolition of intermediary interests was "implemented more efficiently" than the others — though this is relative. Tenancy reform produced ownership rights or protected rights on only 4 per cent of the operated area. Ceilings redistributed less than 2 per cent. Together: about 6 per cent. Against China's redistribution of 43 per cent of agricultural land in 1950–52, Taiwan's 37 per cent, South Korea's 32 per cent, and Japan's 33 per cent, India's performance is of a different order of magnitude entirely.

At the same time Appu resists the temptation to write the reform off as a non-event. The Zamindari system "with all its notorious abuses has disappeared yielding place to a new rural power-structure in which the erstwhile Zamindars share power with their former substantial tenants." The shift to backward-caste political leadership across north India is directly traceable to this transformation. "These are no mean achievements in a democratic set up where the protection of vested interests is guaranteed by the rule of law." But "the reforms left untouched the bottom layer of the agrarian structure consisting of agricultural labourers, share-croppers and other tenants-at-will and rural artisans. They received little benefit from the reform."

### The Political Will Thesis: Evidence from Inside

The identification of lack of political will as the primary cause of failure is backed with first-hand evidence of unusual specificity. Prime Minister Indira Gandhi's meeting with Wolf Ladejinsky is recorded: "He spent a few minutes trying to explain the crux of the problem. Indira Gandhi showed no interest in the subject and after a few minutes she started looking into her papers. Ladejinsky took the hint, said good-bye and left." At the state level, Bansi Lal of Haryana "opened the discussion by shouting at me and accusing me of being the evil influence responsible for the Central Government's adamant attitude on land reform." Bihar's Chief Minister Jagannath Mishra complained within Appu's hearing that share-croppers were "indulging in lawlessness" and that the police were not doing enough to "protect the landlords."

The Bihar vignette about District Officer James Lyngdoh is the most telling. Lyngdoh had taken "deterrent action against the landlords' men" who had forcibly harvested crops grown by share-croppers, and was consequently transferred over Appu's strong protests. Deputy Chief Minister Daroga Prasad Rai's response to Appu's challenge — "a sheepish smile" and the admission that "the landlords were so powerful that he could do nothing" — is a miniature of the entire national story.

Appu's verdict is unsparing: "On the basis of my personal experience of the Central and State Governments, I have no doubt at all that the absence of political will was the prime reason for the poor implementation of land reforms in India."

### The Post-Reform Agrarian Structure and New Perspectives

The chapter closes with an acute analysis of the post-reform situation. The proportion of the workforce dependent on agriculture remained at roughly 70 per cent from 1921 to 1971. Agriculture's share of GDP fell from 60 per cent at independence to 31 per cent in 1991 — a declining share supporting an unchanged number of people. The comparison of Ludhiana and Purnea is used to crystallise the argument that semi-feudal land relations in eastern India remain the principal obstacle to realising the agricultural potential of the eastern Gangetic plains: in the 1960s, while Ludhiana's agricultural output grew at 7.98 per cent per annum, Purnea's declined by 2.29 per cent per annum.

The book's final policy positions reflect a pragmatism born of long experience. P. C. Joshi's analysis of why the second phase of reform is harder than the first is quoted sympathetically: "The second phase... attacks not just the principle and practice of unearned property income... but appears also to launch a war against the principle of income earned through enterprise in agriculture. It provokes, therefore, a large section of the peasantry into a state of active or passive resistance." M. L. Dantwala's view that India has "missed the bus" is cited without endorsement: "While I would still plead for a more egalitarian land ownership, I would urge social scientists concerned with the elimination of poverty and affluence in rural areas to shift their emphasis from re-distribution of land to a more equitable access to non-land resources."

Appu does not fully accept Dantwala's pessimism. The programme of measures he outlines — lifting the ban on leasing (with strict regulation), modernising land records, mounting Operation-Barga-style campaigns in Bihar and eastern UP to record share-croppers' rights, and enforcing existing ceiling laws rigorously against identified landowners — adds up to an agenda that is neither utopian nor defeatist. It is the agenda of someone who has governed, understands the obstacles intimately, and still believes the effort is worth making.

## The Ludhiana–Purnea Counterpoint: Land Reform and the Green Revolution

One of the most analytically powerful passages in the Conclusion is Appu's comparison of agricultural productivity in Ludhiana (Punjab) and Purnea (Bihar), two districts with comparable natural resource endowments — alluvial soils, plentiful ground water, good rainfall — but dramatically divergent agrarian structures and outcomes.

The data Appu presents are revealing. During the 1960s, Ludhiana's agricultural output grew at an annual rate of 7.98 per cent; Purnea's declined by 2.29 per cent. By 1988, the average per-acre yield of wheat was 4.38 quintals in Ludhiana against 1.28 in Purnea; rice yielded 3.24 quintals per acre in Ludhiana and 1.08 in Purnea. Fertilizer consumption in the 1970s was 41,359 metric tons in Ludhiana and 3,255 in Purnea. Gross irrigated area in the 1970s was 4,25,266 hectares in Ludhiana and 98,125 in Purnea.

Appu directly rejects the "glib explanation that the Punjabi farmer is hardworking while his Bihar counterpart is lazy and unenterprising." The real explanation is institutional: "the reasons for the under-utilization of the irrigation potential, the low levels of inputs and the lack of initiative and enterprise on the part of the farmer are all rooted in the semi-feudal relations of production that exist in the agriculture of Purnea." Approximately 40 per cent of land in Purnea in 1972–73 was under share-cropping, with share-croppers having no security, handing over half the produce or more, and having no incentive to invest in inputs or adopt improved practices. The landlord class, for its part, views land "not as a productive asset that should be operated efficiently to maximise their income. To them land is essentially a store of value, a hedge against inflation, an instrument for appropriating the large unearned increments accruing from the huge public investments in irrigation and, most importantly, the very foundation of political and economic power and social status."

This comparison is significant beyond agriculture. It stands as evidence for the proposition — contested but here empirically grounded — that failure to reform agrarian relations in eastern India is the single most important explanation for the paradox of that region's underperformance relative to its natural endowment. Appu notes that the Reserve Bank of India's S.R. Sen Committee (1984) also found that "the persistence of some semi-feudal institutions and forces in the region act as an impediment to investment and dampen the will and capacity for change on the part of farmers in spite of the rich agricultural base."

## The Appendices: Primary Documents of Historical Value

The book's analytical chapters are substantively enriched by six major appendices that are in many cases primary documents of independent importance.

**Appendix I** (Availability of Food Grains 1905–1985) provides the quantitative baseline for the argument about agricultural stagnation under colonialism. The fall from 200.2 kg per capita per annum in 1905 to 152.2 kg in 1945 — a 24 per cent decline over four decades — is the empirical foundation for the First Plan's emergency emphasis on food production.

**Appendix II.1** (Illegal Exactions in Princely States of Rajasthan) is a remarkable primary document. The list of levies exacted by Jagirdars in Rajasthan — many with specific local names — runs to dozens of categories and demonstrates in concrete detail what "abwabs" meant in practice. Items include levies for birth of a son in the Jagirdar's family, on the occasion of his daughter's marriage, on the Jagirdar taking a new wife, for the purchase of a motor car, and — remarkably — for the Jagirdar visiting a city. The document reads as an inventory of a feudal system at its most extractive.

**Appendix IV.1** (Appu's 1971 ceiling paper) is arguably the most important appendix: the actual working paper that shaped the Central Land Reforms Committee's 1971–72 recommendations. Reading it alongside Chapter 4 allows the reader to trace precisely how the policy was developed, which recommendations were accepted, which diluted, and why. It is a rare document — a civil servant's working paper preserved in the published record.

**Appendix V** (Task Force Report on Agrarian Relations, 1973) is the Planning Commission's most comprehensive assessment of land reform failure up to that date. Since copies of the original report are no longer readily available, its inclusion in full is a genuine service to scholarship. The Task Force identified six principal causes of poor performance: lack of political will; absence of pressure from below; inadequacies of the administrative machinery; judicial intervention; absence of correct and up-to-date land records; and lack of supporting facilities for beneficiaries. Appu's own retrospective assessment, two decades later, is that none of these findings requires revision.

**Appendix V.2** (Kosi Division Correspondence) is politically the most charged document in the book: the letters between Appu and Bihar's Chief Secretary protesting the transfer of James Lyngdoh, the district officer who had protected share-croppers' right to harvest the paddy they had grown. The letters make plain that political will to thwart reform was exercised not only by passive inaction but by active transfer of the few officers who took the law seriously.

## Critical Assessment

*Land Reforms in India* has few equals as a combination of policy survey, administrative history, and political diagnosis. As P.C. Joshi observed of the second phase of reform — "The second phase attacks not just the principle and practice of unearned property income... but appears also to launch a war against the principle of income earned through enterprise in agriculture" — the political economy of land reform becomes harder, not easier, over time. Appu's book represents the best available analysis of why the window of opportunity that existed at independence was largely squandered.

Its most important contribution is neither empirical nor comparative but analytical: the demonstration, through meticulously documented evidence, that the failure of Indian land reform was not primarily technical but political. The laws were not weak because their framers did not know what good laws looked like — the Kumarappa Report, the Plan guidelines, and Appu's own working papers show they did. The laws were weak because the dominant political coalition of the Congress — upper and middle caste landowners, superior tenants, and professionals — had every interest in weakening them.

This argument has sometimes been made at the level of structural sociology (Francine Frankel, for instance, or Bardhan's analysis of dominant proprietary classes). Appu makes it at the level of documented, named, datable events: a Chief Minister shouting in a meeting; a district officer transferred for protecting share-croppers; a Prime Minister looking at her papers while a land reform expert spoke. The texture of the evidence is different in kind from aggregate analysis, and it is more convincing.

The book's limitations are those of its era and approach. Gender and land rights are virtually absent — a significant gap given women's systematic exclusion from ownership under most state laws. The relationship between land reform failure and the Green Revolution's uneven distribution of benefits is sketched but not developed. Tribal land alienation — a distinct and in many ways more acute problem — falls largely outside the scope. And the book's focus on the three formal programmes means that the informal reconcentration of land through money-lending, distress sales, and contractual tenancy is treated mainly as a consequence of reform failure rather than analysed in its own right.

But these are criticisms of agenda, not of execution. Within its declared scope, the work is thorough, honest, and analytically rigorous. The appendices — particularly the full text of the Task Force Report on Agrarian Relations (Appendix V), the Kosi Division correspondence (Appendix V.2), and the ceiling proposal paper (Appendix IV.1) — are primary documents of historical importance. For students of Development Studies, Appu's book remains the indispensable starting point for any serious engagement with the politics of land in India.

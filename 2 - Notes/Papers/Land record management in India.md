# Claude Primer
### Systematic Review: "Land Record Management in India" (Kapoor, Esposito & Anand, WP–2024–016)

---

#### 1. Bibliographic Overview

This is a policy-oriented working paper published in February 2024 under Stanford University's US-Asia Technology Management Center and Gurgaon's Institute for Competitiveness. It is explicitly circulated for comment and discussion only — not peer-reviewed — and should be read accordingly. Three authors spanning techno-economic policy, international development (Harvard CID), and policy research collaborate to argue for Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) as a governance solution to India's land records crisis.

---

#### 2. Central Argument and Structure

The paper's thesis moves through three stages. First, it diagnoses India's land records system as structurally dysfunctional — operating on presumptive (not conclusive) titling, fragmented across three siloed departments, and producing records that do not guarantee ownership. Second, it argues that the flagship digitisation programme (DILRMP) has addressed form but not substance: computerisation has proceeded without fixing legal ambiguity, inter-departmental incoherence, or exclusion of marginalised tenure holders. Third, it proposes DLT — specifically permissioned blockchain — as the technological infrastructure capable of delivering the transparency, immutability, and decentralisation that DILRMP cannot. It closes with a governance roadmap for integration.

The structure is: constitutional and historical background → current lacunae → DLT primer → case studies (India and abroad) → critical problematisation of DILRMP → policy roadmap → conclusion. This sequencing is logical but the transition from critique (Section V) to prescription (Section VI) is notably abrupt, a point returned to below.

---

#### 3. Methodology

The paper uses secondary qualitative review — synthesising government reports (DILRMP MIS 2.0, NITI Aayog, CAG performance audit on Tamil Nadu), legislative texts, think-tank analyses (PRS Legislative Research, Vidhi Centre), civil society reports (IT for Change), academic literature, and investigative journalism (Scroll, Article 14). No primary fieldwork is conducted. This is appropriate for a policy working paper, but the paper presents no explicit search protocol, selection criteria, or acknowledgment of possible source bias. The literature is chosen to support the argument rather than to test it — which is an acceptable approach for a prescriptive policy document but limits its academic standing.

Quantitative data is drawn entirely from government MIS portals and the CAG audit. Table 3 on state-wise map digitisation is the most substantive original data compilation, though it is simply reproduced from the Department of Land Resources without independent analysis.

---

#### 4. Key Findings

**On India's land records architecture.** The paper provides a clear and well-sourced account of why India's land records are systematically unreliable. The Transfer of Property Act 1882 registers transactions, not titles; mutation (the update of the Record of Rights after a transaction) is a separate bureaucratic step with no automatic link to registration; and the three departments managing land — Revenue, Survey & Settlement, and Registration — operate with no shared data infrastructure. Nearly two-thirds of pending civil court cases involve land disputes (World Bank, 2007). Average dispute resolution time is approximately 20 years (NITI Aayog).

**On DILRMP's progress and failures.** The paper does useful work presenting the state-level variation in DILRMP implementation. While computerisation of land registrations stands at 94% nationally, geo-referencing of cadastral maps averages just 49.77% and linkage of maps to Records of Rights is 70.97%. States like Punjab (0% geo-referencing), Kerala (0% RoR linkage), and Uttarakhand (33.9% map digitisation) show severe gaps. The Tamil Nadu CAG audit (2022) is used effectively to show that digitisation introduced new errors: approximately 3.22 lakh private parcels were misclassified as government land; 61% of studied villages had significant area discrepancies between manual and computerised records.

**On DLT's potential.** The paper argues that DLT aligns naturally with the Torrens system's three principles — the mirror principle (accurate reflection of assets), the curtain principle (registered facts suffice; no ownership trail needed), and the indemnity principle (state compensates for errors). The Andhra Pradesh pilot (blockchain + GIS via ChromaWay and Zebi Records, approximately 1 lakh land records on-chain in Amaravati) and Sweden's three-phase experiment are presented as proof-of-concept.

**On exclusions.** Section V is the paper's most critical and arguably most important section. Drawing on Gurumurthy et al. (2022, IT for Change) and Bromley (2008), it documents how DILRMP has delegitimised customary tenure, converted communal rights into individual titles (Coorg), and structurally excluded non-landowning farmers, pastoralists, fisherpeople, and tribal communities from the formalisation process. The Jharkhand case — where private data-entry agencies (2008–2016) introduced errors stripping tribal communities of land — is a particularly important empirical anchor.

---

#### 5. Critical Evaluation

**Strengths.** The paper's primary contribution is bringing together constitutional law, administrative fragmentation, technology architecture, and agrarian critique in a single document. For a policy audience unfamiliar with all these registers, the synthesis has genuine value. The inclusion of IT for Change and Scroll/Article 14 investigative journalism alongside government reports is an unusual and commendable methodological choice for a policy working paper — it grounds the critique in documented ground-level harms.

**The central internal contradiction.** The paper's most significant intellectual problem is one it partially recognises but never resolves. Section V demonstrates that digitisation under DILRMP has legitimised erroneous and exclusionary records — in Gujarat, the programme was halted because of cascading correction demands; in Jharkhand, it produced dispossession. DLT's principal claimed virtue is immutability: once a record is on-chain, it cannot be altered. The paper acknowledges this problem obliquely but then proceeds to recommend DLT adoption. If erroneous data is immutably recorded on a blockchain, the technology entrenches the error permanently. This is not addressed adequately in the governance roadmap.

**Technology solutionism.** The paper's institutional critique is sharp — but its prescription reinstates technology as the primary vehicle of reform. The move from "digitisation failed because of institutional, political, and social reasons" to "what we need is a more sophisticated technology" does not follow without a more detailed account of how the institutional and political conditions will change. Rodima-Taylor (2021) is cited — she argues blockchain for land formalisation in developing countries is constrained by its continued dependence on intermediaries and cannot directly facilitate contractual claims — but this insight is not integrated into the policy roadmap.

**Uncritical treatment of case studies.** The Andhra Pradesh and Telangana sections are largely official accounts. Andhra Pradesh's blockchain pilot was confined to the politically and financially contentious Amaravati capital region; the paper does not mention the political crisis around Amaravati's construction (abandoned by the subsequent YSRCP government), which casts doubt on the pilot's durability and scalability. Georgia's adoption is presented as straightforwardly successful, but its post-Soviet political economy, historically weak customary tenure complexity, and small population are not acknowledged as factors limiting transferability to India's federal, plural, and contested agrarian context.

**Absent dimensions.** Gender and land rights are strikingly absent. Given that women's exclusion from formal title in India is well-documented and DILRMP has been shown to worsen this (Gurumurthy et al. 2022), a paper on land records modernisation that does not engage with gendered dispossession is a significant lacuna. Urban informal tenure, despite brief mentions, is not substantively addressed. The political economy of bureaucratic consolidation — the paper recommends merging three departments into one — is presented as a technical recommendation without engaging with the substantial literature on why such consolidations fail.

**Methodological limitations.** There is no stated analytical framework, no systematic literature review protocol, no triangulation across sources, and no attempt to falsify the central thesis. Quantitative claims occasionally lack citations (e.g., the stamp duty comparison across countries). Several references are listed as "(n.d.)" indicating incomplete sourcing. These are partly acceptable limitations for a working paper but limit its use in academic citation.

---

#### 6. Theoretical Positioning

The paper implicitly rests on a de Soto-ian foundation: secure property rights enable collateral, collateral enables credit, credit enables investment, investment reduces poverty. This is never explicitly stated or defended. The paper's engagement with Bromley (2008) in Section V partially destabilises this foundation — Bromley argues that formalisation without addressing employment, urbanisation, and agricultural investment produces neither development nor security — but the prescription does not incorporate this critique.

The paper would benefit from engagement with James Scott's legibility thesis (_Seeing Like a State_, 1998): land formalisation is simultaneously a project of state simplification that destroys the complexity and adaptability of local tenure arrangements, and DLT amplifies the legibility demand by making records permanent and machine-readable. Ostrom's commons governance framework is directly relevant to the CPR de-recognition problem in Section V. The STS literature on sociotechnical imaginaries would help the paper account for why techno-governance promises consistently exceed delivery in contexts of institutional weakness.

---

#### 7. Significance and Suggested Pairing

The paper is a useful introductory document for students in Development Studies engaging with digital governance, agrarian land rights, or Indian political economy. Its particular utility is as a text that can be read critically — its internal contradictions between critique and prescription make it an excellent basis for seminar discussion on the limits of techno-developmental reasoning.

For the TISS Development Studies context, this paper pairs productively with Gurumurthy et al. (2022) on CPR exclusion and data governance; Bromley (2008) on formalisation; Rodima-Taylor (2021) on blockchain in Global South land formalisation; and Goswami, Jha & Lushington (2020) on land title legislation in India — all of which the paper itself cites, though sometimes insufficiently. Adding Scott (1998), Agarwal (1994) on gender and land, and Rangarajan & Saberwal on CPR politics in India would build a more complete theoretical framework.

---

**Summary verdict:** A competent and well-contextualised policy document with genuine value as a synthesis and discussion starter. Its treatment of DILRMP's exclusionary outcomes in Section V is its strongest contribution. Its core weakness is an unresolved contradiction between an institutional critique of digitisation and a prescription for deeper technological integration — a contradiction that is itself theoretically revealing for Development Studies analysis.

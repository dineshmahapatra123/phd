# Comprehensive Review: "Illegibly Legible: Outcomes of a Land Records Modernisation Programme in South India"

**Ramakumar R & Padmini Ramesh | *Journal of Agrarian Change*, 23(4), 2023, pp. 729–754**

---

## I. Overview and Significance

This article examines Telangana's Land Records Updation Programme (LRUP) of 2017–2018, a state-level initiative to digitise and update textual land records. Published in the *Journal of Agrarian Change*, the paper situates this techno-administrative exercise within a broader political economy of land, colonialism, and neoliberalism. The authors are affiliated with the School of Development Studies at TISS, Mumbai — the paper emerged from the NABARD Chair Professor Unit at TISS and is based on fieldwork conducted in 2019, primarily in Adilabad district.

The central argument is that land records modernisation in Telangana failed to achieve its stated objectives not because of purely technical or administrative shortcomings, but because it was structurally disembedded from the historical and political economy of land ownership. The LRUP — driven by the neoliberal agenda of welfare targeting via the *Rythu Bandhu Scheme* (RBS) rather than agrarian egalitarianism — reproduced and in many instances deepened the very illegibilities it claimed to resolve.

The title itself is conceptually rich: "illegibly legible" captures the paper's central irony — that land records which were declared "purified" and made digitally "legible" remained in practice illegible, inaccurate, contested, and politically loaded.

---

## II. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 Land as a Contested Resource

The authors begin with a theoretical mapping of land across four overlapping dimensions: as a *site of production*, as a *commodity*, as a *site of multiple competing non-agricultural uses*, and as an object requiring modern administrative governance. Drawing on Marx (1959) and Polanyi (1944), the paper establishes the foundational tension: land is simultaneously essential to capitalist accumulation and, as Polanyi put it, "eminently ill-suited to be a commodity" — its commodity description being "entirely fictitious."

This theoretical grounding is important because it frames the paper's core question: why do land records modernisation programmes, however technically sophisticated, fail to resolve the political contradictions embedded in land relations?

### 2.2 Scott's Legibility Framework

The authors draw heavily on James Scott's (1998) *Seeing Like a State* to frame the colonial creation of land records as a shift from "illegibility" to "legibility" of customary land tenures. Colonial cadastral surveys simplified, codified, and standardised local practices — not for the cultivators or landowners, but, as Marx (1853) observed, for "the government that taxes it." This process generated anti-colonial resistance and, after independence, "path dependence" in land administration — a colonial legacy that persisted even as political regimes changed.

The Scott framework is used productively, but the authors go beyond it: they show that neoliberal legibility-making does not simply simplify complex realities into state-legible forms, but actively *distorts* them in service of new power configurations — corporate land acquisition, welfare targeting, and the exclusion of tenants.

### 2.3 The Neoliberal Turn in Land Administration

The paper devotes substantial attention to how neoliberalism redefined the project of land records modernisation from the 1980s onwards. Whereas the post-War developmental state had an (admittedly often failed) aspiration to use land records modernisation in support of land reforms and agrarian redistribution, the neoliberal state reoriented the same project toward the creation of free land markets. The authors note that institutions like USAID, the World Bank, and McKinsey actively promoted "conclusive titling" — the Torrens system — as a mechanism to improve the Ease of Doing Business (EoDB) and facilitate investment. By 2005, the World Bank was supervising land administration projects worth more than $1 billion.

The paper is particularly sharp in its critique of **technocracy** — defined, drawing on Cole (2022), as "the administrative and political domination of society by a state elite … through the application of instrumentally rational techniques." Technocracy, the authors argue, does not merely serve efficiency goals; it actively depoliticises land governance, insulates state power from democratic accountability, and "fetishises the need to contain political pressures."

The empirical literature on land titling, the authors show, is itself deeply ambiguous. Miceli and Kieyah (2003) found that even in the most successful US states, fewer than half of land records entered new Torrens-based systems. Atwood (1990) argued that African land titling programmes "would not have the intended impact, would not be economically justifiable, or would even be counter-productive." The paper's theoretical contribution is to explain *why* — not merely by pointing to administrative dysfunction, but by showing how the sociocultural and political economy characteristics of land systematically frustrate technocratic solutions.

The Bhoomi initiative in Karnataka is cited as a telling precedent: rather than reducing corruption and increasing efficiency, as claimed, Benjamin and Raman (2011) argue that Bhoomi was designed "to allow big businesses to locate in the rapidly urbanising peripheries of Karnataka's big cities." The problem was in treating "the illegibility of land records not as a 'political condition' but as a 'normative' problem."

## III. Contextual Background: Land Records in India

### 3.1 The Structure of Land Records

The authors provide an important technical overview of the Indian land records system, which is fragmented across three departments:

- The **Revenue Department** maintains textual records: the Record of Rights (RoR, called Form 1-B in Telangana), the Mutation Register, the *Pahani* (called *Adangal* elsewhere), and the *Khata* register.
- The **Survey and Settlement Department** maintains spatial records: the Field Measurement Book (FMB, called *Tippan* in Telangana) and village/cadastral maps.
- The **Registration Department** maintains transaction records: sale deeds.

Crucially, these three departments have historically worked in silos — information from a sale deed registered in the Registration Department, for instance, is not automatically reflected in the RoR maintained by the Revenue Department. This structural fragmentation is a fundamental, persistent source of error and illegibility. The last resurvey in most Indian villages was conducted during the colonial period — the average age of cadastral maps in 2009 was estimated at over 50 years.

### 3.2 Historical Failure of Past Reforms

The paper documents how successive policy efforts — from the Five-Year Plans of the 1950s, through the D.C. Wadhwa committee of 1987, to the NLRMP (2008) and its successor DILRMP (2014) — have all failed to meaningfully modernise land records. The impact assessment of the DILRMP showed that in Maharashtra, about 50% of sample land parcels recorded more than 20% deviation in area from revenue records. Only about 13% of Indian villages had been resurveyed by 2022, and only 9% of the country's area had been resurveyed.

The reason for this historical failure is explained through political economy: "the neglect of land records in India after independence was closely associated with the efforts of landlords to evade land reform laws; in this sense, the poor quality of land records had the implicit sanction of the Indian state." Tenants were systematically omitted from land records to facilitate their eviction; records were manipulated to circumvent land ceiling laws.

## IV. Telangana's Colonial Legacy

Section 4 is one of the most original and historically rich parts of the paper. The authors trace the unreliability of Telangana's land records in 2017 to over a century of accumulated changes and manipulations.

Telangana inherited multiple and overlapping systems of land administration from the colonial period — Mughal, Nizam, and British — each producing their own documentation systems, nomenclature, and anomalies. The 1932–1934 survey settlement and *bandobust* operation produced the *sethwar*, which became the benchmark document in LRUP — yet it was already the product of a revenue-maximising colonial exercise, not a comprehensive record of actual cultivation or tenure.

Post-independence, the 1950 Hyderabad Tenancy and Agricultural Lands Act abolished the *sarf khas* and *jagirdari* systems and introduced the 38-E certificate for tenant protection. The *khasra pahani* of 1954, however, was prepared without any fresh survey, using the old *sethwar*, and "under pressure from landlords, the village *patwaris* sent the department the list of original holders listed in the *sethwar* and not the names of actual cultivators." This fundamentally corrupted the records from the outset.

The 1973 Land Reforms (Ceiling on Agricultural Holdings) Act triggered another wave of manipulation: "the names of *benamis* of landlords and large landowners were inserted" in place of tenants and actual cultivators, with new survey numbers added to cover up the fraud. "Most registers prepared after 1932–1934, especially in the 1970s, were products of manipulation engineered by landlords — to evade land ceiling laws — with the connivance of revenue officials and the *patwaris*."

By 2017, an official estimate placed the number of errors in Telangana's land records at 4.2 million.

## V. The LRUP: Design and Implementation

### 5.1 Objectives and Motivation

The LRUP was initiated in September 2017, ostensibly to "purify" land records. However, the authors are emphatic that its actual motivation was the imminent introduction of the *Rythu Bandhu Scheme* (RBS) — a direct income support scheme of Rs 5,000 per acre per season, requiring an accurate database of landowners. The TRS government was facing re-election in 2018, and the RBS was seen as a politically expedient rural support measure. The LRUP was thus subordinated to an electoral and welfare-targeting logic, not to a genuine agrarian reform agenda.

As a lawyer expert on land rights observed to the authors: "In the 1930s, the survey settlement and *bandobust* operations were done with the objective of *who should pay the money*. The LRUP was done with the objective of *who should receive the money*. This difference in the objective of the survey is indicative of the attitude with which it was carried out."

### 5.2 Administrative Process

The implementation was organised into two parts: Part A covered undisputed records or undisputed errors; Part B set aside disputed cases and pending court matters. Teams headed by *tehsildars*, deputy *tehsildars*, and Revenue Inspectors visited each village, distributed 1-B extracts, convened *gram sabhas*, and were expected to complete each village in 10 days. The scale was impressive: 30 districts, 568 *mandals*, 10,283 villages, 1,523 teams, 3,500 revenue officers, and approximately 23.8 million acres verified.

The paper provides detailed and valuable ethnographic documentation of the types of disputes encountered: succession and inheritance disputes (*fouthis*), mutation disputes, *sadabainama* (unregistered) transactions, encroachments on government-assigned land, acquired lands, *wakf* and *bhoodan* lands, disputes over names, areas and survey numbers, and disputes in Schedule 5 tribal and forest areas.

## VI. Outcomes and Failures

### 6.1 The Gap Between Claimed and Actual Outcomes

The government claimed a "purity" level of 93% — meaning 93% of PPBs were "cleared." The paper systematically dismantles this claim. As the authors observe, "a purity level of 93% only meant that new data were entered into 93% of the land records based either on the previous entries or on the responses received from persons whom the government assumed as the real landowners."

Complaints began proliferating almost immediately — ranging from arbitrary classification of undisputed lands under Part B, to outright errors in names, survey numbers, and areas, to lands being wrongly entered as government or industrial land. The Hyderabad Pharma City controversy was particularly egregious: non-consenting farmers found their names erased from the Dharani portal overnight and replaced with the name of the Telangana State Industrial and Infrastructure Corporation (TSIIC). Officials dismissed this as "a technical error that occurred in the backend process" — an explanation that the authors present as emblematic of technocratic irresponsibility.

By 2022, more than 300,000 complaints were pending before the Revenue Department. The *Kisan Mitra* helpline data from Adilabad (February 2018 to May 2019) showed that among 386 LRUP-related complaints, 37% concerned non-receipt of PPBs, 20% involved wrong area measurements or missing survey numbers, and 12% were about succession disputes.

### 6.2 Why the Errors Persisted: Five Structural Reasons

The paper identifies five interrelated structural reasons for the failure:

**a. The Conclusive vs. Presumptive Title Problem**

LRUP was neither a genuinely conclusive nor a fully presumptive titling exercise — it was, as the authors put it, "a curious third way." No fresh cadastral resurvey was conducted (only 3.6% of Telangana's area was resurveyed by April 2020), yet the exercise went well beyond simple presumptive updating. New titles were largely derivatives of old, error-ridden presumptive titles. A senior revenue official explained the bureaucratic resistance to resurvey: "A cadastral survey in the whole State is time-consuming. It would also lead to the opening of a can of worms, which is totally uncalled for if the farmers and their neighbours are satisfied with the existing situation."

The government's own 2022 sub-committee recommendation for a fresh digital field survey effectively "quashed the government's own earlier claims that the database had become 'authentic.'"

**b. The Political Urgency Problem**

The LRUP was "hurriedly designed in 2017 to ensure a time-bound introduction of the RBS." The 3-month timeline (extended by 3 more months) was wholly inadequate for verifying over 10,000 villages. Revenue functionaries worked under extreme pressure — "patwaris collected data during the day, performed data entry during the night, slept in the office and woke up next morning to go straight to a *gram sabha*." An expert on land rights noted that earlier pilot efforts took six months for just eight villages; the LRUP gave each village only 9–10 days. Revenue officials told the authors: "the reality is that even if we got 10 months per village, instead of 10 days, ground-level verification could not have been satisfactorily completed."

The Revenue Department was severely understaffed: in the Talamadagu *mandal*, there were 28 revenue villages but only 7 *patwaris*; Adilabad district had 18 *mandals* but only 8–9 surveyors.

**c. The Sethwar Problem**

The *sethwar* of 1932–34 was used as the benchmark document in the Dharani portal, which was programmed to reject any data entry where the area under new survey numbers did not exactly match corresponding areas in the *sethwar*. "Even if the area was more or less by just one *gunta* than in the *sethwar*, the portal did not accept the data." This was a profound technocratic error: the *sethwar* itself was an 85-year-old colonial document that bore little correspondence to ground reality after decades of transactions, partitions, manipulations, and encroachments. Cases that couldn't be reconciled were either resolved through the *tehsildar*'s discretionary powers — itself a source of corruption — or dumped into Part B. An estimated 200,000 cases were classified into Part B "in a hurry, just so that the tasks in the village could be completed on time."

**d. The Exclusion of Tenants**

Perhaps the most politically consequential outcome of the LRUP was the deliberate, legally codified exclusion of tenants from land records. The Telangana government amended the 1971 AP Record of Rights Act in 2020 to remove the requirement to record tenants' interests in the PPB. The Dharani portal was programmed so that the owner and cultivator columns had to carry the same name — effectively erasing tenancy from the official record.

The Chief Minister was quoted as saying: "Rythu Bandhu is meant for farmers. It is not tenant Bandhu." And: "the government does not have details of tenant farmers. Nowhere in the official records have the details of tenant farmers been mentioned … The so-called tenant farmers will not have any right to the land."

According to the RSV's assessment, one-third of RBS payments went to absentee landlords. A survey by the authors in Talamadagu village (May 2019) found not a single absentee landlord sharing RBS benefits with tenants, and about 60% of tenants reported that lease rates had risen after RBS. Tenants were denied bank credit, agricultural extension services, crop insurance, and benefits under PM Kisan — violating the Land Licensed Cultivators Act of 2011. As a *tehsildar* in Adilabad remarked, with exasperation: "Our Chief Minister does not want tenancy in Telangana!"

**e. Subversion by Local Actors and Corruption**

Corruption was rampant throughout. A *tehsildar* and VRO in Ranga Reddy were arrested in July 2019 for demanding bribes to correct land records. Data entry was entrusted to contract workers with "neither stake in nor commitment to their tasks." The abolition of the *patwari* post in 2020 — presented as an anti-corruption measure — paradoxically eliminated the only official with local knowledge of each village's land situation, forcing district collectors to rely on the very same *patwaris* and *tehsildars* the system was supposed to bypass. A leader of the All India Kisan Sabha, A. Prasad Rao, told the authors: "In the Kisan Sabha, we have recorded numerous reports of corruption at the ground level even for discharging legitimate duties."

The *Dharani* portal itself became a site of opacity rather than transparency — with no mechanisms for personal interaction, no accountability for complaint resolution, and no legal immunity for collectors who acted on complaints. One district collector sarcastically described themselves as having been "reduced to '*Dharani* Collectors.'"

### 6.3 Human and Political Consequences

The paper documents harrowing human consequences. Farmers attempted suicide; a female MRO in Abdullahpurmet was killed by a landowner over a land dispute; revenue officers received death threats; petrol was thrown at officers in Karimnagar. The Telangana High Court in 2021 chastised the government for non-responsiveness. A ministerial sub-committee eventually acknowledged 20 types of problems with LRUP and Dharani, including software restrictions that prevented more than one person from buying or selling land — a basic functional failure.

## VII. Concluding Argument

The conclusion brings together the paper's multiple threads into a coherent political economy argument. The LRUP illustrates "the nature of political economy around land" that "can frustrate attempts by the neoliberal state to dislocate land from the bundle of claims and counterclaims that have survived and accumulated over centuries and 'fix' conclusive land titles."

The fundamental problem is that the neoliberal state "envisages land records modernisation as an erasure of history, in disregard of egalitarianism in land distribution and under the assumption that the 'illegibility' of records is a technical problem of administrative dysfunction." Technocracy, aided by poorly designed digital portals, does not resolve these contradictions — it superimposes new, unaccountable systems of power on top of old ones, closing off democratic avenues of redress and insulating the state from accountability.

The complete exclusion of tenants is the paper's sharpest illustration: rather than making land relations more transparent and equitable, the LRUP and Dharani rendered a large and vulnerable agrarian class — those who actually cultivate the land — entirely invisible in official records, denying them credit, welfare entitlements, and legal recognition.

## VIII. Methodological Assessment

The paper uses a mixed-methods approach combining: published literature and official reports; secondary data including DILRMP progress data and Comptroller and Auditor General reports; primary fieldwork in Adilabad district (2019); semi-structured interviews with revenue officials at all levels of the administrative hierarchy; and analysis of complaints data from the *Kisan Mitra* (Kisan Mitra) helpline operated by the Rythu Swarajya Vedika (RSV), covering 475 complaints (February 2018–May 2019) and 510 complaints (November 2020–August 2022).

The multi-level interview methodology — from the Chief Commissioner of Land Administration down to *patwaris* and Revenue Inspectors — is a significant strength, providing both a state-level perspective and a granular view of implementation on the ground. The helpline data is used creatively as a structured form of evidence about the range and frequency of grievances, though the authors appropriately caution that it is not exhaustive.

One limitation the authors do not fully acknowledge is the potential sampling bias in the helpline data — those who call a helpline are likely to be more literate, connected, and aggrieved than the average affected farmer. The most dispossessed — landless labourers, forest dwellers, women — may be systematically underrepresented in the complaint data.

The paper also relies substantially on interviews with revenue officials, who may have incentives to understate their own role in failures or to externalise blame onto political superiors. The authors are aware of this and use multiple, corroborating sources, but a more explicit reflexive discussion of positionality and interview limitations would have strengthened the methodology section.

## IX. Engagement with Literature

The paper engages productively with a broad literature spanning:

- Agrarian political economy (Marx, Polanyi, Byres, Levien)
- State theory and legibility (Scott 1998)
- Land titling debates (Atwood 1990, Miceli & Kieyah 2003, de Soto 2000, Deininger & Feder 2013)
- Indian land records history (Wadhwa 2002, Appu 1996, Saxena 2005, Nayak 2020)
- Technocracy (Cole 2022)
- Karnataka's Bhoomi (Benjamin & Raman 2011, De 2016)
- Telangana's agrarian history (Sundarayya 1972, Khusro 1958, Bobbili & Manohar 1999/2001)

The engagement with Benjamin and Raman (2011) on Bhoomi is particularly productive, as it provides a directly comparable Indian case. The critical engagement with the EoDB framework and the World Bank's promotion of land titling is well-evidenced and analytically important.

The paper could have engaged more with feminist political economy and gender dimensions of land — the exclusion of women from land records and the gendered dimensions of PPB distribution are mentioned only tangentially. Given TISS's rich tradition of scholarship on gender and land (including work by scholars like Bina Agarwal), this is a notable gap.

## X. Strengths

1. **Theoretical depth**: The paper successfully integrates macro-level political economy theory (neoliberalism, technocracy, colonial legacies) with granular empirical analysis of scheme implementation.

2. **Historical richness**: Section 4 on Telangana's colonial legacy is an outstanding piece of historical reconstruction, showing how accumulated contradictions from the Nizam period through post-independence reforms shaped the intractable illegibility that the LRUP inherited.

3. **Policy relevance**: The paper makes a direct and important intervention in live policy debates around India's DILRMP, the Dharani portal, and direct benefit transfer schemes like PM-KISAN and RBS.

4. **Multi-level empirical grounding**: The combination of state-level data, district-level fieldwork, interview evidence, and helpline data provides a compelling and multi-perspectival account.

5. **The tenant exclusion finding**: The documentation of the systematic exclusion of tenants — both as a design choice and as a legal-structural outcome — is a major empirical and normative contribution.

6. **Conceptual innovation**: The "illegibly legible" framing is not merely a clever title but a genuine conceptual contribution — it captures how digital legibility can mask, rather than resolve, political illegibility.

---

## XI. Limitations and Critiques

1. **Gender gap**: As noted, the paper is largely silent on the gendered dimensions of land records and the LRUP. Women's land rights, the recording (or non-recording) of women's names in PPBs, and the gendered impacts of tenant exclusion deserve more attention.

2. **Comparison with other states**: The paper briefly mentions Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, and Kerala as states using more advanced technologies like CORS for resurvey, but does not systematically compare these cases. A comparative analysis — even indicative — would have strengthened the generalisability of the findings.

3. **Agency of subaltern groups**: The paper focuses primarily on the failures of the state and the subversions by landlords and revenue officials. The agency of tenants, tribals, and small farmers in resisting, navigating, or contesting the LRUP receives less systematic attention.

4. **The Dharani portal's technical architecture**: While the paper critiques the Dharani portal's political design, it does not engage in depth with its technical architecture — who designed it, what private contractors were involved, what procurement processes were followed. This would be relevant to critiques of privatisation and public-private partnerships in land administration.

5. **Normative framework**: The paper's normative position — that land records modernisation should be embedded in agrarian egalitarianism and land reform — is made explicit but not fully theorised. What would an alternative, egalitarian model of land records modernisation actually look like in the Indian context? Kerala's experience, briefly cited, could have been explored more.

---

## XII. Broader Implications

The paper's implications extend well beyond Telangana. Its core insight — that technocratic solutions to land governance problems fail when they are disembedded from the political economy of land relations — has direct relevance to:

- The ongoing national DILRMP and its ambition to achieve "conclusive titling" across India
- The PM-KISAN scheme, which similarly ties welfare transfers to land records
- Debates about blockchain-based land registries, which are being promoted by institutions like ORF (Bal 2017) without adequate engagement with political economy
- The broader global debate on land titling and the World Bank's land governance agenda
- Corporate land acquisition facilitated through "clean" digital land databases

The paper also makes a strong case — though it does not frame it explicitly in these terms — for **participatory approaches** to land records modernisation: community-based verification, integration of informal tenure recognition, and democratic accountability mechanisms, rather than top-down technocratic portals.

---

## XIII. Conclusion

"Illegibly Legible" is a rigorous, politically engaged, and empirically rich contribution to the literature on land governance, agrarian political economy, and the politics of digital state formation in India. It successfully demonstrates that land records modernisation, when driven by neoliberal welfare-targeting imperatives rather than agrarian reform, reproduces and deepens existing inequalities rather than resolving them. The title's irony — land made "legible" by the state that remains "illegible" in its actual social relations — encapsulates a broader truth about the limits of technocratic governance in deeply unequal agrarian societies.

The paper is essential reading not only for scholars of agrarian change and land governance in South Asia, but for anyone engaged with the politics of digital public infrastructure, welfare state design, and the role of the postcolonial state in mediating historical property regimes.

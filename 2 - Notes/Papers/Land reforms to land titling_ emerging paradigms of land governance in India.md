# Claude Primer
## Land Reforms to Land Titling: Emerging Paradigms of Land Governance in India*
### Pradeep Nayak | SAGE Publications / Indian Institute of Advanced Study, Shimla | 2021

---

## Prefatory Note: The Animating Provocation

On 21 August 2008, the top civil servant of the Department of Land Resources of the Government of India announced that India was taking "a historic step to move from the present system of presumptive property titles to the system of the so-called clear property titles known as the 'Torrens system', which is prevalent in countries such as Australia, Canada, Malaysia, New Zealand, Singapore, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, the United States." This announcement — buried in government documents and barely noticed in the political sphere — is the starting point of Pradeep Nayak's *Land Reforms to Land Titling*. To Nayak, a senior IAS officer who spent his fellowship years at the Indian Institute of Advanced Study, Shimla, this sentence represents nothing less than a quiet revolution: the Indian state's abandonment of a seventy-year old redistributive land reform agenda in favour of a market-oriented, techno-managerial land titling regime — and it is doing so without any political debate, democratic deliberation, or public scrutiny.

The book's central argument is encapsulated in the Preface with characteristic precision: "The state in India has made a 'historic decision' to switch over from the presumptive nature of land records and ownership to that of a guaranteed title to land or conclusive land title regime by ushering the 'land liberalization' regime." This shift, Nayak insists, "is not being done through the political process of open debates and discussion, and rather in an apolitical way." Drawing on Rob Jenkins's concept, he identifies the land titling initiative as a paradigmatic case of *reform by stealth* — "cloaking change in the guise of continuity" — in which the governing elites pursue a fundamentally transformative policy by "finding innovative ways" that bypass political resistance. "We hardly find any political debates or discussions on the pros and cons of adopting the state guaranteed title to land and replacing the presumptive nature of land property rights in land."

What makes the book so distinctive — and so valuable for students of agrarian studies, land policy, and political economy in India — is precisely its refusal to treat land titling as a merely administrative or technical question. The study, Nayak writes, "seeks to interrogate how the neoliberal land policy of secure individual property rights in land in the form of conclusive title to land would address the land questions in India." The object is not to evaluate the programme on its own terms but to locate it within the larger history of property relations, the sociology of land tenure, and the political economy of neoliberal development. Every chapter of the book deepens this inquiry from a different angle.

## Introduction: The Political Economy of the Paradigm Shift

The Introduction does the difficult work of establishing, simultaneously, the historical significance of the shift and the conceptual vocabulary needed to analyse it. Two terms recur throughout the book and must be distinguished at the outset.

The **presumptive** land title system, inherited from the British colonial administration, holds that entries in the Record of Rights (RoR) — known variously as *patta*, *khatauni*, *pahanipatrika*, or *khatian* — "are presumed to be correct until contrary is proved." The RoR "does not create or extinguish title but has presumptive value which may be proved otherwise wrong." The **conclusive** or **guaranteed title** system, modelled on the Torrens system first enacted in South Australia in 1858, proposes that the state, after a thorough process of adjudication, issues an *indefeasible* certificate of title backed by a state indemnity fund. The National Land Records Modernization Programme (NLRMP, renamed DILRMP in 2016) seeks to achieve this transition through four cardinal principles: a single-window mechanism for land records, the *mirror principle* (cadastral records mirror ground reality), the *curtain principle* (no need to trace history of prior transactions), and *title insurance* (the state indemnifies title holders against losses). The programme is, as the Union government itself claims, "a technology driven and complex project" that has been "the biggest e-governance programme in the world."

The political economy context is set through the lens of Atul Kohli's analysis. Three structural constraints limited post-colonial India's capacity to implement redistributive land reforms: the dominance of a landed upper-caste colonial bureaucracy lacking administrative will; constitutional limitations on the Union government's jurisdiction over state-level land matters combined with protection for private property; and the Congress party's political dependence on landholding local notables. The result was what Kohli called "one of continuous political failure" — the inability to "institutionalise elements of lower class interests within the state." The Congress party's failure to mobilize landless farmers and tenants to pressure for reform meant that "the influential landed castes manipulated the land reform to their advantage and the result was a modest land reform policy."

Nayak traces the abandonment of redistributive land reform through successive Five-Year Plans with fine-grained attention. The Tenth Five-Year Plan (2002–2007) had openly admitted that "land reforms seem to have been relegated to the background in the 1990s," while the Twelfth Five-Year Plan (2012–2017) "clearly delinked the expediency of updating land records with land reforms and set the path forward for formalization and individualization of property rights in land." This Plan declared that "unambiguously recorded land rights, firm in law, are the foundation for investments in higher farm productivity" — a rhetorical framing that converts a social justice instrument into a market efficiency instrument. The Plan further talked about launching the NLRMP "to move decisively" towards a land title regime "from the existing presumptive nature of land records." Meanwhile, D. Bandyopadhyay, one of India's leading experts on land reform, was warning that "land reform has become a forgotten agenda of the Indian state. Marketers have dominated all segments of governance… They were finding the existing land reform laws obnoxious to free play of capital in the land/lease market."

In 2018, the NITI Aayog's document *Strategy for New India@75* called for "ensuring that land markets function smoothly, through efficient allocation of land across uses" and "provision of secure property rights and titles, and clear and consistent regulations around the operations" — language that would have been unthinkable in the First Five-Year Plan era. The trajectory of the Indian state from a socialist redistributive framework to a market-enabling framework on land is, as Nayak demonstrates, fully legible from official documents alone.

## Chapter 1: The Contemporary Discourse on Secure Property Rights in Land — A Critical Survey

This is the theoretical engine of the book, spanning neoclassical economics, legal philosophy, institutional theory, the advocacy of Hernando de Soto, the mechanics of the Torrens system, and the World Bank's land policy — all subjected to a critical but fair-minded reading.

Nayak opens with the foundational observation from C. B. Macpherson that "the concept of property changes over time in the context of larger developments in the political economy and technology," and that "the expectations of the propertied classes shape the meaning, purpose and role of the property and give rise to the contestations over its role in the society from time to time." The neoliberal moment has produced its own conception of property: the secure, individual, exclusive, transferable private holding that minimizes transaction costs and maximizes market efficiency. The key intellectual sources of this conception are identified and interrogated.

**Demsetz's thesis** holds that property rights emerge and change "in response to the desires of the interacting persons for adjustment to new benefit-cost possibilities" — a dynamic, evolutionary account in which private property develops when its social benefits exceed social costs. Three distinctive benefits of property are enumerated: it creates superior incentives for resource development; eliminates rent dissipation associated with open-access regimes; and reduces the number of parties who must agree on measures to control spillover effects. Richard Posner extends this logic: individual ownership through *parcelization* is the lowest-cost method of securing rights, freeing the individual owner from the "burden of monitoring" associated with group ownership. Robert Ellickson adds the observation that "the genius of individual ownership" is done by "parcelization of land" which is "a relatively low cost method of securing individual land rights for avoiding losses." Nayak notes the critical implication: "for the advocates of individualization of property rights, the distributive question of property is uninteresting from the so-called efficiency point of view."

The discussion of **Hohfeld and Honoré** is one of the chapter's most intellectually rich passages. Nayak explains that Wesley Hohfeld (1913) analysed property not as a thing but as "a fundamental legal relation between people," comprising rights, privileges, powers, and immunities — eight basic legal categories — producing the **"bundle of rights"** theory. A. M. Honoré's eleven incidents of ownership — including the right to possess exclusively, use, manage, receive income, use capital, security against expropriation, transmissibility, absence of term, prohibition of harmful use, liability to execution, and residuary character — flesh out this bundle concretely. The importance of this framework for Nayak's argument about India will become clear: India's land tenure is precisely such a *bundle*, distributed across multiple parties (owners, tenants, sharecroppers, widows, tribals, pastoralists) over the same parcel. Any conclusive titling that vests rights in a *single registered holder* inevitably severs this bundle, dispossessing secondary interest holders entirely.

The treatment of **Hernando de Soto** is the chapter's most extensive and critically charged section. De Soto's *The Mystery of Capital* (2000) argues that capitalism fails in developing countries because the poor hold land *informally*, without title, meaning these assets constitute "dead capital" — incapable of being leveraged as credit collateral. De Soto writes that "the migrants do not break the law rather the law breaks them." Their extra-legal occupation is "a mixture of law-legal, conventions, customs and community protection." The solution is formalization: confer formal property rights, unlock the dead capital, release credit, and spur economic development.

Nayak's critique is systematic and well-sourced. Alan Gilbert accuses de Soto of "generating a myth about capitalism and economic growth by prescribing for legalization of the squatters and providing them credit access." Celeste Nyamu Musembi finds that in sub-Saharan Africa, "land titling programmes have failed to establish the link between formal land title and access to credit for small holders." Jan Breman, from his long years studying South Gujarat, observes that de Soto's ideas "are based on preconceived notion rather than historical facts." Rajan and Zingales demonstrate that giving land titles to encroachers of Dharavi — Mumbai's largest slum — would create infinite future demands for such legalization elsewhere, and that "in a slum like Dharavi in Mumbai, where the local community and land mafia enforces property rights, it is unclear how a title from a remote government would enforce the property collateral." Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo observe that "the main problem of lending to the poor is gathering information on their solvency and status" — a problem no land title certificate resolves.

More pointedly: de Soto's formula "has been integral to India's land reform policy since the implementation of the First Five-Year Plan. All state governments in India, guided by political compulsion, confer land rights to landless and homestead-less persons in target-driven mode in rural areas." But no evidence has emerged that such formalization has led to the growth of land market. "Formalization of property rights has opened up the opportunities for alienation of property of the poor landholders to international financial corporates or wealthier groups."

The **Torrens system** is presented through its pivotal points (following William C. Niblack): the registrar's quasi-judicial power to issue indefeasible certificates of title; the curtain principle (no need to trace prior transaction history); the indemnity fund for displaced title holders; and the mirror principle (the register mirrors ground reality). But Nayak immediately identifies the system's structural contradictions. The mirror principle is defeated by **overriding interests** — rights enforceable against registered title holders in rem without being recorded in the register, such as leases, easements, and adverse possession rights. The state guarantee of indefeasibility is demonstrably incomplete: "if 'A' is the true legal owner but 'B' manages to get a land title by resorting to forgery and suppression of facts and sells to 'C' who is now declared as a certified and valid title-holder of the property" — the original owner loses the land and can only claim compensation from the indemnity fund. In England itself, where the Land Registration Act, 2002 first came into force, courts have consistently upheld common-law precedent favouring original title holders over registered fraudulent acquirers — making the formalistic "title by registration," in practice, no more than a "mythical fantasy."

The **World Bank's land policy** is subjected to sustained political critique. The Bank's stated logic — that "secure individualized land property provides benefits of high degree of transfer of rights and the ease in use of land as collateral and productivity increases" — is presented as ideology dressed as technical expertise. The Bank's own scholars (Deininger and Feder) concede that "evidence of improved access of credit due to formalization of land is scant" and that "the formalization of land should not be viewed as panacea." The Bank "pragmatically agrees that land titling is not a panacea for achieving a wide variety of divergent objectives simultaneously." Yet its policy advocacy continues unabated. Nayak identifies the Bank's real agenda: "Its land policy reflects its ideological-political support for neoliberal governance, namely, privatization, globalization and deregulation of a state's economy." The so-called "pro-poor land policy" coincides with what Saturnino Borras Jr. identifies as "efficient land administration and management, land titling and cadastres" — a technical gloss on what is fundamentally a political project of land commodification. "The main political, ideological and theoretical factors behind the land titling policy of the Bank is to challenge the legitimacy and inability of the redistributive agenda of land reforms in developing countries as politically unviable, populist and misconceived policy."

The chapter concludes by marshalling **commons theory** against the neoliberal property rights consensus. Garret Hardin's "tragedy of the commons" — the prediction that open-access regimes inevitably lead to resource destruction — is shown to have been systematically exploited to justify privatization. Elinor Ostrom's lifetime of empirical research counters this: communities can and do manage common resources sustainably through institutional arrangements that assign access, withdrawal, management, exclusion, and alienation rights collectively. The five commons rights categories (access, withdrawal, management, exclusion, alienation) are contrasted with the four classical property regimes (open access, state property, private property, commons property) to show that Indian land tenure systems are *inherently mixed*, not cleanly categorizable, and that the drive to individualize and parcelization these systems is a political imposition, not an institutional improvement.

## Chapter 2: Land Titling Experience in Developing Countries — The Empirical Demolition

Chapter 2 is the book's empirical stronghold, systematically tracking the failure of land titling programmes across Asia, Latin America, Africa, and China, against the backdrop of what Nayak identifies as the "new paradigm of land reforms" promoted by multilateral donor agencies — a paradigm that replaces the state-led redistributive agenda with "privatization and systematic individualization," "land titling, registration, decentralized administration," and "promotion of land rental, easing of restrictions on land transfers and opening for a vibrant land market."

**In Thailand**, one of the World Bank's largest funded land titling programmes, Leonard and Ayutthaya find that the programme "benefited only the urban-based affluent groups," "excluded the poor sections in the forest areas," "led to significant increase in land prices and growth of speculative real estate entrepreneurs," and "overlooked the real problems of the small farmers, that is, how to secure their hold over the landed property."

**In the Philippines and Indonesia**, studies reveal that "farmers were not interested to use their land titles for securing loans as they were apprehensive of being defaulters." In Indonesia, people "preferred to preserve the land titles in cupboard than using land as collateral due to their apprehension of not getting back the land." The Bank's programme in Bandung city "did not significantly contributed to consolidation of housing programme" and "did not target the informal landholders there." In **Cambodia**, "systematic property rights and large-scale land concessions have proceeded in a parallel process" — formalization and land grabbing advancing simultaneously. In **Papua New Guinea**, the government's attempt to "modernise" the Wola tribal land tenure system met with stiff resistance; Paul Sillitoe warns, "Ill-informed discussion and land reform can quickly inflame people who sense a threat to their identity and security."

**In Latin America**, Nicaragua offers the paradigmatic lesson. Rikke Broegaard finds that despite formal titling, "the high cost and cumbersome process of ascertaining and granting land title, lack of trust on the governmental machineries, persistence of information asymmetry, lack of awareness of the legalities in land tenure system and lack of access to the land registry office had defeated the very purpose of the land title system." People continue to "take up informal measures such as planting of trees, and water and soil and conservation measures to gain legitimacy in the perception of their neighbourhood community" — land security based on social networks and customary recognition rather than formal title. In **Peru** — Hernando de Soto's own country — the formal titling process "is prohibitively costly, delaying and riddled with corruption" and "communities continue to rely on the informal methods of land tenure security such as mutual faith, verbal agreements, social norms, mutual respect and arbitration conducted by local political leaders." In **Honduras**, land titling "modernised the sources which can be used to contest rights in land" and "had created new conflicts rather than solving the existing ones." In **Brazil's Amazon frontier**, the drive to prove "beneficial use" of land — the constitutional precondition for resisting redistribution — compelled large landholders to engage in massive deforestation. The result is a government caught in a "formidable challenge on how to reconcile the 'competing demands' for land reform and secure property rights in land."

The **African case studies** are the richest and most theoretically elaborated. Catherine Boone's political-sociological study finds that "Africa is changing from a continent with open land frontiers into one of intensifying land pressure and scarcity," and that "to change the rules of tenure means to change the existing relationships between communities with state and between communities." Archie Mafeje makes the fundamental structural point: "The concept of 'ownership' is alien to African customary law. The property in land is held by and transmitted through lineage system or unilinear descent group. The collective rights in land are jealously guarded by the community as it gives a sense of security to the community members." The consequence: "Such social organization has, therefore, frustrated the expectations of the advocates of the neoliberal property rights." In Cameroon, Ghana, Mali, Niger, and Madagascar, titling programmes consistently benefited social elites — "bureaucrats, politicians, entrepreneurs and the indigenous leaders" — and "compounded the existing colonial era land relations." In Madagascar's Lac Alaotra rice-growing region, "nearly 47 per cent of land remained untitled and the private benefits of land titling was lower than the cost of executing land titling efforts." The joint World Bank–University of Wisconsin Madison study found "no significant correlation among land titling, tenure security and credit market in Kenya, Somalia and Senegal." Daniel Bromley's scathing judgment is quoted approvingly: "Formalization erodes and displaces existing social networks and arrangements that do offer security."

The **gender dimension** runs through the chapter as a structural indictment. IFAD's study finds that land titling programmes in Bolivia and Nicaragua resulted in "concentration of landownership in the hands of men" because "normative and practical issues determine the feasibility of gender sensitive laws." In Mexico, "the land title programme had vested the land rights to the head of the family, usually a male member. The individualization of land rights of men is based on the assumption that women were not the agriculturists." In Ghana, "traditionally the women are the properties of her husband and fathers. If a widow leaves her husband's house or if she remarries to another person, she loses all her user and access rights to land." In Kenya's Maasai communities, "women widows received less than the average parcel of land among the communities, because of the strong patrilineal community norm prevailed over the Islamic inheritance rule." Susana Lastaria-Cornhiel's summary verdict: "The wealth and power associated with land rights are accruing to those able to play the market game."

The **China case study** is theoretically decisive. China has achieved spectacular economic development *without* private individual land titles — through the Household Responsibility System of collective ownership with use rights, and a "double track policy" of leasing urban land for non-agricultural use while retaining public ownership. Meg Rithmire's empirical study shows that "China managed to allow the emergence of a market for farmland in the absence of private ownership and with a set of institutions firmly rooted in its socialist past" — calling it "an interesting deviant case in international and historical comparison." The lesson is unambiguous: the World Bank's insistence that individual land titling is the precondition for economic growth is ideologically motivated, not empirically grounded.

The chapter's conclusion is categorical: "No empirical study by independent scholars has found positive evidence that land titling programmes have provided the so-called land access to the poor and vulnerable sections of society." Sjaastad and Cousins deliver the most damning epitaph: land titling in the developing world constitutes "risky social experiments that gamble with the livelihood of the poor."

## Chapter 3: Property Rights in Land in India — Continuities and Changes

This chapter is the book's most historically rich, tracing property rights from Mughal administration through the layered complexities of British colonial settlement to post-Independence India. The epigraph from the nineteenth-century British civil servant Baden Powell — that the student of Indian land tenures "has probably a vague idea that he is about to enter on a terra incognita or to plunge into some mysterious and unintelligible darkness" — sets the tone perfectly.

The argument begins with British **cartography as an instrument of empire**. Mathew Edney's landmark study demonstrates that "the geographical unity of India is, in short, a creation of British mapping of their empire," converting a diverse, overlapping political landscape into a legible, taxable, administrable entity. The Great Trigonometric Survey (GTS) and revenue surveys were simultaneously epistemological and political projects. Mapping was followed by classification — the establishment of two "master terms," *proprietor* (landlord) and *tenant*, as the grid through which all agrarian relations would henceforth be understood. But as Bhattacharya observes, the actual task of classifying "individual rights in land in villages" was enormously challenging: identifying "layers of cultivators and tenancies such as occupancy, non-occupancy and tenants at will" proved "very challenging due to their lack of clarity among the colonial bureaucracy and accuracy of information collected from people."

Nayak systematically demolishes the colonial mythology of *creating* private property in a propertyless India. Philip Woodruff claimed that "the English at a stroke conferred upon each peasant what seemed a priceless gift. He became the owner of his holding. We had turned collective into individual ownership." Nayak counters with multiple British civil servants and historians: Edward Stanley Robertson noted that "almost all property is held by family groups, not by individuals." Another British official observed that "prior to British rule, the English conception of property in land as a transferable marketable commodity absolutely owned and passing from hand to hand, like any chattel, did not exist." Dharma Kumar is definitive: "There was nothing called absolute private ownership in India during the pre-British period. There was hardly any distinction between the private and public property." The British did not introduce private property to a void; they superimposed an alien conception of *exclusive individual ownership* over a complex social web of *interests* — rights of use, enjoyment, passage, grazing, inheritance, and common access — that had no clean landlord–tenant correspondence. The result was not synthesis but "distortions" that were "alterations in the old structure of the greatest consequences."

The three **revenue settlement systems** are compared in depth. The *Permanent Settlement* of Bengal (1793), based on Cornwallis's reading of Lockean liberalism — "the true function of the administration of justice was to secure private property rights" — conferred hereditary revenue-collecting rights on zamindars, making them propertied intermediaries between the colonial state and actual cultivators. Eric Stokes captures the utilitarian ideological underpinning: the introduction of "English law, particularly a modern law establishing private property rights in land" was explicitly used "as a weapon to transform Indian society by breaking up or modifying the customary and communal land tenures." The *Ryotwari* system of Madras and Bombay, by contrast, settled directly with the cultivating peasant, establishing individual use rights without intermediaries. The *Mahalwari* system of the Punjab and Northwest Provinces recognized village community revenue responsibility, generating a form of corporate proprietary claim.

The chapter's most important analytical contribution concerns the *deliberate* colonial decision to maintain land records in **presumptive** rather than conclusive form. Rothermund explains: "a record of rights with presumptive proof was flexible, it adapted itself to the facts and it created facts without being restricted to hard and fast rules which would only be of disadvantage when dealing with exceptional cases." Courts could disregard presumptive records when they possessed more conclusive evidence. The colonial authorities were fully aware that proposing *conclusive* records would be impracticable given the layers of customary complexity, the unreliability of field-level revenue officials, and the prevalence of oral traditions. Elisabeth Whitcombe's study of North Indian district records reveals systematic inaccuracies: "For a start, the myriad of cultivated patches, crisis-crossed by strips of waste, which made up a pargana frustrated attempts at accurate measurement even on the relatively small scale adopted by the professional survey." The field boundaries "fluctuated from season to season according to fallowing practices." Records were further corrupted by patwaris "subverting the impractical business of collection of detailed and accurate data" and landholders "spurning the role of the amins to collect data on their land tenure details for their extractive practices."

The colonial understanding of the agrarian system is perhaps best captured in a quote from Robert Frykenberg: "Whatever the terminology, attempts to classify and differentiate between myriad varieties of zamindars, Jagirdars, and ryots, whether great or small, become meaningful only if the observer recognises that all kinds of holdings and rights were intricately linked to definite socio-ceremonial and communal as well as economic and political roles. When seen in this perspective, the tenant and landholder look very much the same… Land control and land bondage were mutually shaded and overlapping conditions."

Rothermund's verdict on the entire colonial land records enterprise is quoted in full, as it bears directly on the contemporary policy debate: "A realistic agrarian policy in India is almost impossible in view of this lack of an accurate record of rights not to speak of a record which would distinguish between ownership and tenure so as to provide information about the actual units of cultivation. The land reform measures of independent India were undertaken without any reference to the land revenue system which was allowed to decay further until some states actually abolished land revenue."

The **Odisha case studies** illustrate the complexity that any titling system must navigate. Dalziel's 1934 Final Report on the Revision Settlement of Orissa catalogues revenue-free proprietors (*lakhiraj bahledars* — debottar, pirottar, brahmottar grant holders), sub-proprietors, *tanki bahledars*, other sub-proprietors, tenure holders, *miadi* and *maurasi sarbarakars*, and privileged and ordinary tenure holders. In Balasore district, there were four categories of under-tenants alone. In some princely states of Odisha, the state owned the land but the actual cultivator held the right of proprietorship; tribal lands were inalienable; revenue collection was through village heads on limited-period lease without hereditary rights. Post-Independence India has *added* further categories: ceiling beneficiaries, forest rights holders, homestead rights holders, sharecroppers under statutory protection. A six-category taxonomy for any given parcel of Odisha land is not unusual.

Banerjee and Iyer's comparative study provides the chapter's empirical anchor: districts that were under ryotwari systems (non-landlord areas) show "sustained differences in economic outcomes" compared to formerly zamindari (Permanent Settlement) areas — "lower agricultural investments and productivity in the post-Independence period" in landlord areas, with a "conflictual environment" created by historical landed power relations that continues to shape economic and human development outcomes. The colonial property institutions leave a shadow that extends two centuries forward.

## Chapter 4: Land Titling and the Emerging Political Economy of Land in India

Chapter 4 grounds the book's theoretical arguments in the concrete agrarian and urban realities of post-liberalization India. It is an empirically dense chapter, drawing on NSSO data, five-year plan documents, the critical report of the *Committee on State Agrarian Relations and the Unfinished Task in Land Reforms* (2009), and field studies from multiple regions and states.

P. C. Joshi's formulation sets the analytical frame: "the study of agrarian structure is primarily the study of the groups connected with land." The agrarian social structure in India is "based on land or property structure, namely who owns land and who does not." A hiatus exists, Nayak observes, "in the minds of the political elites on actual conditions prevailing on the ground" — a hiatus that is dangerously exploited by techno-managerial land titling discourse.

Several overlapping processes in the contemporary political economy of land are identified. The **failure of land reform in Bihar**, despite the Land Reforms Commission Report of 2008, is cited as "a concrete indication of the class nature of land relations and dominance of propertied classes in the state machinery and political process." The **rise of intermediate caste landlordism** — where green revolution beneficiaries from intermediate castes have accumulated land as a financial asset and now deploy non-cultivating absentee landlordism — is documented through Jan Breman's studies in Gujarat (new class of absentee landlords "managing their lands by tenancy and in leisurely fashion," while "agricultural labourers are also turning away from agriculture"), studies from Andhra Pradesh (the "rich peasant castes" establishing "an interlinkage with moneylenders–merchants and input agents"), and Tamil Nadu (nearly half the village population now "non-cultivating and non-resident landowners").

The chapter's most important empirical data concern India's **land use dynamics**. Per capita land resource declined from 0.89 hectare in 1995 to 0.27 hectare in 2007–2008. Barren and uncultivated land has been drastically reduced from 40.7 per cent in 1950–51 to 22.6 per cent in 2007–08. The number of towns increased from 5,161 in 2001 to 7,935 in 2011. "It is projected that 50 per cent population would live in urban areas by 2050." These figures set the material context for the demand for a land market: the pressure on land is real, the urbanization drive is intense, and capital needs legible property rights to function. But Nayak's point is that these pressures *cannot be resolved* by titling alone — and that titling, in the absence of robust redistributive protection, will benefit the property-holding classes at the expense of the landless.

The evolution of India's **five-year plan approach to land** is traced with exemplary care. The Eighth Five-Year Plan (1992–1997), formulated in the backdrop of new economic policy, "admitted that the scope for redistributive policies in land reforms is now limited" and shifted stress to "recording the rights of tenants and sharecroppers." The Ninth Plan (1997–2002) talked about "liberalizing tenancy restrictions within the ceiling limits" which would "activate the land market" and "enhance the poor people's access to land." The Tenth Plan admitted that "land reforms seem to have been relegated to the background in the 1990s" and noted that "ceiling on landholding is not a credible policy any more." The Twelfth Plan admitted that "the equitable distribution of land as envisaged in the ceiling laws has failed in all the states" because it would require "massive mobilization of poor and deepening of democratic governance."

The cumulative picture from plan documents reveals what Nayak identifies as a "new discourse on land reforms" since the 1990s: liberalization of tenancy, abrogation of ceiling limits, unrestricted freedom to companies in land acquisition, elimination of prohibition on conversion of agricultural land, formation of land banks for industry, privatization of land records management, and promotion of land rental markets. In urban areas, this translates into: increase in rental prices, diversion of urban land for real estate and infrastructure, permitting FDI in real estate, deregulation of land use zones, and slum evictions through market-based solutions. Nayak notes that between 1997 and 2008, "nearly 2 lakh farmers committed suicide" alongside the consolidation of neoliberal land policies — a correlation that cannot be ignored.

The **tribal land question** in Northeast India is a particularly rich section. Studies from Manipur reveal that "the state intervention in regulating the land tenures system has fuelled the demand for customary landownership due to fear of complete alienation of land." Land records computerization has "created insecurity among the tribals there." In Meghalaya, community land is being converted to private property by Khasi chiefs — "internal land grabbing" that "may change the character of land of Khasi society." In Manipur hills, the administration's push for individual ownership in the RoR "promotes individualization of land alien to the customary owned and held land. It encourages class formation within the community due to unequal landownership among the community." The conclusion from Northeast India studies is emphatic: "The present administrative policy to land has led to the hardening of ethnic identities and conflicts and break-up of communities."

The Draft National Land Reforms Policy, 2013, provides a remarkable data point from within the state apparatus: "nearly 60 per cent of the country's population has right over only 5 per cent of the country's land, whereas 10 per cent of the population has control over 55 per cent of land." Rural landlessness has increased from 35 per cent in 1987 to 49 per cent in 2011–2012. The "reversal of land reforms since the 1990s is coincided with an increase in the proportion of rural households that did not cultivate land and inequality in operational holdings." The statistics indict the neoliberal land policy of the state from within its own documentation.

## Chapter 5: Legal and Institutional Mechanisms for Land Titling

This is the book's most technically demanding chapter — and perhaps its most original contribution — systematically analysing the constitutional provisions governing land, the structure of the RoR system, the nature of land disputes, and a critical examination of the Draft Land Titling Bill, 2011.

On **constitutional provisions**, Nayak demonstrates that land sits primarily in the *State List* (Entry 18 — rights in land, tenures, landlord-tenant relations) and the *Concurrent List* (Entry 6 — transfer of property other than agricultural land; Entry 42 — acquisition and requisition). Crucially, the Directive Principles of State Policy under Article 39 direct the state to ensure that "the ownership and control of the material resources of the community are so distributed as best to sub-serve the common good" and that "the operation of the economic system does not result in the concentration of wealth." The 73rd Amendment vests Panchayats with explicit responsibilities for "land improvement, implementation of land reforms, land consolidation and soil conservation." The constitutional grammar of Indian land governance is, in Nayak's reading, distributive — a mandate for an interventionist state, not merely a facilitating one.

The chapter contains an exhaustive account of what a typical **RoR actually records**. The 2009 Committee on State Agrarian Relations quotes the multi-dimensional nature: "The rights of the individuals and the communities in respect of the land, water resources, trees and forests, use of land, cultivation, incidence of payment of rent, change in the physical features, the authority which the Government exercises in respect of this land, method of change in the rights, etc., are all included in a body of documents called the records-of-rights." The RoR records nine categories: ownership rights, homestead rights, patta rights, dakhalkar rights, sharecroppers' rights, lease rights, hold-over rights, permissive possession rights, and — in addition — easement rights for roads, irrigation, bathing, grazing, worship in temples and mosques, burning ghats, etc. "In the tribal areas of Jharkhand, the rights structure varies not only from tribe to tribe but even within the tribes after every distance of 10 to 15 kilometres." No standardized, digitized, individually-anchored title certificate can capture this living institutional complexity.

The **Draft Land Titling Bill, 2011** receives the book's most critical legal scrutiny. Nayak identifies its structural problems: the proposed *Land Titling Officer* is vested with quasi-judicial powers to adjudicate competing claims to title, raising constitutional questions about delegating judicial functions to executive officers — particularly given the Torrens system's vulnerability, discussed in Chapter 1, to constitutional challenge on due process grounds. The bill makes no provision whatsoever for the protection of tenants, sharecroppers, women with secondary rights, or tribal communities with customary interests. "The provisions of the draft land titling bill clearly take the interests of the propertied classes solely into account and ignore the interests of landless persons such as tenants, women and others secondary interests holders." Nayak's Chapter 5 conclusion is pointed: "The main idea behind such a land titling policy strategy is to create the regime of individualization of landholding and remove restrictions on land transactions so that the land market can grow and land becomes a commodity."

The analysis of **land disputes** draws on state legislation and Supreme Court judgements. The Andhra Pradesh Land Grabbing (Prohibition) Act, 1982, defines land grabbing as "every activity of grabbing of any land… without any lawful entitlement and with a view to illegally taking possession of such lands, or to construct unauthorised structures thereon for sale or hire." States have enacted such statutes precisely because land fraud is pervasive — and yet the land titling programme is premised on the assumption that a state with the administrative capacity to adjudicate and guarantee millions of titles accurately can be constructed. The Supreme Court's ruling in *S. P. Chengalvaraya Naidu v. Jagannath* is invoked: "A fraud is an act of deliberate deception with the design of securing something by taking unfair advantage of another… Non-production and even non-mentioning of the release deed at the trial is tantamount to playing fraud on the court." Once fraud is detected, "all the advantages gained by playing fraud can be taken away" — a principle directly in tension with the Torrens system's *indefeasibility*. Meanwhile, the World Bank has itself admitted that "it is a common 'misconception' to expect that land title guarantees the exact length and breadth of the boundary of the registered parcel of land" — an admission that has "serious implication on the so-called indefeasibility of conclusive land titles in India."

The **adverse possession** doctrine — by which a trespasser who occupies land "openly, peacefully and in a manner hostile to the interests of the legal owner" for twelve years acquires prescriptive title under the Limitation Act, 1963 — is extensively discussed. Nayak notes that this doctrine protects "the poor and informal landholders" and that the shift to conclusive titling risks extinguishing it, removing a crucial safety net for the informally settled poor.

## Chapter 6: Land Market, Conclusive Title and Revisiting Land Reforms

Chapter 6 stages the book's central political confrontation: between the neoliberal advocacy for land market development through titling and legalization of tenancy, and the rights-based demand for revisiting the redistributive land reforms agenda.

The opening data point is politically instructive: a 2011–2012 study in West Bengal, Rajasthan, and Haryana reveals "that close to 61 per cent of farmers were willing to part with their farmland in Singur, West Bengal, if they were given an adequate compensation," while "70 per cent rural youth say 'no' to farming as their livelihood." This demographic shift is real, and Nayak does not deny it. But his point is that the pressure for land market development does not *negate* the simultaneous demand for protective land rights by the 49 per cent of rural households that remain landless.

The **World Bank's specific prescriptions for India** are quoted in detail from its report "India: Land Policies for Growth and Poverty Reduction." The Bank advocated: reducing stamp duties to facilitate land transactions; legalizing tenancy and land leasing; replacing ceiling laws with regulations that facilitate rental markets; allowing beneficiaries of land reform to transfer their land through lease; removing restrictions on sale of land to non-agriculturalists; revising compulsory acquisition laws to facilitate direct negotiation between farmers and industry; and providing "a wider range of ownership patterns" among tribals due to "break-up of traditional community patterns." Nayak observes with dry precision: "If a perceptive reader compares the suggestions of the Bank in its widely referred report with the policy strategies reflected in India's Ninth Five-Year Plan to Twelfth Five-Year Plan on land matters, he/she would clearly observe the similarities of approach to land questions of India." The policy capture is documented from within official documents.

The Bank's logic is quoted directly: "greater reliance on markets can achieve original land reform goals" better than state-led redistribution. Nayak demolishes this claim: "In the real world, the land market does not help those who need for their subsistence, but those who have the capability to buy, invest and speculate the prices of land." A Maharashtra study reveals that even in areas with digitized land records, "the use of land as collateral is low in India compared to other economies" and "informal sources of getting finance are popular due to lower paper work and transaction cost." The "people have sentimental values attached to their land. Land titling may not directly facilitate collateralization of land as other enabling conditionalities are required."

The demand for revisiting **redistributive land reform** emerges with equal force. The Draft National Land Reforms Policy, 2013, admits that "nearly 70 per cent of people depend on land as farmers and farm labourers" and that "the link between the landlessness and poverty in India is undeniable." The Ekta Parishad's march of 100,000 poor persons from Gwalior to Delhi on 2 October 2012 — demanding "land to the landless and poor, the speedy disposal of cases of land alienation by the courts, the loss of farmland, updating land records, secured land rights and pro-poor land reforms" — reveals that "land issues are still a political agenda and have the potent ground for a massive mobilization of landless farmers and labourers." The Working Group on Land Relations for the Eleventh Five-Year Plan recommended "lowering the ceiling limits in view of the improved productivity under the impact of new technology, bringing the lands irrigated through private and perennial sources under the ambit of ceiling laws, suitable amendments to prevent benami transactions," and "setting up of fast-track tribunals for speedy disposal of ceiling laws."

The PESA Act (1996) and FRA (2006) are studied as case studies in the gap between statutory intent and implementation reality. PESA's provisions — empowering Gram Sabhas to prevent tribal land alienation, require consultation before acquisition in Scheduled Areas, and restore unlawfully alienated tribal land — "have not been implemented in letter and spirit in any of the states having Schedule Areas, as these provisions legally take away the administrative and legal power of revenue administration functionaries." The FRA, despite recognizing both individual and community forest rights, has resulted in only 3 per cent of the minimum potential for *community* forest rights recognition being achieved — with individual rights being preferred by the bureaucratic-driven implementation machinery. The Pathalgadi movement in Jharkhand, in which tribal communities erected stone plaques inscribed with constitutional provisions, is identified as one manifestation of a growing assertive politics that refuses both the dispossession of neoliberal land acquisition and the inadequacy of formal statutory protection.

The chapter maps three competing approaches: (1) strong policy advocacy for liberalizing land laws and promoting land markets; (2) an approach that sees land rights in terms of livelihood, sustainability, and constitutional governance; and (3) a reformist position advocating for revisiting (not abandoning) land reforms, such as legalizing tenancy with tenant protections. Nayak's judgment is sobering: "No state government is interested to disturb the existing status quo in landownership by way of the implementation of redistributive measures like ceiling on landholding and abolition of absent landlordism." The guaranteed title to land has not even figured in official documents such as the national policy on agriculture or national policy for farmers — its advocacy has come almost exclusively from the World Bank and its funded research organizations.

## Chapter 7: Conclusion — Looking Beyond Techno-Managerialism

The concluding chapter synthesizes the book's findings through a series of theoretical frameworks that give them normative depth.

James C. Scott's analysis of **state legibility** is invoked to illuminate the DILRMP's underlying logic. Scott's argument — that modern states seek to render social realities legible through simplification and standardization for administrative control — maps perfectly onto the land titling agenda. "The legibility exercise would inevitably require a detailed codification of property regimes in land, inheritance rules and customary practices." The cadastral system has "only utilitarian value of making land as commodity. 'Any value that the land might have for subsistence purposes or for local ecology was bracketed as aesthetic ritual or sentimental values' by high modernists such as policymakers and experts on land." The DILRMP is thus a project of *state simplification* that "would benefit those well off and informed sections who can grasp the complex statutes" while disadvantaging small farmers and secondary interest holders.

The study finds **"depoliticizing politics of land reform"** by the Indian state with the help of the World Bank — "repudiation of the rationale for land reform as not politically viable in the contemporary political economy." The Annual Activity Reports of the Ministry of Rural Development have "stopped highlighting the achievements of land reform programmes since 2007–2008" and now give importance to "improving land management and land records." The NITI Aayog's 2017 policy documents advocate a shift from the abolition of tenancy to legalization of tenancy — a formal inversion of the post-colonial redistributive programme. Nayak connects this to Jenkin's observation: "The sort of manipulative and obfuscatory tactics employed regularly in the implementation of reform are, however, a much truer representation of the political reality in India."

Karl **Polanyi's** concept of *fictitious commodities* is deployed to interrogate the moral foundations of land commodification. Land, Polanyi argued, is "another name for nature, which is not produced at all" — its description as a commodity is "entirely fictitious." The market organisation of land "would mean the subordination of the substance of society itself to the laws of the market." David Harvey's formulation is also invoked: commodification of land "implies the existence of property rights over processes, things and social relations, which a price can be put on them, and they can be traded subject to legal contract." For Nayak, this analysis connects directly to the SDGs 2015 agenda binding nations to "protect, restore and promote sustainable use of terrestrial ecosystems, sustainably manage forests, combat desertification, and halt and reverse land degradation" — a commitment entirely at odds with the commodification agenda.

**Elinor Ostrom's** prediction frames the book's normative alternative: "many individuals will work, live and play in the next century in institutions governed and managed by mixed systems of communal and individual property rights." Her critique of "naïve over reliance on the presumed efficiency of private property" and her recognition that successful small-holder agriculture requires not merely land title but "a wide variety of valuable resources" — credit, infrastructure, effective courts, strong local governments, user-managed forests, pastures, and fisheries — constitutes the practical political vision that land titling advocates systematically exclude.

**Douglass North's** institutional economics adds the temporal dimension: "Informal constraints such as customs, conventions and practices do not change immediately in response to the introduction of formal rules." The institutional constraints governing India's land relations are "a complex mix of formal and informal constraints." Their "persistence of inefficiency" is not a technical failure to be corrected by a new property rights system but "historical factors relating to formal and informal constraints" — and vested interests that "may lead to a situation of more efficient results but may antagonize entrenched interests." North's observation is deployed as a caution against institutional transplantation: "Even when efficient property rights are devised, they will still typically have features that will be very costly to monitor or enforce, reflecting built in disincentives or at the very least aspects of exchange that provide temptation to renege, shirk, steal or cheat."

The book's final intellectual move deploys **Jeremy Waldron's** political philosophy of property against the land titling consensus. Waldron's warning is quoted in full: "private property involves a pledge by society that it will continue to use its moral and physical authority to uphold the rights of owners even against those who have no employment, no food to eat, no home to go, and no land to stand on from which they are not anytime liable to be evicted." The guaranteed title system, by exclusively serving the interests of registered title holders, is not merely technically flawed — it is *morally inconsistent* with a constitutional democracy's obligations to its most vulnerable citizens.

The book closes with a judgment as measured as it is damning: "The attack on the existing presumptive property rights in land record does not have any theoretical or empirical reasoning of its being prejudicial to the interests of the poor and landless. Rather criticism against the presumptive land records is guided by politically and ideologically motivated objective of building a regime for land market and its institutions." Harold Lemel's observation anchors the conclusion: land titling "is not simply a technical matter but involves complex social intervention. The implementation of land title, the criteria of its evaluation and judgements of its outcomes would inevitably require 'knowledge of the social and historical circumstances into which it is to be inserted'." The DILRMP has no such knowledge — and is not designed to acquire it.

## Critical Assessment: Strengths, Limitations, and Scholarly Significance

**Strengths**: *Land Reforms to Land Titling* is a landmark work in Indian land policy scholarship for several reasons. First, its *disciplinary range* is exceptional: few Indian scholars have integrated legal history of the Torrens system, the anthropology of African customary land tenure, the political economy of five-year plan documents, constitutional provisions of the Seventh Schedule, and the philosophy of Polanyi and Ostrom in a single coherent argument. Second, Nayak's *insider credibility* — writing as a senior revenue administrator who has worked the RoR system from the ground up — gives the legal-institutional analysis a granularity and credibility that purely academic accounts cannot achieve. Third, the book's *historical depth* is its greatest analytical contribution: demonstrating that the very reasons the colonial authorities chose to maintain *presumptive* (not conclusive) land records — the irreducible complexity and diversity of India's land tenure system — remain fully operative today, and are simply being wished away by techno-managerial enthusiasm. Fourth, the book's comparative chapter on developing country experiences is the most comprehensive critical survey of land titling failures available in Indian academic literature.

**Limitations**: The book's weakest sections involve its treatment of post-2015 developments — the Smart Cities Mission, PMAY, digital payments infrastructure — and their interaction with land markets. Kerala and West Bengal, where land reform achieved measurable redistributive outcomes, receive insufficient treatment despite being the most important empirical counterpoints to the neoliberal claim that redistribution is administratively infeasible. The book occasionally lapses into a binary between redistribution and commodification that underestimates the complexity of reformist positions (such as legalization of tenancy with genuine tenant protections) that do not fit neatly into either camp. The World Bank's more recent internal debates — including significant self-criticism from some of its own researchers — are cited selectively.

**Significance for Development Studies**: For students at institutions like TISS engaging with agrarian studies, land acquisition, tribal rights, gender and property, or the political economy of neoliberal development, this book is indispensable. It provides the most thorough critical account available of the NLRMP/DILRMP policy complex, its genealogy, its comparative failures, and its ideological foundations. Its argument — that the shift from land reform to land titling is a neoliberal political project disguised as administrative modernization, implemented through stealth to evade democratic scrutiny, and structurally incapable of serving the interests of India's landless poor — is a contribution that demands sustained scholarly engagement. In an era when land conflicts are intensifying, tribal communities are in revolt, and the agrarian crisis deepens, Nayak's book reminds us that the question of *who benefits from land governance reform* is not a technical question. It is, as it has always been, a political one.

## **Transfer of Property Act**

Documents concerning transfer of immovable property by way of sale, lease, mortgage or exchange are drafted in India against the background of the Transfer of Property Act, 1882, or its principles. This Act, while providing for the consequences and machinery of transfer, does not envisage that the document of title shall originate from a public authority. For example, if the transaction is one of sale of immovable property, section 55(1)(b) of the Act imposes an obligation on the seller to produce the documents of title for the inspection of the prospective purchaser. But these documents of title are not certified by the state


## **Registration Act**

The Registration Act, 1908 provides for the registration of documents but not for the registration of titles. Registration of documents in India is compulsory in some cases and voluntary in others. It is compulsory where some provision either in the Transfer of Property Act (for example, section 54 in the case of an outright sale of immovable property) or some provision in the Registration Act (for example, section 17 dealing with various transactions concerning immovable property) provides for compulsory registration. In all other cases, unless provided by a special law, registration of a document is optional, particularly in the case of wills.

**Conveyancing in India**

The traditional system of conveyancing and registration in India contemplates that each private person who wishes to undertake or enter into a transaction concerning immovable property must investigate the title in the property himself. He may investigate it to the best of his ability and yet some defect may remain. The reason is that while the successive links as investigated and as leading to the present transaction may have been satisfactorily investigated as they stand, it is possible that some of the intermediate documents might themselves be defective because of external factors (forgery, want of consent, incompetence by reason of age, etc) and other causes

Land may go on being transferred, without quick consequential mutations in the record, so that the record as it exists and continues to exist today hardly reflects the present day reality regarding ownership of the land.

The Torrens system, as stated above, is intended to secure, by an official determination, registration of title to land. Title insurance as a private measure is, of course, a possible alternative. # Claude Primer
**Guaranteeing Title to Land: A Preliminary Study — D.C. Wadhwa (EPW, 1989)**

## Provenance and Context

This is a preliminary study submitted by D.C. Wadhwa to the **Planning Commission of India** in his capacity as Chairman of a One-Man Committee on Record-of-Rights in Land appointed by the Planning Commission. Published in _Economic and Political Weekly_ in October 1989, it is explicitly framed as a scene-setter — not a set of final recommendations, but a systematic mapping of the problem, comparative international experience, and a broad proposal for India. It covers administrative, legal, scientific, constitutional, and legislative dimensions.

---

## I. The Core Problem: India's Fiscal Records vs. Title

### What the Records Actually Are

The present records-of-rights in land in India are fiscal in nature. The person shown on the records as responsible for paying land-revenue for a particular piece of land is presumed to be the proprietor of that land unless it is proved otherwise. Thus title to land is only incidental.

This is the paper's foundational diagnosis. Indian land records were designed for revenue collection — to identify who pays _land revenue_ — not to certify ownership. Proprietary status is merely a by-product of the revenue entry, not an independently established legal fact.

### The Supreme Court Confirms the Problem

As late as August 1989, it was affirmed by the Supreme Court of India: "It is firmly established that the revenue records are not documents of title." Thus, whatever be the entry in the records-of-rights in land, it would be permissible to challenge it in the appropriate court or tribunal.

This position arises because property legislation and legislation relating to registration of documents were never framed in India with the objective of providing a state guarantee of title to land.

### The Transfer of Property Act, 1882

Documents concerning transfer of immovable property by way of sale, lease, mortgage or exchange are drafted in India against the background of the Transfer of Property Act, 1882. This Act, while providing for the consequences and machinery of transfer, does not envisage that the document of title shall originate from a public authority. The seller must produce their own documents of title, but these are private documents — not state-certified.

### The Registration Act, 1908

The Indian Registration Act, 1908, provides for the registration of documents but not for the registration of titles. Registration is compulsory only in certain cases (e.g., outright sale of immovable property), and the registering officer is not supposed to concern himself with the validity of the document. This is a critical structural gap: registration of a document does not validate the underlying transaction or confirm title.

### Conveyancing in India: The Chain-of-Deeds Problem

The traditional system of conveyancing and registration in India contemplates that each private person who wishes to undertake or enter into a transaction concerning immovable property must investigate the title in the property himself. Even diligent investigation can fail because intermediate documents in the chain may themselves be defective due to forgery, lack of consent, minority, undiscovered wills, or a fundamentally weak original source. It is elementary that no successive transaction can ride higher than the original and ultimate source.

### Factual State of Land Records

Preliminary investigations have revealed that in all parts of the country, records relating to land are in a very bad shape. Agricultural land in many areas is still recorded in the name of a person who died long ago and whose legal successors are now the owners but their names are not entered in the records.

Land may go on being transferred, without quick consequential mutations in the record, so that the record as it exists and continues to exist today hardly reflects the present day reality regarding ownership of the land. Millions of cases of mutation and measurement are pending in the country.

The result: such disharmony between the record and the reality not only destroys the utility of the record — which thus becomes a negative defect — but also misleads any person who has to deal with the land... this unhappy situation has led to incessant litigation and the clogging of the judicial machinery in the country.

### The Law of Evidence Problem

The Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (Sections 32 and 35) attaches special importance to public records, envisages their accuracy, and grants them elevated evidentiary status. Underlying all these special provisions of the evidence law is the assumption that public registers and other documents would be accurate. When records are outdated and inaccurate, the entire juristic premise of the Evidence Act collapses: it is undeniable that these presumptions fall to the ground if the records are not properly revised from time to time.

Wadhwa invokes the **Domesday Book** (1085–86, William the Conqueror) as the historical ideal — a survey so meticulous and authoritative that when a dispute arises concerning written down facts and an appeal is made to the book itself, the evidence that the book gives cannot be stretched at naught. India's records are the precise opposite of this standard.

## II. The Two Possible Remedies

Two possible remedies have been thought of. The first is a change from a system of registration of deeds to a system of registration of title to land — commonly known as the Torrens system. The second is the practice of arranging for the title insurance.

---

## III. The Torrens System: Origins, Philosophy, and Features

### Genesis

The Torrens system originated in the state of South Australia and the person to whom the credit for this goes was Sir Robert Torrens. He was dissatisfied with English conveyancing. While serving in the customs, he had become familiar with the law of shipping and had conceived the idea that his purpose might be achieved by applying to land the principles which regulate the transfer of shipping. He modelled it on the English Merchant Shipping Act — the idea being that title to land should be as simple and certain as title to a ship.

The **Real Property Act, 1858** (South Australia) was the result, and after it came into force, it replaced the previous unwieldy system of conveyancing by a certificate guaranteed by the state and made the conveyancing simple, quick and cheap.

### Main Features

The main features of the Torrens system are: (a) achievement of certainty of title to land; and (b) simplification of conveyancing relating to land. Certainty of title to land is achieved by certifying, or guaranteeing, by the state, the validity of title to land. Once the title to land of a person is registered by the state as above, the title of the registered proprietor is paramount, subject to certain specified exceptions. The purchaser of the land registered under the system need not go behind the certificate.

### Basic Philosophy

Under the traditional system, the state only registers the _transaction_ when recorded in writing. Under the Torrens system, the state, in effect, guarantees title to land (with a few exceptions). Without such certification of title to land, the potential buyer of property is protected only by a so-called 'abstract'. The 'abstract' is really a legal history of the title to land. The attorney of the purchaser may examine an 'abstract' and give an opinion as to the validity of the title, but there is no guarantee that the attorney's opinion is correct.

Her Majesty's Land Registry's own explanatory leaflet captured the benefit precisely: registration of title "gives finality. It does away with the repeated, imperfect and costly examination of past title. It removes the possibility of bona fide mistakes as to the past title or the existing burdens affecting the land. It removes the ever-present possibility of fraud by duplication or suppression of deeds. It gives state-guaranteed safety and that positive security against adverse claims which the system of conveyancing by deeds can never give."

### Essential Working Features (Enumerated)

The paper lists four operational pillars of the system: (i) unambiguous definition of land parcels, rights, and entitled persons; (ii) title depends on the _act of registration_, not on private documents or judicial orders; (iii) working records must be kept up-to-date, protectively duplicated, and regularly cleared of obsolete matter; (iv) control of both land survey and bookkeeping must vest in organs of central government, even if field operations are territorially decentralised.

## IV. International Comparative Survey

### Australia

The Torrens system spread from South Australia (1858) to Queensland (1861), New South Wales, Tasmania, and Victoria (1862), and Western Australia (1874). New Zealand adopted it in 1870.

**Technological innovation:** The New South Wales Land Titles Office, reputedly the largest centralised land registry in the world, leads the rest of Australia in the development of modern management methods and technical innovations in processing dealings with land. By 1983, NSW had computerised its Torrens Register (Automated Land Title System), yielding immediate online access, rapid registration, and elimination of out-of-file search problems.

### England

The Land Registration Act, 1925, is the mature consolidating statute, conferring on registration conclusive title to land on the registered holder, subject to certain conditions as to rectification and correction.

Wadhwa compares the English and Torrens systems: England applies registration sporadically; Torrens countries apply it comprehensively. English titles are more rectifiable (in cases of fraud, duress, illegality, mistake); Torrens titles are nearly sacrosanct. England operates the system almost as title insurance, which differs from the Australian approach. The English chief land registrar holds wide judicial powers, which keeps disputes out of courts.

### Canada

Canada has multiple systems operating in parallel, which Wadhwa describes as a rich comparative laboratory. In 1971, the Ontario Law Reform Commission made a comparative study and reached the conclusion unequivocally that "a land titles system is superior to a registry system in almost every material respect in which comparison can be made at present. A land titles system is also the system that can best be adapted to fit the needs of the future, particularly when seen as a major component of an integrated land information system, or when considered on the basis of potential compatibility with electronic data storage and retrieval technology."

In the Maritime Provinces (New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Prince Edward Island), deed registration was being converted to title registration with computerisation. In Quebec, the French system of deed registration operates under Napoleonic Code principles — registration confers priority but does not cure title defects.

The paper also discusses the **Alsace-Lorraine case** — a region that oscillated between German (Grundbuch) and French (deed registration) systems. Under German influence, registration of title was introduced in 1891. The German Civil Code (1899) made register entry the _sole admissible evidence_ of title. The result in Alsace-Lorraine was a hybrid midway between German and French systems.

### USA: Title Insurance as Dominant Alternative

In the United States, Illinois was the first state to enact, in 1895, a title registration statute based on the Torrens legislation. In all, 19 states enacted Torrens statutes between 1895 and 1917 and five states have since repealed them.

Why did Torrens fail in the USA? Judicial determination of title to land before initial registration greatly increased the time and cost of first registration and in effect this requirement dealt the Torrens system the mortal blow from which it has never been able to recover in the United States. Additionally, there is a great deal of opposition to this system from the "vested-interest groups — notably title insurance companies, professional abstractors and some attorneys — who thrive on and would perpetuate the confusion which current recording systems create."

Instead, the USA developed a private **title insurance** industry. There are approximately 160 title insurance corporations in the United States, the first having been established in 1870. Of these, about 125 are 'local companies' that confine their title insurance to one state, and most of them maintain what they call 'title plants' which are simply registers of title to land operated by private enterprise.

The critique is sharp: the title plant duplicates the public record (or what the public record could be, if it were organised as efficiently as the title plant), and yet a further copy of the records may be made when another company sets up in competition in the same area. Somebody must pay not merely for the official record, but also for each additional set of records, though one set, properly organised, would be adequate. The somebody who pays is the prospective landowner.

A note in the _Yale Law Journal_ is quoted as an "indictment of American jurisprudence": the system has enabled title guaranty companies to "put by millions in surplus, after paying immense dividends, salaries and clerical expenses, all extorted as a tax on land titles and transfers, for what has been somewhat sarcastically put as insuring against everything but loss."

Professor Barnett's observation is cited: the United States is rapidly becoming virtually the only country in the world whose land title system is not founded upon Torrens-type principles.

### Afro-Asian Models

According to the Encyclopaedia Britannica, more than 50 countries in the world have adopted the Torrens system. The paper details three models of particular relevance:

**Singapore (1956):** Provides three pathways into the register — automatic registration of new state grants, voluntary application, and registrar-directed registration. 1970 amendments added compulsory registration where planning permission to develop or sub-divide unregistered land is granted.

**Malaysia (1965):** The National Land Code unified two previously different systems (deed-based in Penang and Malacca; Torrens-based in nine Malay states). Under the provisions of this Code, registration confers an indefeasible title to land except in certain circumstances, though there is no provision for indemnity.

**Kenya (1963):** Wadhwa gives this the most detailed treatment because it is the model he finds most instructive for India. The Registered Land Act of Kenya is intended to serve as substantive law as well as the law of registration. Key provisions: registration of a person vests absolute ownership; rights acquired on registration or for valuable consideration are indefeasible; a purchaser for value need not look behind the register; and a government indemnity fund compensates those who suffer damage from rectification, errors, or omissions — except where the claimant contributed through fraud or negligence.

## V. Concluding Arguments

### Why India Needs This Change

The paper summarises the **defects of deed registration**:

A deed does not in itself prove title; it is merely a record of an isolated transaction. If properly drawn, it shows that a particular transaction took place, but it does not prove that the parties were legally entitled to carry out the transaction and consequently it does not prove the transaction valid. A deeds register also cannot show matters affecting title that are not the subject of a deed — for example, succession on death, which gives title by operation of law and not by act of the parties.

And the **advantages of title registration**: "Security, simplicity, accuracy, expedition, cheapness, suitability to its circumstances and completeness of record" are the main advantages of this system. It also reduces greatly litigation over land.

### Registration as Aid to Government

The preparation and maintenance of records-of-rights in land got low priority in all the seven five-year plans of the states when they decided their programmes of development. It was forgotten that the maintenance of an up-to-date records-of-rights in land was a "social and economic service, part of the administrative infrastructure so necessary for the developing of an increasingly complex economy."

The major bottleneck in all land acquisition proceedings in the country is the absence of an up-to-date and authentic records-of-rights in land. The delay in acquisition proceedings on this account sometimes leads to enormous cost overruns and time overruns of various developmental projects, big and small.

### Land Reform and Protective Legislation

Wadhwa makes a pointed argument about land reform implementation. Post-independence land distributed to landless persons under various schemes came with restrictions on transfer. As at the time of registration of sale-deeds the validity of the transactions is not examined, a large number of such allottees are reported to have transferred those lands and have become landless again. A title registration system would prevent this because all restrictions would appear on the register. Similarly, tribal land laws prohibiting alienation are being widely violated — again, a title register with restrictions recorded would check this.

## VI. Proposal and Concrete Suggestions

### Broad Proposal

Tentatively, it would seem that the time has come for a gradual, phased and well-planned change-over to the system of registration of title to land in India. Models recommended for detailed study: **Australian** (freshness of approach, some states already computerised) and **Kenyan** (the two-in-one advantage of combining substantive land law with registration law in one enactment).

Constitutional questions — legislative competence as between Union and states — would also need to be resolved.

### Land Title Assurance Fund

The financial liability objection is addressed head-on. South Australia financed its initial Assurance Fund by a levy of 0.2% on the value of land brought under the Act. England financed theirs from registration fees. Experience shows the fear is overblown: the highest tribute that could be paid to the success of the Torrens system is the infrequency of claims on the Assurance Fund. Both Australia and England eventually closed or dramatically scaled back their funds due to minimal claims.

### Land Title Corporation

If state governments are reluctant, a separate autonomous statutory corporation may be envisaged for the purpose — a 'Land Title Corporation' or 'Land Title Assurance or Guarantee Corporation'. In fact, in terms of efficiency and accountability, a separate organisation like the corporation may, perhaps, be preferable. This corporation could build its information system on modern lines using computers from inception, drawing on Sweden's Land Data Bank System as a model.

### The Concrete Operational Plan

This is the most practically detailed part of the paper. The key insight is that India already has a base to work from:

Under the present system of survey and settlement operations in India, the most important part of the work of a settlement officer is the determination of the proprietary rights in land, with appeal from his decision to the court. At law, an entry in the records-of-rights in land, with regards to proprietorship of land, as determined by the Settlement Officer, is prima facie evidence of ownership of land.

The problem is that surveys are outdated and mutations have not been recorded, causing huge divergence between record and ground reality. Wadhwa's step-by-step proposal:

1. **Revisional surveys** should be undertaken nationwide as a priority plan scheme with Central assistance, with wholesale revision of all pre-1950 surveys.
2. During these revisional surveys, introduce registration of title, converting presumptive titles into conclusive ones.
3. **Pilot approach first**: Start in a district or two in each state where revisional surveys have been completed recently.
4. **Publication process**: Keep draft records open for public inspection for **three months** (rather than the current one month) to allow objections.
5. **Uncontested cases** get conclusive titles recorded straightaway. **Contested cases** go to civil courts and receive conclusive titles after a court judgment _in rem_ (with the same procedure Germany followed when introducing its Grundbuch in 1900).
6. The operation should advance **plot by plot, village by village, district by district**.
7. For **north-eastern states** and areas without any prior survey, presumptive rights are determined during first survey operations, then allowed to ripen into conclusive titles after approximately **thirty years**.

## VII. The Quintessence

Wadhwa closes with a comprehensive summation of why the system matters:

- For landowners: guaranteed title enables dealings with _security, expedition and cheapness_; the certificate will record all encumbrances, restrictions, easements, mortgages — making land more marketable and a better inter-generational store of value.
- For rural borrowers: a registered proprietor or tenant can borrow money quickly and cheaply on the security of their landholding.
- For fiscal concerns: with abolition of land revenue in some states, the existing presumption (payer of land revenue = owner) has been undermined. Title registration replaces that presumption with a more robust basis.
- For litigation: continuous finality of title reduces both civil and criminal litigation.
- For the state's own land: the state is unable to protect even its own rights in its own lands, which is evident from the large-scale encroachment of government lands in all parts of the country. Title registration protects government land, village commons, and public bodies' land as well.

The closing sentence captures the entire argument: **"The quintessence of this system is security."**

# AI Primer
**Guaranteeing Title to Land in India: Institutional Failure, Comparative Lessons, and Reform Possibilities**

## **Introduction**

Land occupies a foundational position in any socio-economic system. It is not merely a factor of production but also a repository of wealth, identity, and power. In agrarian societies such as India, land constitutes the primary economic asset, shaping access to credit, social hierarchy, and political influence. Despite this centrality, the institutional framework governing land rights in India remains fundamentally weak. D.C. Wadhwa’s seminal 1989 paper, _“Guaranteeing Title to Land: A Preliminary Study,”_ provides a rigorous critique of this system and proposes a transition toward a regime of guaranteed land titles.

The central thesis of Wadhwa’s work is that India operates under a **presumptive system of land titles**, where records serve fiscal and administrative purposes rather than establishing definitive ownership. This stands in contrast to systems in other parts of the world that provide **conclusive, state-guaranteed titles**. The consequences of this institutional design are profound: uncertainty of ownership, high transaction costs, and pervasive litigation. This essay reconstructs Wadhwa’s arguments, situates them within broader theoretical frameworks, and evaluates their continued relevance in contemporary India.

---

## **I. The Nature and Importance of Land Records**

Wadhwa begins by emphasizing the critical role of land records in the functioning of an economy. Land, he argues, is not merely an economic resource but a **“mother resource”**, giving rise to various other forms of economic value. In both rural and urban contexts, it constitutes the most valuable asset. Consequently, rights to land must be:

- Efficiently recorded
- Easily transferable
- Capable of definitive verification

However, the Indian system fails on all three counts.

The importance of land records is also embedded in the legal framework, particularly in the Indian Evidence Act, 1872. Sections 32 and 35 of the Act accord significant evidentiary value to public records, based on the assumption that such records are:

1. Carefully prepared
2. Regularly updated
3. Faithful representations of reality

As Wadhwa notes, these assumptions underpin the entire evidentiary regime:

> “The legal system assumes that public records are prepared carefully… maintained accurately… and are faithful to the external world.”

Yet, this assumption is deeply flawed. When records are not updated or accurately maintained, the legal presumption collapses, undermining the legitimacy of the evidentiary framework itself.

---

## **II. The Conceptual Distinction: Acts in Law vs Acts of the Sovereign**

A key conceptual contribution of Wadhwa lies in distinguishing between:

- **“Acts in the law”**: private transactions such as sale, inheritance, or lease
- **“Acts of the sovereign”**: public records maintained by the state

These two must align. Public records should faithfully reflect private transactions. However, when this alignment breaks down, the system becomes incoherent:

> “The two are inter-linked and should harmonise… If that desideratum is not fulfilled, then the very justifiability… falls to the ground.”

This insight anticipates later developments in institutional economics, where the alignment between formal rules and actual practices is seen as critical to institutional effectiveness.

---

## **III. The Indian Legal Framework: Absence of State-Certified Title**

The most significant weakness of the Indian system is its failure to provide **state certification of title**. Wadhwa makes this point unequivocally:

> “The statute law of India… does not profess to provide for a state certification of title to land.”

Even though records-of-rights exist, they do not confer ownership. Instead:

- They are **presumptive**, not conclusive
- They can be **challenged in court**
- They serve primarily **fiscal purposes**

This position has been repeatedly affirmed by the Supreme Court of India:

> “Revenue records are not documents of title.”

The root of this problem lies in the design of Indian property law:

- The Transfer of Property Act, 1882 governs the transfer of property but does not provide state-certified title
- The Registration Act, 1908 registers documents, not ownership

Thus, India operates a system of **registration of deeds**, not **registration of title**.

---

## **IV. The Consequences: Uncertainty and Litigation**

The absence of conclusive titles produces a series of systemic problems:

### **1. Burden of Verification**

Every buyer must independently verify the title by examining the chain of documents. Even then, defects may remain due to:

- Forgery
- Undisclosed wills
- Lack of consent
- Incompetence of parties

As Wadhwa notes, “no successive transaction can ride higher than the original and ultimate source.”

---

### **2. Record–Reality Gap**

Land records often fail to reflect actual ownership due to:

- Delayed mutations
- Outdated surveys
- Informal transfers

He observes:

> “The record as it exists… hardly reflects the present day reality regarding ownership.”

---

### **3. Litigation Explosion**

This mismatch leads to widespread disputes:

- Ownership conflicts
- Boundary disputes
- Possession claims

The result is:

> “Incessant litigation and the clogging of the judicial machinery.”

---

## **V. Historical Benchmark: The Domesday Survey**

To illustrate what an authoritative land record system looks like, Wadhwa invokes the Domesday Survey commissioned by William the Conqueror in 1085–86.

The Domesday Book recorded:

- Land ownership
- Types of land
- Tenants and labourers

Its authority was absolute:

> “The evidence it gives cannot be set at naught.”

This serves as a benchmark for what land records should ideally achieve: **finality and certainty**.

---

## **VI. Alternative Systems: The Torrens Model and Title Insurance**

Wadhwa identifies two main alternatives to the Indian system:

### **1. The Torrens System**

Originating in Australia, this system provides:

- State-guaranteed title
- Indefeasibility of ownership
- A central register as the final authority

Its objective is:

> “To secure… a title… impregnable against attack.”

Under this system:

- Title depends on registration, not documents
- Buyers need not investigate past transactions
- The state compensates for errors

---

### **2. Title Insurance (USA)**

This is a private alternative where:

- Insurance companies guarantee title
- Losses due to defects are compensated

However, Wadhwa is critical of this system:

- It duplicates public records
- It imposes costs on buyers
- It reflects institutional inefficiency

---

## **VII. Comparative International Experience**

Wadhwa surveys several countries:

- **Australia**: Successful implementation of the Torrens system
- **England**: Gradual adoption with insurance mechanisms
- **Canada**: Hybrid systems combining Torrens and deed registration
- **Africa and Asia**: Widespread adoption of Torrens principles

He notes that over 50 countries have adopted such systems, demonstrating their global viability.

## **VIII. Advantages of Title Registration**

The transition to a system of conclusive title offers multiple benefits:

### **1. Certainty of Ownership**

The register becomes the final authority, eliminating disputes.

### **2. Reduced Transaction Costs**

No need for extensive title searches or legal verification.

### **3. Enhanced Credit Access**

Land becomes a reliable collateral.

### **4. Administrative Efficiency**

Government functions—such as land acquisition—become smoother.

### **5. Reduced Litigation**

Finality of title minimizes disputes.

Wadhwa summarizes these advantages as:

> “Security, simplicity, accuracy, expedition, cheapness… and completeness of record.”

## **IX. Proposed Reforms for India**

Wadhwa advocates a **gradual transition** to title registration:

### **1. Revisional Surveys**

Update land records to reflect current reality.

### **2. Conversion of Titles**

Transform presumptive titles into conclusive ones.

### **3. Pilot Implementation**

Begin with selected districts.

### **4. Assurance Fund**

Establish a fund to compensate for errors.

### **5. Institutional Reform**

Possibly create a Land Title Corporation.

### **2. Legal Complexity**

## **XI. Contemporary Relevance**

Despite being written in 1989, Wadhwa’s analysis remains strikingly relevant. India has undertaken digitization initiatives such as the Digital India Land Records Modernization Programme, but the fundamental issue persists:

- Records remain **presumptive**
- Title is still **judicially determined**
- Litigation continues

Digitization has improved accessibility but not transformed the underlying legal regime.

## Introduction

For a large proportion of the households in rural areas that own small and marginal land holdings, land is the most important economic resource, especially in low- or middle-income agriculture-based economies. In most of the developing nations around the world, women play critical roles in agricultural operations in spite of having limited access to and control over land (Quisumbing et al., 2014). Even if women own land, either the area of these plots is smaller than or their quality is inferior to the lands owned by men. Apart from this, even women’s land rights are found to be less secure than those of men. Gender and Land Rights Database (GLRD) of the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO, 2018) shows that the share of women agricultural landholders globally is less than 15 per cent and even higher inequalities in land access are found in North Africa and the Middle East, where less than five per cent of the landowners are women. In India, while 73 per cent of rural women workers were engaged in the agriculture sector, only about 14 per cent of the women are operational holders (Agriculture Census, 2015–16).

While broadly women can acquire land by various means, such as inheritance, gift, purchase, or government transfers, Agarwal (1998) mentions that all these sources are structurally and operationally skewed against equal participation of women. Among these, inheritance is usually the most important, especially in South Asia where land is largely privately owned and women are more financially constrained than men in their ability to purchase any. But here also women face widespread discrimination since religious laws have mostly governs the inheritance of land, and these laws have not been favorable toward women. Therefore, when they access land through markets and redistributive reforms they are less likely than men to get land because of discrimination in land markets, differences in incomes and access to credit, and social discrimination (World Bank, 2012).

Though the country has witnessed some ground-breaking decisions, such as the recent amendment in the Hindu Succession Act, which guarantees equal rights to daughters as sons, the bias against women in the cultural and social ethos has restricted the guarantee of equal land rights for women. Not only is this but the government’s initiative on distribution of wasteland to landless households to facilitate rural farmers is also restricted due to various loopholes. Under these programs, "households" have been considered a single unit, assuming that the distribution of resources is equal amongst the members. However, in most cases, the land has been transferred to men in the family. Sometimes, women get preference, but they are just a handful. For example, in 2017, the Uttar Pradesh government distributed land titles to 331 formerly landless households in two villages in the Mirzapur district, in which single women got just 7 per cent of the land titles. Clearly, such programs have focused on changing household rights to land, not those of individuals within the household (Lastarria-Cornhiel et al., 2014). Evidences from South Asia, Africa, and Latin America also demonstrates that women are disadvantaged in both statutory and customary land tenure systems (Agarwal, 1994, Lastarria-Cornhiel, 1997, Deere and León, 2001, Deere et al., 2011), and that men and women within households do not necessarily pool resources (Haddad et al., 1997). Hence, such land distribution programs not necessarily implies that women within those households have equal and secure land rights.

Insecure land rights create obstacles for women engaged in agriculture or allied activities or running a home-based enterprise (World Bank, 2019). This often constrains women’s economic prospects and makes them vulnerable to poverty and violence. On the other hand, studies from Nepal (Allendorf, 2007b, Mishra and Sam, 2016) and Ethiopia (Mason, 1998) show that secure land rights play an important role in improving the economic security and decision-making power of women. Having secure land rights enables them to make investment decisions, acquire better quality inputs for land, or participate in land rental markets, leading to higher economic gains. Therefore, it is imperative to ensure equal land rights for women for their empowerment and economic welfare. The gender gap in land rights has recently received attention worldwide from development practitioners and activists. The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) has also flagged the importance of providing women with equal and secure land rights (United Nations, 2017) in promoting sustainable development.

In India, especially in the northern and central parts (which is also the focus of this paper), men domination has been prominent in most aspects of life. Over the years, there has been significant improvement in the participation of women in various indicators such as education and employment; however, women’s land ownership patterns have failed to report the same trend. The available databases also signal India's slow growth of women land owners. While National Family Health Surveys (NFHS) data shows that the proportion of women land owners (either single or joint ownership) in the age group of 15–49 years has increased from 28.3 per cent in 2015–16 to just 31.7 per cent in 2019–21, the Agriculture Census also show growth in women land owners from 11 per cent (15.1 million) in 2005–06, to 12.8 per cent (17.65 million) in 2010–11 and further 13.9 per cent (20.25 million) in 2015–16.

Although these datasets and a few others reflect the change in women's land ownership over time, none of these patterns is comparable due to differences in their methodologies and terminologies. The datasets, such as the socio-economic caste census 2011 lacks sex-disaggregated figures; the NFHS data is restricted to the age group of 15–49 years and is not disaggregated for single and joint ownership; the Agriculture Census data considers only the sex of the head of household. Hence, the lack of reliable and dis-aggregated State-wise data sources across time is a significant gap in understanding the inequities in landholding in India. Bina Agarwal et al. (2020) mentioned in her paper that accurate figures are needed to measure the real progress in women’s empowerment since the estimates drawn using different methodologies without sex-specific data can lead to a misleading picture.

To fill the data gap and reflect ground realities, the paper uses the information in the digital copies of the land records downloaded from the States and UTs revenue portals. This work is new since none of the studies has evaluated the land record copies before to get a true sense of the extent of inequities in women’s land ownership in rural India. The paper does not seek to study the changing pattern of women land owners over the years; the idea here is to go beyond and understand different aspects of discrimination or bias women land owners face once they own the land. The findings could provide essential policy pointers in identifying the steps that need to be reinforced to empower women in terms of land ownership patterns in a true sense, where they can practice their land rights rather than just having names on land titles.

With this view, the paper explicitly provide answers to the following research questions: i) How land titles are distributed in rural India between men and women by: type of ownership (single/ joint), share of land holding, size of land holdings and quality of land; ii) How significant the variations are between men and women land owners across and within States/ UTs at different stages; iii) Whether there exist any statistical significant relationship between the type of ownership by women and size of land owned; and iv) Whether State laws/ provisions and State’s socio-economic profile bears any influence on women’s land ownership or not. The findings are expected to deepen the understanding on the extent of bias against women land titles and will help in identifying how States may promote better ownership and control over land by women.

After this Introduction, Section 2 discusses the origin and barriers for women’s land rights, the rationale behind having equal land rights for women and the initiatives taken by the Union and State governments in India. Section 3 summarises the data and methodological approach, and Section 4 presents the key findings of the women's land ownership pattern in rural India by type of ownership, land size and land quality. It also identifies the inequities in women's land titles by ownership pattern and land size classification. Further, it explores the relationship between women land ownership, state socio-economic profile, and legal provisions implemented at the state level. Section 5 presents the conclusions and policy discussions.

**Preface**

Every generation probably overestimates its impact on the flow of history. We believe that our discoveries and inventions and more importantly our way of thinking will forever change the world. Did Kilby and Noyce think this when they were independently creating the integrated circuit in the 1950s? Did Gutenberg and his ilk imagine their impact while creating the movable-type printing press in the 15th century? We can keep going back in history - all the way to the cave men and women who tamed fire. Did they know the profound effect their ingenuity would have on the generations to come?

Nevertheless, there is an overwhelming feeling that we are currently in the midst of the most important technological revolution in the history of humanity - not just a slight upwards tilt of the curve, but a substantial step function jump. People have compared the potential impact of AI on society to that of the industrial revolution.

Numbers from OpenAI and Google show that 10% of humanity already uses LLM-based AI products on a weekly/ monthly basis. But given that roughly 6 billion people use the internet and social media apps - there is still a long way to go for consumer AI to become ubiquitous. For humanity-scale adoption - a few foundational shifts would need to take place. This is not entirely a manifesto for the future or a set of predictions. Think of what follows as a personal AI wishlist for 2026.

---

2023 was the year the public discovered what large language models could do. 2024 was the year where every interface became a chatbot and 2025 was clearly the year of agents. 2026 must become the year of Invisible AI - where the technology fades into the background of truly great product design. We must overcome the early sins of AI user experience and bring AI (perhaps kicking and screaming) from its adolescence into its adulthood.

Invisible AI: The 2026 Wishlist

**I: The Anti-Wish List**

1\. We stop obsessing about model version numbers. The shift has already begun - with words like *auto, instant, fast, thinking*, etc taking over. If the average user has to worry about parameter count and token cost - then we have lost the plot. These matter to developers - but not to your average user.

2\. We abolish the chatbot tax. No more waiting for the blinking cursor. We embrace *Zero-UI execution*. If I am looking at an email about a flight, the AI should manifest a "Book Hotel" button, pre-filled with my dates and budget. AI should live in the context of the app as a ghost-menu of "Next Probable Actions," not a sidebar assistant asking, "How can I help you today?"

3\. We get rid of AI slop. Slop curators intercept hundreds of incoming AI-generated messages and summarize them into a human truth and the most inspiring creative (human + AI) outputs.

**II: World Models and Spatial Intelligence**

I really like the analogy of LLMs as "wordsmiths in an enclosed dark room" - eloquent but blind, never having touched or seen the real world. 2026 should see us move from LLMs to Large World Models (LWMs) - not just able to predict the next word but the next state of an environment.

1\. Context-aware wearables - smart glasses that predict what could potentially happen next (don't pet that dog - it's getting ready to attack).

2\. Robots that can move away from hard-coded instructions for every task - "clean up the mess" means throwing away the banana peel, wiping (and not vacuuming) the spilled water and carefully keeping the baby's toys in a basket.

**III: The New UX - Agentic Design**

2025 gave us agents that could book a table. 2026 needs agents that can represent our preferences in a negotiation. In 2026, the best interface will be the one that doesn't exist until it's needed. Agentic UX moves from Reactive (waiting for a prompt) to Anticipatory (sensing intent).

1\. The "Liquid" Interface

We've lived through the "Chatbot" era where everything was a text bubble. Agentic UX in 2026 uses "Liquid UI" - interfaces that have no fixed form. If an agent is helping you plan a trip, it shouldn't just talk; it should instantly manifest a temporary, interactive map or a comparison table that dissolves once the task is done. UI should be just-in-time, surfacing as "Action Cards" or "Spatial Overlays" based on the user's current context.

Success Metric: Time-to-Intent (TTI) - how many seconds between the user thinking of a task and the AI providing the "Commit" button?

2\. The "Sandbox" Pattern

For high-stakes actions (like moving money or editing a shared work doc), the wish is Safe-to-Try Mode. The agent simulates the outcome first - "If I execute this automation, here is what your calendar and budget will look like" - giving the user a "Commit" button rather than just an "Undo" button.

3\. Pause & Intent Recognition

Instead of clicking "Submit," the AI monitors micro-signals. It detects a long pause or a "hover" of hesitation and steps in: "You usually book refundable tickets; should I switch the toggle for you?"

4\. The Permission Spectrum

Automation requires a "Permission Spectrum," not a binary toggle. The product must allow the user to delegate authority in tiers:

\- Level 1 (Inquire): "I found a flight. Should I book it?" Ask me first.

\- Level 2 (Inform): "I booked the flight. Here is the confirmation." Tell me after.

\- Level 3 (Autonomous): "The flight is booked, added to the calendar, and the ride-share is scheduled." You are only notified if something goes wrong. Just do it.

Success Metric: Cognitive Load Reduction. Does the user feel like they are managing a staff or performing a chore?

5\. The "Agentic Handshake" (Agent-to-Agent)

We need a way for my agent to talk to your agent. The agent doesn't just "do tasks" - it holds my "User Manual" (my budget, dietary needs, brand loyalties) and negotiates with other AIs.

\- Social Orchestration: If we're grabbing dinner, our agents negotiate availability and food preferences in the background, surfacing only the final "Does 7 PM at The Grill work for both of you?"

\- Commercial Negotiation: You tell your agent, "I need a flight to Tokyo under $1,000." Your Personal Agent broadcasts this intent to Airline Agents. They negotiate. Your agent filters results based on your preferences (legroom, meal choice) and presents a single "Commit" button.

**IV: Hardware - from Screens to Air**

In 2026 hardware should be "digital air" - always there, but mostly invisible. By moving AI to the edge (on-device models), we unlock form factors that don't need huge screens or battery-draining cloud connections.

Body Area Network

\- Contextual Observer (Pin/Pendant): screenless; with a camera + mic sensor fusion; bone conduction whispers that only you can hear; augmented memory (Gary from Veep whispering important facts in your ear)

\- Hearables (Whisper Layer): silence the construction noise but boost toddler's voice

**V: Context as Currency**

The industry will have realized that a "smart assistant" that only knows what you did inside one app is actually quite dumb. Context is the new currency and interoperability is the mint.

1\. The "Universal Handshake" (extending MCP & A2A)

By 2026, the Model Context Protocol (MCP) (pioneered by Anthropic) and Agent-to-Agent (A2A) (driven by Google) act like the "HTTP for Agents." They let agents plug into any API, database, or file system securely. They allow an agent from one company to securely "read" the context provided by another, provided you've given permission.

2\. Semantic "Suitcases"

![Semantic Suitcase](https://labs.anshumani.com/objects/uploads/c770c24f-70f4-4e79-be39-795c67938f5b.png)

Semantic Suitcase

Think of your context as a "semantic suitcase" that follows you. When you move from your work email (Microsoft) to your travel app (Airbnb), your personal agent hands over a "Context Token." This token tells Airbnb, "This user is on a budget, hates red-eye flights, and has a meeting in London at 9 AM," without Airbnb ever seeing your actual emails.

3\. Decentralized Identity (The "Context Key")

\- Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs): instead of logging in with your email, you log in with your Personal Context Key.

\- Permissioned Memory: you can grant an agent "View-Only" access to your calendar for 5 minutes to solve a scheduling conflict. Once the task is done, the "Context Bridge" is severed.

\- Zero-Knowledge Context: using advanced cryptography, your agent can prove things about you (e.g., "This user is over 21 and has $500 in their budget") to a platform agent without revealing your actual age or bank balance. The app trusts the token, not the data, ensuring privacy is mathematically guaranteed.

> ([[Guaranteeing title to land_ a preliminary study.pdf#page=2&selection=204,8,228,8|p.2324]])
>  Underlying all these special provisions of the evidence law is the assumption that public registers and other documents would be accurate. This assumption has so many branches; the legal system assumes that public records are prepared carefully after a verification of the facts; that they are maintained accurately by reflecting changes, whenever those changes take place; and that, in general, they are faithful to the external world and to the transactions and events therein, which they seek to preserve 'for posterity

Wadhwa’s argument here exposes a fundamental contradiction in the Indian land governance system: the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 accords high evidentiary value to public records on the assumption that they are accurately prepared, regularly updated, and faithfully reflect real-world transactions. In effect, the legal system treats records-of-rights as reliable representations of ownership. However, in practice, these assumptions break down because land records are often outdated, mutations are delayed or unrecorded, and there is a persistent gap between recorded ownership and actual possession. As a result,<mark style="background: #FF5582A6;"> the legal presumption that “record equals reality” collapses, undermining the very basis on which courts rely on such documents.</mark> This creates systemic uncertainty, increases transaction costs, and fuels litigation, revealing a deeper institutional failure where the administrative machinery does not sustain the legal fiction of accurate and authoritative land records.

> ([[Guaranteeing title to land_ a preliminary study.pdf#page=2&selection=240,8,256,26|p.2324]])
>  Juristic theory labels such private transactions as "acts in the law". while it calls the public records themselves as "acts of the sovereign". The two are inter-linked and should harmonise ,with each other. If that desideratum is not fulfilled, then the very justifiability of statutory provisions df the law of evidence concerning public records falls to the ground


This passage draws a sharp conceptual distinction between two layers of the legal system. “Acts in the law” refer to **private transactions**—such as sale, inheritance, or lease—through which rights in land are actually created or transferred. In contrast, “acts of the sovereign” are the **public records maintained by the state**, which formally document and certify these transactions. Wadhwa’s point is that these two must **correspond and remain aligned**: the state’s records should accurately reflect what has happened in reality between private parties. If this harmony breaks down—i.e., if records do not capture actual transactions—then the legitimacy of treating public records as reliable legal evidence collapses.

> ([[Guaranteeing title to land_ a preliminary study.pdf#page=3&selection=2,0,52,45|p.2325]])
> As regards the Indian law as to title to land, the statute law of India as in force at present, broadly speaking, does not profess to provide for a state certification of title to land under the aegis of a public authority. No doubt, regarding agricultural land, the record-of-rights in land and similar documents, by virtue of provisions in the land-laws of various states, create a structure, at least in theory, of permanent records of transactions concerning such land. But the entries in such records are not conclusive even though they may be relevant in a court of law, and may be given a presumptive status by land-laws. As late as August 1989, it was affirmed by the Supreme Court of India as follows: "It is firmly established that the revenue records are not documents of title.. . *.2 Thus, whatever be the entry in the records-of-rights in land, it would be permissible to challenge it in the appropriate court or tribunal. This position arises because property legislation and legislation relating to registration of documents were never framed in India with the objective of providing a state guarantee of title to land.

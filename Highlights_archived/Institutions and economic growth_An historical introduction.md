# 1. English Development

North argues that England’s institutional evolution gradually produced **secure property rights, parliamentary authority, and financial institutions that reduced transaction costs**, which ultimately supported economic growth.

## Fiscal Crisis and the Rise of Parliament

The origins of English institutional change lie in the **fiscal pressures of warfare**, especially during the Hundred Years’ War. Monarchs needed revenue and therefore negotiated with Parliament. This bargaining process gradually expanded parliamentary power. As North notes, the right of Parliament to legislate and influence national policy was effectively “purchased” through the taxes granted to the Crown.


This exchange between the Crown and Parliament created a political structure in which rulers were constrained by representative institutions.

---

## Tudor Administrative Transformation

During the sixteenth century, Tudor rulers reorganized the state into a more structured bureaucracy overseeing taxation and economic regulation. The **wool trade** played a key role in this arrangement, involving cooperation between merchants, Parliament, and the Crown. In this system:

- Parliament gained authority over taxation

## The Seventeenth-Century Constitutional Conflict

The struggle between the Crown and Parliament intensified during the Stuart period. Political conflicts over taxation, monopolies, and civil liberties eventually led to the **English Civil War**. Legal thinkers such as Edward Coke asserted the supremacy of **common law over royal authority**, strengthening institutional limits on the monarchy.


Although political alliances were fluid, there was increasing demand for **broadly protected rights and predictable legal institutions**.

---

## The Glorious Revolution and Institutional Consolidation

The decisive shift occurred after the **Glorious Revolution of 1688–1689**, when parliamentary supremacy became firmly established. This political transformation strengthened property rights and enabled the creation of important economic institutions, including the **Bank of England (1694)** and new financial instruments.

These innovations lowered transaction costs and improved the credibility of long-term contracts, particularly in capital markets. As a result, England developed a financial system capable of supporting investment and economic growth.


In North’s interpretation, the combination of political constraints on rulers and expanding market institutions created a framework conducive to sustained economic development.

# 2. Spanish Development

North contrasts the English experience with Spain, where institutional evolution moved toward **centralized bureaucracy, economic regulation, and declining economic dynamism**.

---

## Castile and the Rise of Centralized Monarchy

Before unification, the Spanish kingdoms had different political traditions. The Crown of Aragon had relatively strong representative institutions (Cortes) that limited royal power. Castile, however, experienced constant warfare and internal conflict, and its Cortes played a weaker role.

After the union of Ferdinand and Isabella, political authority became increasingly centralized in Castile. The monarchy consolidated power over the nobility and church, creating a highly centralized political system that shaped Spanish governance and colonial administration.


---

## Fiscal Structure and Economic Consequences

Spain relied heavily on specific revenue sources. One major institution was the **Mesta**, a powerful guild of sheep herders that paid the Crown for privileges such as seasonal migration rights. While profitable for the monarchy, the Mesta harmed agricultural development and weakened property rights in land.


Another important revenue source was the **alcabala**, a sales tax that discouraged commerce and increased transaction costs.

---

## Bureaucratic Expansion and Regulation

As Spain built a vast global empire, governing it required an elaborate administrative system. The monarchy created a large bureaucracy and issued extensive regulations controlling economic activity. By the seventeenth century, hundreds of thousands of royal decrees had been issued concerning the governance of the Spanish colonies.

The Spanish state attempted to regulate trade through:

- guild systems
    
- state trading monopolies
    
- price controls.
    
…
    

These policies restricted economic flexibility and discouraged market development.

---

## Fiscal Crisis and Economic Decline

The costs of maintaining the Spanish empire eventually exceeded revenues. As treasure inflows declined and military expenditures increased, the Crown raised taxes and repeatedly defaulted on debts. The result was a long period of **economic stagnation and institutional rigidity**.


In North’s framework, the Spanish institutional structure favored centralized political control and revenue extraction rather than the creation of efficient economic institutions.

**Brunella** @brunellaism 2025-12-26

one of my favorite james c. scott arguments is that every civilization builds its subsistence base around a cereal bc only cereals can really function as a taxation substrate (they're “visible, divisible, assessable, storable, transportable, and ‘rationable’”). you don't have lentil states, chickpea empires or cassava nations, but all of the earliest agrarian states all were built on wheat, barlet or millet

the only partial exception are the Inca, who relied on maize and potatoes i.e. most of their subsistence was structurally less legible and harder to seize (tubers can be left underground if the taxman comes, and if the state wants them anyway it has to dig them up one by one which ends up being less calorically/market valuable). common braudel W, highland ecologies are refuges bc compared to grain belts they're fiscally illegible

my other favorite argument on this is john v murra's work on how ancient andean polities built large scale political economies on top of this tax illegibility by integrating into vertical archipelagos held together by exchanges of labor time instead of money (the ayni, the minka, the mita)
